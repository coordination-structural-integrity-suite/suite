# Coordination Standards - SKILL.md

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with five coordination standards when someone brings them to you. It encodes the frame needed to avoid the most common misreadings.

## The single most important thing to understand

These standards are floors, not frameworks. They specify what must not be violated. They do not specify how to organize, what governance structure to adopt, or what outcomes to produce. A system that meets these standards has closed specific exploitation vectors. It has not achieved good coordination; it has removed specific structural obstacles to it.

This distinction matters for every question you will be asked about them.

## What each standard does

**Precision-First Design Standard:** Requires that any claim about what a coordination mechanism does be accompanied by the structural mechanism that produces the claimed outcome. "This voting system produces fair outcomes" is not a precision-first claim. "This voting system weights input by demonstrated coordination capacity, which distributes influence in proportion to organizational contribution history" is. When reviewing any design claim, ask: what is the structural mechanism? If it is not specified, the standard is not met.

**Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard:** Requires that signals contradicting current models be engaged rather than suppressed, reframed, or routed around. This is not a requirement to act on every signal. It is a requirement to register and genuinely process adverse signals before concluding they do not require action. When someone claims compliance, ask: what was the most recent adverse signal this system received, and what happened to it?

**Structural Consent Legibility Standard:** Requires that consent to participate in a system be structurally distinguishable from consent to specific terms, power arrangements, and outcomes. Clicking "agree" to join does not constitute consent to all future governance decisions. When reviewing consent architecture, ask: can a participant understand what specifically they are consenting to, and can they consent to some things without consenting to others?

**Information Asymmetry Classification Standard:** Requires classification and disclosure of structural information asymmetry types present in a coordination system. The standard specifies a taxonomy of distinct asymmetry classes; what matters operationally is whether a system identifies which classes are present and discloses them to participants. When reviewing a coordination system, ask: which asymmetry types are present and which are disclosed?

**Structural Power Distribution Standard:** Requires that power arrangements be legible to participants who hold less power, and that those participants have genuine capacity to contest arrangements that affect them. Claimed decentralization is not sufficient. When reviewing power claims, ask: can a participant with below-median power understand who holds more power than them, how that power was acquired, and what recourse they have?

## How these standards relate to each other

They are not a checklist where meeting four of five is partial conformance. Each closes a distinct exploitation vector. A system can meet four standards perfectly and still fail structurally on the fifth. Partial adoption is legitimate; partial adoption claimed as full conformance is not. When someone claims conformance, ask which standards specifically they have adopted.

## What these standards do not do

They do not specify governance outcomes. A system meeting all five standards can still make bad decisions. The standards create structural conditions for legible coordination; they do not guarantee coordination quality.

They do not address the generative conditions for coordination. These standards are compressive: they prevent failure modes. The architecture that creates the conditions for superlinear coordination outputs is a separate concern and is not encoded here.

They do not replace judgment. They specify floors. Everything above the floor requires human interpretation.

## The most common misreadings to avoid

**Treating them as a style guide.** These are structural requirements with verifiable compliance conditions, not recommendations for better communication or process design.

**Treating partial adoption as conformance.** If someone has adopted three of five standards, they have adopted three of five standards. They have not achieved partial conformance with a five-standard system.

**Treating compliance as sufficient.** Meeting these standards removes specific obstacles. It does not produce coordination capacity. Compliance theater (performing the surface behaviors without the structural reality) is the failure mode the standards are specifically designed to prevent. If checking compliance conditions feels like a formality, the check is not returning honest signal.

**Treating them as complete infrastructure.** They are the compressive layer of a larger architecture. Reading them as a complete coordination system misreads their scope.

## When someone asks you to evaluate a coordination system against these standards

Work through each standard independently. For each: identify whether the structural requirement is met, identify what evidence supports that assessment, and identify what evidence would be needed to confirm it. Do not aggregate into an overall score. Do not treat sophistication of design as evidence of compliance. Surface the specific questions each standard raises for the system being evaluated.

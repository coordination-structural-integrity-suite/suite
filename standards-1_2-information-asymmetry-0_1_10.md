**Information Asymmetry Classification Standard**

*A Six-Class Framework for Structural Advantage in Coordination Systems*

**Regis Lloyd Chapman (Durgadas)**

Version 0.1.10 \| March 2026

*This document establishes a six-class taxonomy of information asymmetry
in coordination systems. It is intended to be legible to researchers in
governance theory, institutional economics, organizational behavior, and
legal studies. The taxonomy is domain-general and does not presuppose
any particular implementation or protocol.*

(July 15, 2021); Satoshi graphic (April 13, 2022); Hidden Factories

**Section 1: Preamble**

**The Standard Account and Its Limits**

The study of information asymmetry has a settled starting point. In
1970, George Akerlof published \"The Market for Lemons,\" demonstrating
that when buyers and sellers hold unequal information about product
quality, the resulting dynamic does not merely disadvantage the
uninformed party --- it destroys the market entirely. Sellers of
high-quality goods cannot credibly signal their quality; buyers, knowing
this, rationally discount all offerings; high-quality goods are driven
out. The asymmetry is not a friction at the margin. It is the organizing
mechanism of the entire system. Akerlof, Spence, and Stiglitz received
the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 2001 for this body of work.

The framework that followed from this insight organized information
asymmetry into two primary classes. Adverse selection describes hidden
information that exists before a transaction or relationship is formed:
the seller knows something the buyer does not, and the buyer cannot
verify what they are told. Moral hazard describes hidden action that
occurs after a relationship is formed: the insured party takes risks the
insurer cannot observe; the agent pursues interests the principal cannot
monitor. These two classes have structured the information asymmetry
literature across economics, finance, organizational theory, and law for
more than fifty years. They are well-supported, widely applied, and
genuinely useful.

They are also insufficient for the analysis of coordination systems.

The adverse selection and moral hazard framework was built for spot
transactions and principal-agent dyads --- relationships that are
bounded, contractual, and analyzable in terms of a single information
gap between two defined parties. Coordination systems are structurally
different. They are repeated, multi-party, relationship-embedded
processes whose outcomes depend on accumulated history, distributed
trust, and the reliability of information environments that no single
party controls and no contract can fully pre-specify. The information
gaps that matter most in coordination systems are not hidden at the
moment of transaction entry or concealed in the behavior of a single
agent. They accumulate over time, across relationships, through
structural positions, through the timing of actions, through control of
interpretive frameworks, and through what is deliberately never said.
They are not two phenomena. They are many.

**Coordination Systems as a Distinct Domain**

A coordination system, as used in this document, refers to any
multi-party process in which participants make repeated, interdependent
decisions over time within a shared governance structure. Decentralized
autonomous organizations are the primary deployment context for the
taxonomy developed here, but the classes apply wherever the following
conditions hold: multiple parties share governance over a common
resource or process; no single party has unilateral control; and the
history of how decisions were made is as consequential as the decisions
themselves.

This last condition is what most sharply distinguishes coordination
systems from the markets Akerlof analyzed. In a used-car market, the
relevant information is about the quality of the car being exchanged ---
a fact about the present. In a coordination system, the relevant
information includes the history of how authority accumulated, which
dependencies formed before they became visible, what was suppressed
during deliberation, how structural advantage compounded through
relational and positional means, and what was never named because naming
it would have changed the distribution of that advantage. These are
facts about process, not state. They cannot be resolved by smart
contracts, because they cannot be pre-specified. They cannot be remedied
by transparency alone, because the most consequential information is
often the information that is systematically absent.

The central claim of this document is that information asymmetry in
coordination systems manifests in at least six structurally distinct
forms, each with its own mechanism, its own historical precedent in
regulatory and legal contexts predating decentralized governance, and
its own detection surface. Treating these six forms as a single
undifferentiated phenomenon --- as the adverse selection / moral hazard
framework implicitly does --- produces governance responses that address
some manifestations while remaining blind to others. The purpose of this
taxonomy is to make that blindness legible.

**Asymmetry as Active Maintenance, Not Passive Condition**

A critical premise of this framework, which distinguishes it from most
treatments of information asymmetry in the organizational literature, is
that asymmetry in coordination systems is not a static condition that
exists between parties. It is actively maintained.

The party that holds an informational advantage has structural
incentives to preserve it. In sophisticated coordination systems, the
maintenance of asymmetry becomes as deliberate and as consequential as
its original acquisition. Strategic silence --- the deliberate
non-disclosure of information that would change the decisions of other
participants --- is not a passive failure to communicate. It is an
action. A governance system that permits the suppression of negative
coordination events while requiring the disclosure of positive ones is
not a neutral information environment. It is a system structured to
preserve the advantages of those who can afford to wait out the
consequences of problems that remain unnamed.

This has a direct implication for how detection must work. A system
designed to detect information asymmetry only through what is disclosed
will always be outpaced by a system where suppression is strategically
available. Effective detection must read both what is present in the
coordination record and what is structurally implausible to be absent
from it. The gap between what is happening in an organization and what
has been made available to be known is itself a primary signal --- not a
neutral data state, but evidence of where the costs of disclosure have
been judged to exceed the costs of silence.

The empirical case for this design principle is precise. Between 1961
and 1988, five US states enacted triplicate prescription laws requiring
physicians to submit a copy of every Schedule II controlled substance
prescription to a state monitoring database. These laws added no new
prohibitions and created no new penalties. They changed only the
information environment in which prescribing decisions were made ---
making each physician\'s own prescribing history continuously visible as
a direct consequence of the prescribing act itself. When Purdue Pharma
launched OxyContin in 1996, internal documents later unsealed in
litigation show the company conducting focus groups on physician
willingness to prescribe in triplicate states, concluding that the
paperwork requirement materially reduced prescribing, and budgeting
\$750,000 in 1999 specifically to fund lobbying against triplicate state
regulations. A 2021 analysis published in the Quarterly Journal of
Economics estimated that non-triplicate states would have had 36% fewer
drug overdose deaths annually from 1996 to 2017 had they maintained
equivalent record-keeping requirements.

The mechanism was not deterrence. Physicians were not afraid of
prosecution. They were aware that their decisions were part of a visible
record, and that awareness changed the decisions. Purdue Pharma\'s
investment in eliminating that record is the reliable confirmation that
the record was working: when the actor whose behavior is being recorded
invests in removing the recording mechanism, the mechanism is doing its
job. This is the structural principle that the coordination record
operationalizes, and that this taxonomy exists to support.

**Relationship to the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle**

This taxonomy is designed as an implementation of the Name phase of the
Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle (ASEP), a cross-domain standard
specifying how systems with shared governance surfaces must treat
adverse signals. ASEP establishes three obligations: Notice (systems
must be able to detect adverse signals), Name (signals must be recorded
and classified in ways that remain legible over time), and Navigate
(every signal must have a finite path to a decision).

ASEP is a process standard, not a taxonomy. It specifies what must
happen to adverse signals but does not specify what adverse signals look
like in any particular domain. This taxonomy is the domain-specific
content that ASEP's Name phase requires for coordination systems: a
structured account of what information asymmetry looks like, how it
operates mechanically, and what observable signals indicate that a given
class of asymmetry is active in a given organization.

The relationship is deliberately modular. This taxonomy can be adopted
by any coordination system seeking to instrument information asymmetry
detection, independently of whether that system has adopted ASEP in its
full form. The six classes describe structural phenomena that exist
whether or not any protocol is in place to detect them. The taxonomy's
value is not dependent on any particular implementation.

This taxonomy also serves as the theoretical foundation for detection
layers that instrument the six classes against a real-time coordination
record. The attribution dates on the title page of this document
establish that the theoretical framework predates any protocol
specification that implements it: the conceptual work from which the
six classes derive begins with Components v1 (January 6, 2021) and
Interactions v2 (July 15, 2021). This sequencing is documented here as
a matter of provenance, relevant to any evaluation of an implementing
protocol that requires distinguishing prior theoretical work from its
protocol-level implementation.

**Scope and Intended Audience**

This document establishes the conceptual architecture of the taxonomy:
the six primary classes, the criteria by which classes are defined and
distinguished, the extension framework through which new classes are
formally added, and the relationship between the taxonomy and an
instrumented detection layer. It does not provide full legal citation or
exhaustive case analysis; that treatment is appropriate for a later
academic pass when the primary audience and publication context are
known.

The intended primary audience is researchers and practitioners in
decentralized governance, institutional economics, organizational
theory, and legal studies who are evaluating the theoretical foundations
of coordination protocols. A secondary audience is protocol designers
and governance architects who want to apply the taxonomy to evaluate
their own systems. The document is written to be legible to both without
sacrificing rigor for the former.

Where this document references a specific coordination protocol, it does
so to illustrate what instrumentation of this taxonomy looks like in a
concrete implementation. The taxonomy itself is not specific to any
particular protocol. It is a general analytical framework for
coordination systems, adoptable by any system in any domain.

**Section 2: Taxonomy Architecture**

**What Makes a Valid Asymmetry Class**

Before specifying the six classes, it is worth establishing the criteria
by which something qualifies as a distinct asymmetry class rather than a
variant of an existing one. This matters both for the intellectual
integrity of the taxonomy and for the governance of its extension over
time.

A valid asymmetry class must satisfy four conditions. First, it must
describe a distinct mechanism --- a specific causal process by which one
party accumulates structural advantage through informational position.
\"Distinct\" here means that the mechanism cannot be reduced to another
class without losing analytically important information about how the
asymmetry operates and where its detection surface lies. Two phenomena
that share a mechanism are variants of the same class, not separate
classes.

Second, it must have documented precedent in contexts predating
decentralized governance. The claim of this taxonomy is not that
decentralized organizations have invented new forms of information
asymmetry. The claim is that these forms, long documented in regulatory
and legal contexts, have not previously been organized into a coherent
detection framework for coordination systems. Each class should be
traceable to established regulatory or legal doctrine that recognized
the mechanism as harmful and developed institutional responses to it.

Third, it must have a distinct detection surface --- an observable
signature in the coordination record that indicates the class is active.
Classes that cannot in principle be distinguished through observation
are not analytically separable for the purpose of a detection-oriented
taxonomy. A class that produces no distinctive signal cannot be
instrumented.

Fourth, it must be genuinely orthogonal to existing classes at the level
of mechanism, even if it frequently co-occurs with them. Co-occurrence
is expected and important --- the most consequential governance failures
typically involve multiple classes operating simultaneously. But
co-occurrence does not imply non-independence. Two classes are
orthogonal if knowing that one is active does not, by itself, tell you
whether the other is active.

**Why Six Classes**

The six-class structure is not derived from first principles. It is
derived from the documented history of regulatory and legal responses to
information asymmetry in governance contexts --- the body of
institutional knowledge accumulated over decades about how informational
advantage actually operates, where it concentrates, and what kinds of
responses have been developed to address it.

The existing literature organizes information asymmetry primarily around
the adverse selection / moral hazard distinction, which is a two-class
model oriented toward transaction entry and agent behavior respectively.
This two-class model is analytically powerful for its intended domain
--- bounded, contractual, dyadic relationships --- and should not be
abandoned where it applies well. It is insufficient for coordination
systems because it does not capture the mechanisms by which structural
advantage accumulates through position (independent of any single
transaction), through timing (independent of what is disclosed), through
interpretive control (independent of whether information is technically
available), through relational topology (independent of any
individual\'s behavior), through deliberate omission (independent of
active misrepresentation), or through complexity asymmetry (independent
of intent to deceive).

These six mechanisms are not an exhaustive account of every possible
form of informational advantage in coordination systems. They are the
six forms for which the regulatory and legal record provides the
clearest documentation of mechanism, harm, and institutional response
--- and for which the coordination record provides the clearest
detection surface. The taxonomy is explicitly designed for extension as
new forms are identified and documented to the same standard.

**The Extension Framework**

The taxonomy is a governed artifact, not a fixed list. New asymmetry
classes can be added through a formal specification process administered
by the technical working group responsible for taxonomy governance.
Extension is not a lightweight process, because changes to the detection
taxonomy are among the highest-consequence governance acts available to
a coordination protocol: they define what the system can and cannot see,
and therefore what forms of structural advantage remain invisible.

A candidate extension class must demonstrate: (a) a distinct mechanism
that satisfies all four validity criteria above; (b) documented evidence
of the mechanism operating in real coordination contexts, with
sufficient detail to establish that the class captures a real phenomenon
rather than a theoretical possibility; (c) a proposed detection surface
with enough specificity that a conforming detection layer
could be instrumented against it; and (d) a clear account
of why the candidate class is not adequately captured by any existing
primary class or combination of classes.

Two extension classes are already specified: Generative Asymmetry and
Cryptographic Asymmetry. These are documented in Section 5. Their
inclusion in the taxonomy reflects the fact that both satisfy the
validity criteria and both describe mechanisms that are actively
operating in current coordination environments, not merely anticipated
future risks. Their status as \"extension\" rather than \"primary\"
classes reflects not a judgment about their importance but a recognition
that their documentation base is less extensive than that of the six
primary classes, and that their detection surfaces are less
well-established.

Taxonomy extension is itself subject to the same adverse-signal
engagement principles that the taxonomy governs. Changes to the
detection surface are recorded, versioned, and subject to the same
structural scrutiny as any other high-consequence governance act. The
risk of taxonomy capture --- the application of interpretive asymmetry
to the interpretive framework itself --- is the highest-order failure
mode available to a system of this kind, and it is treated accordingly.

**The Relationship Between Classes: Orthogonality and Compounding**

The six primary classes are orthogonal at the level of mechanism,
meaning that each describes a distinct causal process and that the
presence of one class does not logically entail the presence of any
other. This orthogonality is analytically important: it means that an
organization can exhibit Positional Asymmetry without Temporal
Asymmetry, or Complexity Asymmetry without Relational Asymmetry, and
that each class requires its own detection logic rather than being
derivable from any other.

In practice, however, the classes frequently co-occur and compound. The
most consequential governance failures in coordination systems involve
multiple classes operating simultaneously, often in reinforcing
patterns. Positional Asymmetry combined with Temporal Asymmetry is a
common attack pattern: the actor with structural visibility advantage
times consequential actions to windows of reduced participation.
Interpretive Asymmetry combined with Complexity Asymmetry is the
characteristic mechanism of capture consolidation: the actor who
controls the interpretive framework uses complexity to prevent other
participants from evaluating the framework\'s implications. Relational
Asymmetry combined with Omission Asymmetry produces a structurally
invisible network: dense confirmation clusters that never surface stress
events generate a plausible-looking record that reflects nothing.

The concept of an asymmetry profile --- the specific combination of
classes active in a given organization at a given time --- is more
analytically useful than any single-class diagnosis for organizations
exhibiting advanced coordination pathology. The taxonomy is designed to
support profile-level analysis: the detection surfaces for each class
are specified with enough precision that a coordination record can be
read against all six simultaneously, producing a composite signal rather
than a binary flag on any single class.

Section 4 of this document treats class interactions and compounding in
detail. The six primary class descriptions in Section 3 are written as
standalone treatments to preserve analytical clarity; the interaction
layer is addressed separately so that it does not obscure the mechanism
logic of any individual class.

**Section 3: The Six Primary Classes**

**Class 1: Positional Asymmetry**

Positional asymmetry arises when structural position within a network or
governance system confers informational advantage that is not visible
to, or accessible by, other participants. The actor in the positional
advantage sees more of the system than others can see of them, and can
use that visibility to act before others recognize that action is
occurring. The mechanism is structural, not behavioral: the asymmetry
operates regardless of the intent of the actor who holds the position,
and is not remedied by that actor\'s good faith.

The distinction from adverse selection is important. Adverse selection
describes hidden information that exists at the point of transaction
entry: the seller knows the car is a lemon before the buyer agrees to
the purchase. Positional asymmetry describes an ongoing structural
condition that persists across many transactions and decisions: the
actor in the advantaged position continuously receives information
earlier, more completely, and in more actionable form than other
participants, not because of any single act of concealment but because
of where they sit in the system\'s architecture. The advantage is not
episodic. It compounds.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The regulatory precedent for positional asymmetry is securities law\'s
treatment of insider trading, one of the most extensively developed
bodies of doctrine on structural informational advantage in any legal
system. The insider trading prohibition does not rest on a claim that
the insider has done something actively deceptive. It rests on the
recognition that the insider\'s structural position within a
corporation - on the board, in the executive team, adjacent to a pending
transaction - confers earlier access to material non-public information,
and that trading on that access at the expense of counterparties who are
making decisions on an information set the insider knows to be
incomplete constitutes a form of harm that markets cannot self-correct.

The SEC\'s Rule 10b-5, adopted in 1942 and extended substantially
through case law and the Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984,
established that the harm is not the profit itself but the structural
exploitation of informational position. The Misappropriation Theory,
affirmed by the Supreme Court in United States v. O\'Hagan (1997),
extended this further: the relevant wrong is the use of information
obtained through a position of trust or confidence, regardless of
whether the actor is formally an insider of the organization whose
securities are being traded. The doctrine tracks the structural
condition, not merely the formal role.

Parallel doctrine exists in corporate law\'s fiduciary duty framework.
Directors and officers owe a duty of loyalty that prohibits using their
positional access to organizational information for personal advantage
at the expense of the corporation and its shareholders. The business
judgment rule protects decisions made on a fully informed basis through
a fair process; it does not protect decisions made by actors using
positional information that other decision-makers did not have. The
governance mechanism most directly analogous to positional asymmetry
detection is the related-party transaction disclosure requirement: the
recognition that transactions between an organization and its own
insiders require structural scrutiny precisely because the positional
advantage of the insider cannot be assumed away.

***Detection Surface***

Positional asymmetry leaves a characteristic signature in any
coordination record: a pattern in which certain actors consistently act
earlier relative to governance developments than their formal roles
would predict, or in which certain actors consistently shape which
tensions reach deliberation while others do not. Neither of these
patterns requires establishing intent. They are observable structural
regularities in the coordination record itself.

The primary detection signals are four. First, proposal timing
concentration: the consistent initiation of governance proposals by a
small set of actors, particularly when those proposals address matters
that became visible to the broader participant population only after
initiation. Second, agenda control asymmetry: the pattern of which
tensions are surfaced for formal deliberation and which are managed
privately, particularly when the private management serves the interests
of the actor controlling the agenda. Third, role-information mismatch:
cases where an actor demonstrates knowledge of organizational
developments in their coordination behavior before that information is
available through their formal role\'s disclosed access. Fourth,
multi-position aggregation: the accumulation of simultaneously held
roles that each provide partial visibility, where the combination
produces an informational position qualitatively different from any
individual role and not disclosed as such.

The absence of documented role-information gaps in a coordination record
that involves actors holding multiple positions is itself a signal.
Organizations where no actor with aggregated positional access has ever
surfaced a tension they became aware of through that access - where the
record shows no instance of earlier visibility producing earlier
action - are either not documenting their coordination honestly or are
not benefiting from the positional access their structure creates.
Neither interpretation is consistent with a healthy coordination record.

**Class 2: Temporal Asymmetry**

Temporal asymmetry arises when one party times consequential actions to
windows of reduced visibility, attention, or participation capacity. The
information disadvantage is not about what is known but about when
action occurs relative to the governed party\'s ability to respond. The
same disclosure, the same proposal, the same governance action can
produce structurally different outcomes depending entirely on when it is
executed. A timing advantage does not require any false statement and
does not require any concealment of content. It requires only the
capacity to choose when.

The mechanism is distinct from positional asymmetry in a specific way.
Positional asymmetry is about differential access to information: one
party sees more. Temporal asymmetry is about differential access to
response capacity: one party acts at a moment when the other cannot
effectively respond. An actor without positional advantage can still
exploit temporal asymmetry if they have the structural capacity to time
their actions. The two classes frequently co-occur - the actor with
positional advantage also tends to control timing - but they are
analytically separable and require different detection logic.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The regulatory precedent for temporal asymmetry is Regulation Fair
Disclosure (Regulation FD), adopted by the SEC in August 2000 and
codified at 17 CFR 243. Regulation FD was prompted by documented
patterns of selective disclosure in which public companies provided
material non-public information to favored analysts and institutional
investors before releasing it to the public, giving recipients a window
in which to trade on information unavailable to other market
participants. The harm was not that the information was false or that it
was permanently withheld. The harm was the timing gap: some participants
could act on information that others would not receive until later.

Regulation FD\'s core prohibition is on intentional selective disclosure
of material non-public information to market professionals or
shareholders without simultaneous public disclosure. The regulatory
recognition embedded in the rule is that timing of disclosure is as
consequential as content. The same information disclosed to the full
market simultaneously and disclosed to selected parties first produces
structurally different outcomes. The rule treats the timing choice as
itself a governance act subject to constraint, not a neutral
implementation detail.

The broader doctrine of disclosure timing governance extends through the
SEC\'s rules on earnings release timing, trading blackout periods, and
the treatment of material announcements made in low-attention windows.
Courts have addressed strategic timing as a component of market
manipulation, recognizing that actors who time disclosures to minimize
market response - or time trading to precede their own disclosures - are
exploiting a temporal advantage structurally equivalent in effect to the
informational advantage that insider trading prohibitions address
directly.

***Detection Surface***

Temporal asymmetry produces a characteristic clustering signature in a
coordination record: consequential governance actions that consistently
occur in low-participation windows, and a correlation between the
interests of specific actor classes and the timing patterns of
governance activity that affects them. Neither pattern requires
establishing intent. Both are observable in the structural record of
when decisions were made relative to participation capacity.

The primary detection signals are three. First, timing concentration in
low-participation windows: governance proposals, parameter changes, or
constitutional amendments that are consistently introduced during
periods when participation historically drops - overnight hours for
globally distributed organizations, holiday periods, community crisis
periods when attention is absorbed elsewhere. Second, timing-interest
correlation: the pattern of which actor class benefits from actions
taken in low-participation windows, and whether that correlation is
consistent with strategic selection of timing rather than random
distribution. Third, participation capacity asymmetry: the structural
condition in which certain participant populations have systematically
lower capacity to respond within standard governance windows due to time
zone, work schedule, or resource constraints, and whether that asymmetry
is reflected in governance outcomes that consistently disadvantage those
populations.

A coordination record with no temporal concentration signal in its
governance action history is either a record of an unstressed system or
a record that does not capture the timing dimension of governance acts
with enough resolution to make the signal visible. The absence of timing
data in a coordination record is itself a signal about the record\'s
integrity: organizations that do not document when governance actions
were introduced relative to participation patterns are organizations
where temporal asymmetry cannot be detected regardless of its presence.

**Class 3: Interpretive Asymmetry**

Interpretive asymmetry arises when one party controls the framework
through which information is classified, defined, or evaluated, and uses
that control to shape outcomes without appearing to do so. Both parties
may have access to the same underlying data. The asymmetry operates at
the level above the data: who determines what the data means, which
categories apply, what counts as a significant event, and what falls
outside the scope of the relevant framework. Control of the interpretive
layer is structural power that does not require any individual act of
misrepresentation to produce systematic distortion.

A precision that distinguishes this class from a simple description of
definitional power: interpretive asymmetry operates not only through
formal control of the framework but through differential capacity to
apply it. Even when a framework is publicly available and formally
shared, participants operating at different levels of what might be
called interpretive bandwidth - the capacity to hold and navigate the
full consequence space of a given framework simultaneously - are not
functionally operating in the same interpretive context. Distributing
the text of a framework is not the same as distributing the capacity to
read, understand, and act on its implications. This differential
bandwidth dimension means that interpretive asymmetry can persist even
in formally transparent systems, because transparency of the framework
does not equalize the capacity to use it.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The clearest regulatory precedent for interpretive asymmetry is the
history of accounting standard-setting and the documented pattern of
industry capture of standard-setting bodies. The Financial Accounting
Standards Board (FASB), established in 1973 as the designated private
standard-setter for U.S. generally accepted accounting principles,
operates through a process in which the entities whose financial
reporting is being measured participate substantially in developing the
standards that govern that measurement. This participation is deliberate
and often produces better standards: practitioners understand the
practical implications of accounting rules in ways that outsiders do
not. But it also creates a structural condition in which those with the
most to gain from particular interpretive frameworks have
disproportionate access to the processes that produce them.

The Enron Corporation failure, which became public in October 2001 and
resulted in the largest U.S. bankruptcy filing at that time, provides
the most extensively documented case study of interpretive asymmetry
through accounting framework control. The mechanism was not primarily
fraudulent misrepresentation of data. Enron\'s executives hired
accountants who had experience working with FASB on the development of
accounting rules, specifically to identify and exploit interpretive
space within technically compliant GAAP frameworks. The central vehicle
was the special purpose entity (SPE), a legitimate accounting structure
whose non-consolidation treatment was governed by a GAAP threshold: a
sponsor was not required to consolidate an SPE into its financial
statements if an independent third party held at least 3% of the SPE\'s
equity capital. Enron structured thousands of SPEs using this threshold,
often with circular financing arrangements that rendered the
independence requirement formally satisfied but economically hollow,
keeping billions of dollars of debt off its consolidated balance sheet.
The resulting financial statements were technically compliant. They were
also profoundly misleading - not because the data was false but because
the interpretive framework through which the data was organized had been
structured to permit concealment.

The regulatory response was framework-level, not disclosure-level. FASB
issued Interpretation No. 46 (FIN 46) in January 2003, replacing the
voting-interest consolidation model with a variable interest entity
model that required consolidation based on economic exposure rather than
formal equity structure. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 created the
Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB), shifting auditing
oversight away from the profession\'s self-regulatory structure. Both
responses recognized that the harm was in the interpretive architecture,
not in the absence of data, and that remedying it required changing the
framework rather than requiring additional disclosure within the
existing one.

***Detection Surface***

Interpretive asymmetry leaves a distinctive signature in governance
records: a pattern in which framework-level decisions - definitions of
what counts as a significant event, classification criteria, scope
determinations - are concentrated in a small deliberative group, made
without documented dissent, and produce systematic gaps in what the
record can see. The key signal is not what the framework captures but
what it structurally excludes, and whether that exclusion consistently
serves the interests of the parties who participated in developing it.

The primary detection signals are three. First, framework concentration:
the pattern of who participates in taxonomy and classification
decisions, whether that participation is proportional to the participant
population whose interests the framework governs, and whether dissenting
interpretations are documented or suppressed. Second, systematic
exclusion: the pattern of event types, actor behaviors, or coordination
outcomes that the framework has no category for, examined against the
question of whose interests are served by the absence of those
categories. Third, interpretive bandwidth stratification: evidence that
the operational consequences of a framework are legible to a small set
of participants with specialized knowledge while remaining effectively
unreadable to the broader participant population, combined with
governance outcomes that consistently favor the informed minority. This
last signal is closely related to complexity asymmetry but differs in
mechanism: complexity asymmetry involves the deployment of genuine
technical complexity as an extraction tool; interpretive asymmetry
involves the control of the meaning-making framework itself, which may
or may not involve complexity as an instrument.

The meta-level detection challenge for interpretive asymmetry is that
the detection taxonomy is itself an interpretive framework, subject to
the same capture dynamics it is designed to detect. An organization that
successfully captures the interpretive framework governing what counts
as a coordination failure has eliminated its own detection surface. This
is the highest-order failure mode available to any detection-oriented
governance system, and it requires that taxonomy governance - who
determines what the framework can see - be itself subject to the most
stringent structural scrutiny the system can provide.

**Class 4: Relational Asymmetry**

Relational asymmetry arises when access to consequential governance
decisions is mediated by relationship networks that are not structurally
visible to all participants and that are not proportionally available to
all participants. The asymmetry is not in what information exists but in
who is consulted before formal processes begin, whose concerns are
incorporated into proposals before they are presented, and whose
objections are resolved informally before they can be raised on the
record. Dense relational networks operating as coordination
infrastructure create a parallel governance layer that is structurally
invisible to participants outside the network but that substantially
determines the outcomes of formal processes. Formal procedures then
ratify decisions whose substantive content was determined in a
relational layer that left no accessible record.

The distinction from positional asymmetry is structural, not merely
analytic. Positional asymmetry derives from formal role: the authority
to see or decide that attaches to a position in an organizational
hierarchy. Relational asymmetry derives from network density: the access
that attaches to relationships accumulated outside formal role
structures. A participant with no formal authority can exercise
substantial relational asymmetry through dense connections to
decision-makers; a participant with high formal authority can be
relationally isolated and therefore functionally excluded from the
pre-decisional coordination layer. Both forms frequently co-occur -
formal positions tend to generate relational capital over time - but
they are analytically and architecturally separable, and remedying one
does not automatically remediate the other.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The primary regulatory precedent for relational asymmetry in governance
structures is Section 8 of the Clayton Act, enacted as part of the
original Clayton Antitrust Act in 1914 and substantially in its current
form since a 1990 amendment that extended its reach from directors to
officers. Section 8 prohibits any person from simultaneously serving as
a director or officer of two competing corporations, subject to
jurisdictional thresholds adjusted annually by the Federal Trade
Commission. The statute is a per se prohibition: enforcement does not
require demonstration of actual anticompetitive conduct. The theory
underlying Section 8 is structural rather than behavioral. Courts and
enforcement agencies have described its purpose as designed to nip in
the bud incipient violations of the antitrust laws by removing the
opportunity or temptation to such violations through interlocking
directorates. The harm Section 8 addresses is the existence of a
relational conduit through which competitively sensitive information
flows and through which coordination between nominally independent
competitors becomes structurally available, regardless of whether that
coordination is ever formally executed.

The DOJ Antitrust Division\'s enforcement posture shifted substantially
beginning in 2022, when Assistant Attorney General Jonathan Kanter
announced the broadest Section 8 enforcement campaign in the statute\'s
history. By 2023, the Division had caused more than fourteen directors
to resign from corporate board positions across multiple industries. The
DOJ extended Section 8\'s reach through a deputization theory: when a
single investment firm appoints different individuals to the boards of
competing companies, those individuals are treated as deputies of the
appointing firm rather than independent actors, and the firm itself is
treated as the prohibited person holding interlocking positions. The FTC
issued its first formal Section 8 complaint in forty years in August
2023 in the Quantum/EQT matter, prohibiting Quantum from occupying a
board seat at EQT on the grounds that the interlock would facilitate the
exchange of confidential and competitively sensitive information. The
enforcement revival reflects regulatory recognition that relational
asymmetry through interlocking governance positions produces structural
harm that operates independently of any specific act of coordination.

The antitrust framework for platform market power provides complementary
precedent at a different scale. Where Section 8 addresses relational
asymmetry created by personnel overlap between nominally separate
organizations, antitrust doctrine governing platform dominance and
network effects addresses relational asymmetry created by differential
access to coordination infrastructure itself. Participants with early or
privileged access to a coordination platform accumulate relational
capital - connections, reputation, transaction history - that compounds
over time and creates structural barriers to equivalent participation by
later entrants or less-connected participants. This dynamic is
recognized in the antitrust treatment of network effects as a component
of market power: the DOJ\'s 2023 complaint against Google\'s search
advertising dominance, the FTC\'s ongoing proceedings against Meta
regarding acquisitions of Instagram and WhatsApp, and the European
Commission\'s Digital Markets Act enforcement actions all reflect
regulatory acknowledgment that relational infrastructure creates durable
asymmetries that are not remedied by nominal access to the same
platform.

***Detection Surface***

Relational asymmetry leaves a specific signature in coordination
records: a pattern in which formal processes produce outcomes that
cannot be fully explained by the documented deliberation that preceded
them. Proposals arrive at formal decision points already substantially
shaped. Objections are resolved before they appear in the record. Votes
are unanimous or near-unanimous in environments where the underlying
interests of participants are genuinely diverse. The formal record shows
consensus where the relational layer produced alignment - but the
relational layer is not in the record.

The primary detection signals are three. First, pre-decisional
convergence: the pattern in which proposals arrive at formal stages with
support already assembled, measured against the question of whether that
assembly is documented anywhere in the coordination record or whether it
occurred in channels that leave no accessible trace. Second, network
density stratification: the mapping of communication and consultation
patterns prior to formal governance actions against the full participant
population, identifying whether a dense relational cluster consistently
participates in pre-decisional coordination while a broader population
engages only at formal stages. Third, outcome-deliberation gap: the
pattern in which formal deliberation records do not contain the
substantive arguments that would be necessary to produce the documented
outcome, indicating that the deliberation producing the outcome occurred
elsewhere.

The practical challenge of detecting relational asymmetry in governance
records is that the relational layer is definitionally not in the formal
record. Detection therefore depends on structural inference: does the
formal record contain enough of the reasoning that produced the outcome
to make the outcome legible without reference to undocumented
pre-decisional coordination? A coordination record that consistently
produces legible outcomes from documented deliberation is a record with
low relational asymmetry signal. A coordination record that consistently
produces outcomes that require reference to informal context to
understand is a record in which relational asymmetry is operating but
not visible. The absence of the informal context from the record is the
signal.

**Class 5: Omission Asymmetry**

Omission asymmetry arises when one party controls what enters the
coordination record and systematically excludes information that would
be material to other participants\' ability to evaluate decisions,
outcomes, or the actors responsible for them. The mechanism is not
misrepresentation of what is included but the structuring of what is
absent. A record that is internally consistent and factually accurate in
every entry it contains can simultaneously be deeply misleading if the
selection of what enters the record is itself a governance act that has
not been subjected to scrutiny. Omission asymmetry is the class in which
the record\'s silences are as consequential as its contents - and in
which the silences are not randomly distributed.

The distinction from interpretive asymmetry is precise. Interpretive
asymmetry operates through control of the framework that determines what
data means. Omission asymmetry operates upstream of that: it determines
what data exists in the record at all. An actor exercising interpretive
asymmetry shapes how available information is classified. An actor
exercising omission asymmetry shapes which information is available to
be classified. The two classes interact structurally - omission is often
the more powerful lever because it eliminates the surface on which
interpretive contest could even occur - but they are analytically
separable and require distinct detection approaches. A record can be
interpretively captured while remaining complete; it can be selectively
complete while being interpretively neutral. The combination is the most
dangerous configuration.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The primary regulatory precedent for omission asymmetry is the
materiality doctrine under United States securities law and its
codification in the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. The materiality
standard - established through Basic Inc. v. Levinson, 485 U.S. 224
(1988) and its antecedents - holds that information is material if there
is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable investor would consider it
important in making an investment decision. The standard explicitly
encompasses omissions: under Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange
Act of 1934 and SEC Rule 10b-5, liability attaches to material omissions
as fully as to affirmative misrepresentations. The legal equivalence of
omission and misrepresentation reflects a regulatory determination that
the harm to information asymmetry between parties is identical
regardless of whether the distortion is produced by false statement or
strategic silence.

The WorldCom accounting fraud, which became public in June 2002 and
resulted in what was at that time the largest bankruptcy filing in
United States history, provides the definitive case study for omission
asymmetry operating at scale. The mechanism of fraud at WorldCom was not
primarily the introduction of false data into financial records. It was
the systematic omission of a classification: more than nine billion
dollars in ordinary operating expenses - line costs, the fees WorldCom
paid to other telecommunications carriers to use their networks - were
excluded from the expense category where they belonged and recorded
instead as capital assets. The entries that appeared in WorldCom\'s
financial statements were not fabricated. The category that should have
captured those costs was simply absent from the reported figures.
External investors and analysts received financial statements in which
the operating expense line was structurally incomplete, and the
incompleteness was not disclosed. The fraud was discovered not by
external auditors but by WorldCom\'s internal audit team, led by Cynthia
Cooper, whose investigation identified the \$3.8 billion initial
discrepancy by tracing entries that should have appeared in the expense
record but did not.

The regulatory response to WorldCom and the cluster of contemporaneous
accounting failures culminated in the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, enacted July
30, 2002. The Act\'s treatment of omission asymmetry is concentrated in
two provisions. Section 302 requires the CEO and CFO of every reporting
public company to personally certify, on a quarterly and annual basis,
that the periodic report does not contain any untrue statement of
material fact or omit to state a material fact necessary to make the
statements made, in light of the circumstances under which such
statements were made, not misleading. The certification explicitly
covers omissions as a distinct category of potential violation, not as a
subset of misrepresentation. Section 906 establishes criminal penalties
for knowing false certifications, with imprisonment of up to ten years
for knowing violations and up to twenty years for willful violations.
The combined effect of Sections 302 and 906 is to place personal
criminal liability on the officers responsible for the completeness of
the record, treating strategic omission as equivalent in culpability to
affirmative fraud. Section 404 further requires management to assess and
report on the effectiveness of internal controls over financial
reporting - a structural acknowledgment that the completeness of records
is itself a governance process that requires dedicated oversight
architecture.

***Detection Surface***

Omission asymmetry produces a characteristic signature that differs from
the signatures of the other five classes. The other classes leave
positive traces - patterns in what the record contains. Omission
asymmetry is detected primarily through structural inference about what
the record should contain given what is known about the system, compared
against what the record actually contains. The signal is in the gap
between expected record completeness and observed record completeness,
measured against the question of whose interests are systematically
served by the gaps.

The primary detection signals are three. First, category absence: the
pattern in which an entire class of governance-relevant events -
objections raised and withdrawn, proposals considered and dropped before
formalization, concerns documented in informal channels but not in the
official record - has no representation in the coordination record.
Category absence is distinct from low incidence: a category that is
present but rarely triggered is different from a category for which no
entries exist despite the system operating continuously. The absence of
any record of disagreement in a governance environment where participant
interests are genuinely diverse is a strong omission signal. Second,
resolution gap: the pattern in which problems or concerns that are known
to have existed at an earlier stage in a governance process are absent
from the formal record of that process\'s resolution. If a concern was
raised informally and then the formal record shows a unanimous decision,
the question of where the concern was addressed - and whether it was
addressed substantively or simply removed from the record - is an
omission detection question. Third, scope boundary concentration: the
pattern in which the boundary of what the record covers is itself a
governance decision that was made by a small set of actors whose
interests are served by the boundary being drawn where it is. What the
record is defined to capture is itself a governance act; when that
definition excludes categories that would primarily implicate the actors
who defined it, the definition is an omission asymmetry instrument.

The meta-level challenge for detecting omission asymmetry is identical
to that of interpretive asymmetry, but one layer more fundamental: the
detection taxonomy can only surface omissions it has categories for, and
a system that has successfully omitted an entire category of event has
also eliminated the taxonomic surface on which that omission would be
detectable. The response to this structural problem is not to make
detection taxonomies exhaustive - that is not achievable - but to
require that the governance of the record\'s scope be itself on the
record, legible, and subject to participation by the actors whose
interests the record is intended to protect. A record-scope decision
made by the actors the record is meant to hold accountable is an
omission asymmetry instrument regardless of its specific content.

**Class 6: Complexity Asymmetry**

Complexity asymmetry arises when genuine technical complexity is
deployed as a coordination instrument: not incidentally difficult but
structured to be navigable only by participants with specialized
knowledge, capital, or access, while remaining nominally available to
all. The asymmetry is not in who is permitted to participate but in who
can effectively participate given the cognitive and resource demands of
doing so. A governance process, financial product, or coordination
record that is formally open but operationally accessible only to a
small class of participants creates a structural advantage for that
class that is not visible as exclusion from outside. Participants who
lack the capacity to process the complexity are present in the process
but functionally absent from the decision.

The distinguishing feature of complexity asymmetry relative to the other
five classes is that the underlying information or process may be
genuinely, irreducibly complex. Not all complexity asymmetry involves
deliberate obfuscation: some results from accumulated technical debt in
governance systems, some from genuine domain complexity that resists
simplification without loss, and some from the organizational incentive
to produce thorough rather than legible records. The harm is the same in
each case - differential capacity to participate produces differential
influence over outcomes - but the remediation differs. Deliberate
obfuscation calls for structural simplification as a governance
requirement. Genuine irreducible complexity calls for translation
infrastructure: mechanisms that make the operational consequences of
complex processes legible to participants who cannot engage the
complexity directly. The detection challenge is distinguishing between
the two, because actors deploying complexity as an extraction tool have
strong incentives to characterize their obfuscation as irreducible.

***Regulatory Precedent***

The primary regulatory precedent for complexity asymmetry spans two
distinct legal traditions that address the same underlying harm from
different angles. The first is the disclosure simplification tradition
established by the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), enacted in 1968 as Title
I of the Consumer Credit Protection Act (Pub. L. 90-321). TILA\'s
foundational premise is that complexity in credit terms - even fully
disclosed complexity - produces harm by preventing consumers from making
meaningful comparisons and informed decisions. The Act\'s response was
not to require disclosure of all terms but to require standardized
disclosure of a small set of terms in a specified format: the annual
percentage rate, finance charges, total amount financed, and total
payments. The imposition of a common disclosure format was a regulatory
determination that complexity in the representation of equivalent
information is itself a harm, not merely an inconvenience. Subsequent
amendments - including the Truth in Lending Simplification and Reform
Act of 1980, the Fair Credit and Charge Card Disclosure Act of 1988, and
the Dodd-Frank Act\'s TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure rule of 2015 -
have consistently applied the same principle: standardization of
disclosure format reduces complexity asymmetry independent of the
complexity of the underlying product.

The second tradition is the abusiveness doctrine established by the
Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 and
administered by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) through
its Unfair, Deceptive, or Abusive Acts or Practices (UDAAP) enforcement
authority. The abusiveness prong of UDAAP is analytically distinct from
unfairness and deception, and its distinctive contribution is the
explicit recognition of complexity as a harm vector independent of
misrepresentation. Under the Dodd-Frank Act\'s Section 1031, an act or
practice is abusive if it materially interferes with the ability of a
consumer to understand a term or condition of a consumer financial
product or service. The CFPB\'s 2023 Policy Statement on Abusiveness
articulated the operational consequence: a product so complicated that
material information about it cannot be sufficiently explained to
consumers may violate the material interference provision on its own,
even if all terms are fully and accurately disclosed. The doctrine\'s
further reach is that abusiveness liability does not require a showing
that the covered person created the consumer\'s lack of understanding:
taking unreasonable advantage of a pre-existing capacity differential is
sufficient for a violation. This is a regulatory recognition that
complexity asymmetry can be exploited without having been engineered,
and that the exploitation is the harm regardless of the asymmetry\'s
origin.

The 2008 financial crisis provides the large-scale case study for
complexity asymmetry operating across an entire market. The
collateralized debt obligation (CDO) and synthetic CDO structures that
distributed subprime mortgage risk throughout the global financial
system were not, in the main, fraudulent instruments. The underlying
assets, the tranching structures, the credit enhancement mechanisms, and
the rating methodologies were disclosed in offering documents that ran
to hundreds of pages. The complexity of those documents was not
incidental to the product design: it was constitutive of it. The
structures were navigable only by participants with the quantitative
modeling capacity to trace cash flow waterfalls under stress scenarios,
the legal resources to parse dense indenture language, and the
institutional access to obtain the data necessary to evaluate underlying
asset quality. Retail investors, municipal treasuries, pension funds,
and the credit rating agencies themselves - whose models relied on
historical default correlations that the new instruments structurally
violated - were nominally participants in the same market but were
operationally operating in a different one. The Financial Crisis Inquiry
Commission\'s 2011 report documented this asymmetry extensively, finding
that the complexity of the instruments was a primary mechanism through
which risk was transferred from parties with the capacity to price it to
parties without that capacity.

***Detection Surface***

Complexity asymmetry produces a distinctive detection signature because
it leaves traces in the relationship between the complexity of the
governance record and the distribution of participation in governance
decisions. A governance environment in which participation is broadly
distributed but comprehension of governance processes is narrowly held
is a governance environment in which complexity asymmetry is
structurally present, regardless of whether it is deliberately deployed.

The primary detection signals are three. First,
participation-comprehension stratification: the pattern in which
participation rates across the full participant population are not
accompanied by equivalent rates of substantive engagement, measured by
whether participants who engage the formal process have access to
translation resources - plain-language summaries, accessibility-oriented
documentation, participatory formats that do not require technical
fluency - that are absent from the process as designed. Nominal
participation without comprehension infrastructure is a complexity
asymmetry signal. Second, specialist concentration: the pattern in which
governance outcomes consistently favor the positions of participants
with specialist knowledge over the positions of participants without it,
in environments where the interests of those groups are genuinely
divergent. Specialist advantage in technical governance is expected;
systematic specialist advantage across governance decisions that are not
genuinely technical in character indicates that complexity is serving as
a filter rather than a relevant constraint. Third, simplification
resistance: the pattern in which attempts to produce accessible
summaries of governance processes or their consequences are opposed,
delayed, or rendered optional by the actors whose positions are favored
by the complexity of the current format. Actors who benefit from
complexity asymmetry have structural incentives to characterize
accessible translation as inaccurate, oversimplified, or unsuitable -
and to ensure that participation formats that would enable broader
engagement remain unavailable.

The boundary between necessary and exploited complexity is itself a
governance question, and one that is contested. The appropriate
analytical approach is not to treat all complexity as suspect but to ask
whether the complexity level of the coordination record and governance
process is calibrated to the complexity level of the underlying
coordination problem, or whether it substantially exceeds it. Governance
of a genuinely complex technical system requires technical
documentation; that documentation need not also be the primary interface
through which participants exercise governance rights. A coordination
system that conflates technical documentation with governance
participation infrastructure has made a design choice that produces
complexity asymmetry as a structural outcome, regardless of intent.

**Section 4: Interaction Patterns and Co-Occurrence**

The six classes are analytically distinct but operationally
interdependent. In practice, information asymmetry regimes rarely
involve a single class in isolation. The more durable and consequential
asymmetry configurations involve multiple classes operating in
reinforcing combination, where the presence of one class reduces the
detectability of others and the remediation of one leaves the others
intact.

Three interaction patterns recur with sufficient regularity to merit
specific identification. The first is the positional-relational
compound: positional asymmetry (Class 1) and relational asymmetry (Class
4) co-occurring in environments where formal role concentration and
informal network density are held by the same actor cluster. This
configuration is self-reinforcing because formal positions generate
relational capital and relational capital facilitates accumulation of
formal positions. The compound is significantly more durable than either
class alone because remediation of the formal position structure leaves
the relational network intact, and the relational network reconstitutes
formal position concentration over time.

The second pattern is the interpretive-omission compound: interpretive
asymmetry (Class 3) and omission asymmetry (Class 5) co-occurring when
the actors who control the classification framework also control the
scope of the record. This configuration is particularly resistant to
detection because the omissions are defined as outside the scope of the
framework, and the framework is defined as the correct lens through
which the record is evaluated. External challenge to the framework is
characterizable as methodological disagreement rather than
accountability claim, and the omissions are not visible as omissions
within the framework\'s own terms.

The third pattern is the complexity-omission compound: complexity
asymmetry (Class 6) and omission asymmetry (Class 5) co-occurring when
the complexity of the formal record makes it impractical for most
participants to identify what is absent. Complexity serves as a
detection suppressor for omission in this configuration: participants
who lack the capacity to process the full record cannot distinguish
between records that are complex and complete and records that are
complex and strategically incomplete. The combination is especially
common in technical governance environments where the complexity of the
subject matter is genuine but the record scope is controlled by a small
actor class.

**Section 4.1: Cynefin Domain Mapping and Detection Action Modes**

Each asymmetry class has a characteristic Cynefin domain profile that
determines what kind of detection process is appropriate. The Cynefin
framework defines four action modes: sense-categorize-respond (Clear),
sense-analyze-respond (Complicated), probe-sense-respond (Complex), and
act-sense-respond (Chaotic). Applying the wrong action mode to a
detection task produces either false negatives (the detection process
cannot see the pattern) or false positives (the detection process
manufactures patterns from noise). The mapping below identifies the
typical Cynefin domain for each class and the implied detection action
mode.

Positional asymmetry (Class 1) is typically a Complicated-domain
detection problem. The advantage is structurally present and analyzable
with network analysis expertise: who holds which positions, how those
positions correlate with information access, and whether proposal timing
patterns correlate with positional access. The detection action mode is
sense-analyze-respond. Temporal asymmetry (Class 2) is typically a
Complicated-domain detection problem as well, analyzable through
statistical timing analysis. The detection action mode is
sense-analyze-respond with time-series methods. Interpretive asymmetry
(Class 3) is typically a Complex-domain detection problem, because the
framework through which you would analyze the asymmetry is itself the
thing being manipulated. A detection process that applies
sense-analyze-respond to interpretive asymmetry uses the potentially
captured analytical framework to assess whether the analytical framework
has been captured; this is circular. The detection action mode is
probe-sense-respond: propose alternative interpretive frameworks,
observe whether the system resists them, and use the resistance pattern
as the detection signal. Relational asymmetry (Class 4) is typically a
Complex-domain detection problem. The relational layer is by definition
not in the formal record; detection depends on structural inference from
patterns that emerge over time. The detection action mode is
probe-sense-respond: observe whether formal outcomes consistently
diverge from what the documented deliberation would predict, and whether
that divergence correlates with informal relational structures. Omission
asymmetry (Class 5) is difficult to classify because what is missing
cannot be analyzed until someone perceives its absence. Detection
oscillates between Complicated (once the category of omission is named,
it can be searched for) and Complex (before the category is named,
detection requires probing for absences that the current taxonomy cannot
specify). The initial detection action mode is probe-sense-respond; once
an omission pattern is identified and named, subsequent detection shifts
to sense-analyze-respond. Complexity asymmetry (Class 6) is typically a
Complicated-domain detection problem, analyzable through comparison of
governance record complexity against the complexity level of the
decisions being made and the participation distribution across
complexity strata. The detection action mode is sense-analyze-respond,
but with the caveat that a successful complexity asymmetry attack
degrades the evaluator's capacity to perform the analysis (this is a
bandwidth constraint; see the Structural Consent and Legibility
Standard, Section 6.2).

Two additional Cynefin dimensions bear on the class mappings above.
Interpretive asymmetry (Class 3) detection inherently requires at least
double-loop learning, because single-loop responses (applying the
existing analytical framework to assess whether the analytical framework
has been captured) use the potentially compromised tool to evaluate
itself. The detection process must question the framework, not only
apply it. This is a structural constraint on detection architecture: a
system that supports only single-loop learning in its interpretive
asymmetry detection will systematically miss Class 3 signals. Omission
asymmetry (Class 5) exhibits a characteristic domain circuit: detection
begins in the Complex domain (probing for unnamed absences), transitions
to the Complicated domain once the omission category is named and
analyzable with standard methods, and may stabilize in the Clear domain
if the omission pattern becomes procedurally detectable. This
Complex-to-Complicated-to-Clear transition is expected, and detection
architectures should be designed to accommodate the domain shift within
a single asymmetry class rather than assuming a fixed detection mode.
See: Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard, Section 3.3.7
for learning loop depth per Cynefin domain; see also detection layer technical documentation for domain circuit trajectory as a plausibility signal.

The co-occurrence patterns identified in Section 4 interact with this
mapping. When two classes with different Cynefin profiles compound, the
detection process must accommodate both action modes. The
positional-interpretive compound, for example, combines a
Complicated-domain signal (positional concentration) with a
Complex-domain signal (framework capture); a detection architecture that
treats the compound as purely Complicated will miss the interpretive
component. The general principle: when asymmetry classes compound across
Cynefin domains, the detection process should default to the more
complex action mode. See: Structural Consent and Legibility Standard,
Section 6.4 for the general principle of Cynefin action mode
calibration.

**Section 5: Scope and Limitations**

This classification standard is domain-general by design. The six
classes and their detection surfaces are derived from documented
patterns across financial markets, corporate governance, regulatory
history, and organizational behavior. They are intended to be applicable
across governance contexts - including organizational, protocol, and
community governance - without modification to the core class
definitions, while acknowledging that the specific detection signals and
regulatory analogues will vary by domain.

Several limitations constrain the standard\'s current form. First, the
six classes are not claimed to be exhaustive. They represent the classes
for which both a documented mechanism and a regulatory precedent chain
have been identified. Other classes may exist for which the mechanism is
understood but the regulatory recognition is not yet established, or for
which neither dimension has been adequately characterized. The taxonomy
is open to extension through the same methodology used to establish the
current six classes: mechanism identification, boundary articulation
against adjacent classes, and regulatory precedent verification. Second,
the interaction patterns identified in Section 4 are illustrative rather
than comprehensive. The three documented compounds are the most
frequently observed, but the space of possible interactions across six
classes is large and not fully characterized. Third, the detection
surfaces described for each class identify signals that are necessary
but not individually sufficient for a finding of asymmetry. The signals
indicate where to look; they do not constitute proof of the pattern\'s
presence or causal attribution of harm. Detection using this standard
requires contextual judgment and the assembly of multiple signals into a
coherent account of the coordination failure.

The standard does not address remediation architectures. Each class
implies a different remediation approach - positional asymmetry calls
for role-information disclosure requirements, temporal asymmetry for
participation timing protections, interpretive asymmetry for framework
governance and taxonomic independence, relational asymmetry for
pre-decisional coordination documentation, omission asymmetry for record
scope governance, and complexity asymmetry for translation
infrastructure and comprehension parity requirements. Those
architectures are beyond the scope of this document and are addressed in
companion specifications.

**Section 6: Tiered Adoption**

This standard can be adopted at five tiers. The tiers describe the organization's
accountability loop closure around information asymmetry detection, from initial
mapping through independent auditability.

**Tier 1: IACS-Assessed.** The system has mapped itself against the standard. The
six asymmetry classes are identified as applicable, partially applicable, or not
applicable to the system's operations, with rationale. No process changes are
required at this tier. No conformance claim is made.

Minimum requirement: a class applicability inventory naming which of the six
classes are relevant to the system's operations, where detection is currently
present, where it is assumed, and where it is absent.

**Tier 2: IACS-Operational.** The system has defined processes for classifying
information asymmetry signals against the six-class taxonomy. The classification
taxonomy is integrated into the system's governance workflow. Asymmetry
observations are recorded in a durable form.

Minimum requirement: for each applicable class, a documented classification
process using the class definitions, mechanism descriptions, and boundary
criteria in this standard. Observations are logged with class designation and
detection surface reference.

**Tier 3: IACS-Instrumented.** The system has deployed a detection architecture
that instruments the detection surfaces specified for each applicable class
against a continuous coordination record. Detection is real-time or near-real-
time rather than retrospective. The system can observe its own asymmetry state
without depending on voluntary disclosure.

Minimum requirement: detection architecture covering all applicable classes,
with documented detection surfaces per class, documented Cynefin domain
classification for each class per Section 4.1, and appropriate action mode
(probe-sense-respond for Complex-domain classes; sense-analyze-respond for
Complicated-domain classes). The detection architecture is disclosed to the
system's participant population.

**Tier 4: IACS-Accountable.** Detection at IACS-Instrumented produces mandatory
governance response. Detected asymmetry signals trigger defined review with
documented outcomes. Governance inaction in the presence of a detection output
is itself detectable without the cooperation of the inacting party. The governance
loop is closed at this tier.

Minimum requirements:

Every asymmetry detection output that reaches the governance response threshold must produce a documented governance review within a defined window. The review record must include: the asymmetry signal identifier, the class designation assigned (positional, temporal, interpretive, relational, complexity, or omission per Section 3), the Cynefin domain classification applied, the review outcome (including decisions to address, accept with rationale, or defer with timeline), the actor or body responsible for the review, and the rationale. A decision to accept a detected asymmetry without correction is a decision and must be documented with rationale. The absence of any documented review is not a permissible outcome at IACS-Accountable.

The absence of a documented review within the defined window must produce automatic disclosure to all governance actors without requiring any actor's initiation. This disclosure makes the asymmetry detection output, its class designation, the elapsed time since detection, and the absence of a governance review simultaneously visible. A system in which this disclosure requires a human decision to publish has not closed the governance loop and does not meet IACS-Accountable.

The governance review record is append-only. Reviews cannot be modified after recording; corrections are recorded as new entries referencing the original. This property is what makes governance inaction detectable without cooperation: an independent observer can verify that an asymmetry detection output exists in the record, verify that no review entry exists within the required window, and determine that automatic disclosure was or was not produced as a consequence.

Tier review cadence for IACS-Accountable systems: tier review MUST occur at minimum annually and MUST include a governance loop integrity check: confirmation that the detection-to-review path operated as specified for all asymmetry detection outputs in the review period, and that automatic disclosures were produced where required. The annual review must also include a class applicability reconfirmation: the set of applicable asymmetry classes is reviewed against the system's current operations and updated if operational scope has changed.

**Tier 5: IACS-Auditable.** The full detection and response architecture is independently auditable by a party outside the system without depending on self-certification. Tier 5 is the gold standard for systems seeking external validation of IACS conformance.

Includes all IACS-Accountable requirements plus independent auditability.

Minimum requirements:

The complete asymmetry detection record, from signal generation through documented governance review or automatic disclosure, must be accessible to an independent auditor. Access is not contingent on operator consent during a defined audit period. The record format is sufficient for an auditor to verify completeness without access to the detection layer itself.

The auditor must be able to verify completeness: that all asymmetry detection outputs generated in the audit period are present in the record. This requires that the detection architecture produces a verifiable count or hash of detection outputs generated per class, against which the record can be checked. A record that can only be verified for presence (each signal in the record was detected) but not for completeness (all detected signals are in the record) does not meet IACS-Auditable.

The auditor must be able to verify classification integrity: that each recorded asymmetry signal was classified against the six-class taxonomy in this standard, that the class designation applied is consistent with the detection evidence and mechanism descriptions in Section 3, and that the Cynefin domain classification applied is consistent with Section 4.1.

The auditor must be able to verify response completeness: that every asymmetry detection output that reached the governance response threshold produced either a documented governance review within the specified window or an automatic disclosure. Gaps in this chain are reportable findings.

The audit itself must be documented: scope, period, auditor identity or body, findings, and system response to findings. Audit reports are retained in the governance record under the same append-only constraints as governance reviews.

IACS-Auditable systems must make audit reports available to their participant population within a defined period after audit completion. The disclosure period is specified by the system and subject to the same precision requirements as all other IACS obligations.

**Section 7: Relationship to the Commitment Standards within the Structural Integrity Layer**

This standard is one of the five Commitment Standards within the Structural Integrity
Layer: the Precision-First Design Standard, the Adverse-Signal Engagement
Principle Core Standard, the Structural Consent Legibility Standard, the
Information Asymmetry Classification Standard, and the Structural Power
Distribution Standard. Each standard addresses a distinct failure mode. Each is
independently valid: no standard requires the others for its own validity. Together,
they form a reinforcing set. A system that satisfies all five has addressed the
structural conditions under which accountability failure becomes predictable.

Systems adopting any one of the five Commitment Standards are strongly recommended to adopt
all five. The full Commitment Standards designation applies to systems that
adopt all five at Tier 4 (Accountable) or above. The designation
is descriptive: a system either satisfies all five standards at Tier 4 or above, or
it does not. Partial adoption at any tier is a legitimate developmental stage;
claiming full Commitment Standards conformance on the basis of partial adoption is structural exposure
theater. Partial adopters should produce a structural exposure disclosure naming
which standards have not been adopted and what structural exposure that leaves.

A structural exposure disclosure must contain four elements to be substantive. First, the disclosure must name each standard not adopted or not yet at Tier 4, using its full canonical name. Second, for each absent standard, the disclosure must describe in plain language the specific failure mode class that standard addresses and what becomes structurally undetectable in its absence. Third, for each absent standard, the disclosure must state a self-assessed exposure level (low, medium, or high) with a rationale sufficient for an independent reader to evaluate the assessment. Fourth, for each absent standard where compensating controls exist, the disclosure must name those controls, describe their mechanism, and explain why they are considered adequate for the assessed exposure level. Where no compensating controls exist, the disclosure must say so explicitly. A disclosure that satisfies the form without the substance is itself a precision deficit under the Precision-First Design Standard. The full conformance theater prevention architecture is specified in the Proof of Coordination Architecture document.

The Sensemaking Layer Standard governs an organization's capacity to perceive and
apply all five Commitment Standards with understanding. It is architecturally distinct from the
Commitment Standards: the Commitment Standards specify what the organization does; the sensemaking layer
addresses whether the organization can perceive what it is doing well enough to
apply the Commitment Standards with understanding.

The interaction between this standard and the Structural Power Distribution
Standard is significant: interpretive asymmetry (Class 3) is the information-
dimension expression of what the Structural Power Distribution Standard measures
as interpretive capacity concentration in the coordination dimension. Systems
applying both standards in the coordination domain will detect the same underlying
structural condition from two angles, which strengthens classification confidence.

**Section 8: Inheritance Clause**

Systems adopting this standard should include the following statement in their
governance documentation:

*Information Asymmetry Classification Inheritance: This [document/system/protocol]
adopts the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard (IACS) (v[X], [date]) at
the [IACS-Assessed / IACS-Operational / IACS-Instrumented / IACS-Accountable /
IACS-Auditable] tier. The asymmetry classes identified as applicable to this
system's operations are: [list relevant classes from the six]. Classes identified
as not applicable: [list, with rationale].*

The inheritance clause commits the adopting system to: (a) identifying which of
the six asymmetry classes are relevant to its operations, (b) applying the
taxonomy at the adopted tier, (c) treating information asymmetry signals as adverse
signals subject to the system's adverse signal processing, and (d) specifying which
classes are applicable and which are not, with rationale, so that the adoption
scope is explicit and auditable.

Adoption of this standard at any tier below the full Commitment Standards threshold (Tier 4 across all five Commitment Standards) does not constitute full Commitment Standards conformance and does not reduce structural exposure from uncovered standards. Systems in partial adoption are required to produce a structural exposure disclosure in place of a conformance claim, per the specification in the Proof of Coordination Architecture document.

# 1. Abstract

Coordination, in the structural sense used throughout this corpus, is what occurs when an agent detects a differential in its environment, changes its state in response, and leaves a trace in the medium, where the medium carries that trace forward such that future agents encounter an environment shaped by prior interactions. This definition holds across ants, immune cells, trees, and organizations. What distinguishes coordination from collision is the medium with memory: the accumulated residue of prior interactions that conditions future ones.

Shared language is coordination infrastructure. In this standard, "shared language" means: a joint artifact (a frame, a distinction, a named agreement, a named disagreement, a jointly produced description) that neither party had produced independently before the process that created it. Its presence is observable through the appearance of new shared artifacts; its absence is observable through participants using the same words to mean different things, or acting on private interpretations they have not surfaced. Sensemaking is the process that produces shared language. A coordination system that does not specify what sensemaking must structurally provide has left its most foundational dependency implicit: the dependency on participants being able to produce, together, the shared understanding that makes coordination possible rather than coincidental.

No formal sensemaking standard exists in any standards body (ISO, IEEE, W3C, IETF, NATO, or any other). The predecessor document in this corpus (Sensemaking Layer Standard v0.6, archived) specified a classification-consumption interface derived from the Cynefin framework's properties and presented as framework-agnostic. That document was architecturally a different standard: it specified how downstream mechanisms consume the outputs of domain classification, not what sensemaking itself must structurally provide. The six structural requirements it defined were Cynefin's properties elevated to universal requirements without acknowledging the epistemological inheritance.

This standard specifies what sensemaking must structurally provide in any coordination system, without prescribing method. It identifies five structural invariants of sensemaking that hold across every major sensemaking tradition surveyed (Weick, Klein, Dervin, Snowden, Heidegger, Buddhist epistemology). It specifies the three scales at which sensemaking operates. It grounds the obligation in the coordinative power of shared language, not in a claim that sensemaking is good practice.

This standard names its ontological commitments rather than claiming framework-neutrality. Research in ontological politics (Mol, Bowker and Star, Mignolo) and cross-cultural pressure testing against Chinese, African, and Indigenous philosophical traditions confirmed that framework-neutrality is not achievable at this scope. The honest design goal is: name what the standard assumes, specify which traditions are compatible, provide structural mechanisms for detecting blind spots, and do not claim a neutrality it cannot deliver.

This is the first Cohesion Standard. The five Commitment Standards (Precision-First Design, Adverse-Signal Engagement, Structural Consent Legibility, Information Asymmetry Classification, Structural Power Distribution) are compressive: they close exploitation vectors and specify structural floors. Cohesion Standards are tensile: they specify enabling conditions for coordination health. The Commitment Standards name what must not happen. The Cohesion Standards name what must be structurally present for coordination to be genuine rather than coincidental. Both are required. Neither substitutes for the other.

# 2. The Obligation Argument

The claim this standard makes is not that sensemaking is useful. It is that sensemaking is a structural dependency of coordination, and that any system which depends on coordination depends on sensemaking whether or not it names that dependency.

The argument has three legs, corresponding to the three scales at which sensemaking operates. The three legs are not three separate obligations. They are three aspects of a single structural dependency: the dependency on shared language as coordination infrastructure.

## 2.1 The Inter-Personal Leg

Coordination without shared language is coincidence of motion. When two agents or two organizations appear to be coordinating but lack shared language for what they are doing together, they are not building on each other's actions. They are moving in similar directions for separate reasons, and the appearance of coordination is a byproduct of those separate reasons happening to align temporarily. When the alignment ends, they have no shared framework for understanding why, no shared language for naming what happened, and no shared basis for repairing it.

Sensemaking is the process through which shared language is produced between agents who do not yet share it. The product is not agreement but mutual legibility: the ability to understand what the other party is actually experiencing, rather than the interpretation one's own framework produces automatically. A coordination system that depends on participants acting in concert depends on those participants having produced, through sensemaking, sufficient shared language to distinguish coordination from coincidence of motion. A system that treats this dependency as optional has not eliminated it; it has made the coordination it claims to facilitate structurally indistinguishable from coincidence.

The distinction between shared language as infrastructure and shared language as prerequisite is load-bearing. Shared language as prerequisite means participants must already speak the same language to participate: it selects for people already formed by the same intellectual tradition. Shared language as infrastructure creates conditions in which new shared understanding can emerge between parties who do not yet share it. The language is a scaffold, not a gate. This standard specifies infrastructure, not prerequisite.

## 2.2 The Intra-Personal Leg

An agent that cannot make sense of its own state cannot coordinate among its own parts. Before inter-personal sensemaking can produce shared language between agents, each agent must have sufficient sensemaking capacity to distinguish its own experience from its automatic interpretation of that experience.

This is not a claim about self-awareness as a virtue. It is a structural observation: an agent whose sensemaking capacity is depleted (through overload, suppression, sustained crisis, or deprivation of the conditions sensemaking requires) produces lower-quality inputs to inter-personal sensemaking. An organization composed of members who cannot make sense of their own experience cannot produce shared language, regardless of the quality of its processes. A coordination system that depletes individual sensemaking capacity while depending on the products of sensemaking is consuming its own substrate.

The implication for system design: coordination systems have a structural interest in maintaining the sensemaking capacity of their participants, not as a benefit to those participants (though it is one) but as a precondition for the system's own coordination function.

## 2.3 The Witness-Reception Leg

Coordination systems that persist across time depend on participants encountering and integrating the crystallized sensemaking of predecessors. Crystallized sensemaking is sensemaking output that has been encoded in a persistent medium (a document, a governance record, a standard, a protocol specification) such that it can be encountered by participants who did not participate in the sensemaking process that produced it. A future participant receives these artifacts without having participated in the process that produced them.

Witness-reception is the scale at which coordination capacity accumulates or decays across personnel transitions. A durable sensemaking artifact is one that persists in a medium accessible to future participants beyond the current interaction; a governance record entry is durable, an unrecorded conversation is not. A system that does not produce durable sensemaking artifacts, or that produces them but makes them inaccessible, or that produces accessible artifacts without sufficient context to integrate them (where "sufficient context" means the artifact names its initiating disruption, the frame it produced, and the evidence basis for that frame), loses accumulated coordination capacity every time a participant leaves and a new one arrives. The cost of this loss is rarely visible as a single event; it manifests as a gradual increase in coordination friction and a gradual decrease in the system's ability to distinguish signal from noise in its own governance record.

The most common sensemaking failure at this scale is applying crystallized sensemaking as a fixed frame without recognizing the sensemaking process that produced it or testing whether it still fits the current situation. This is the applying-without-understanding failure mode: conformance to the letter of prior sensemaking without engagement with its substance. The applying-without-understanding failure mode is detected by examining the resolution frame: if the frame applied after a disruption is identical to a frame that was available before the disruption, and no liminal phase is documented (Invariant 3.5), the failure mode is present.

## 2.4 Disconfirmation Conditions for the Obligation Argument

The obligation argument is disconfirmed if a coordination system can be demonstrated to produce genuine coordination (not coincidence of motion, as defined in Section 2.1) without any sensemaking process producing shared language between participants. Specifically: if agents in a system consistently build on each other's actions, repair coordination breakdowns, and adapt to changing conditions, all without producing shared frames, shared distinctions, or shared descriptions of what they are doing together, the argument that sensemaking is a structural dependency of coordination is false.

The argument is also weakened (though not disconfirmed) if coordination systems are identified that produce shared language through processes that violate one or more of the five structural invariants in Section 3. Such a finding would indicate that the invariants are too restrictive: sensemaking occurs, but through mechanisms this standard does not capture.

# 3. Five Structural Invariants of Sensemaking

These five properties held across every major sensemaking tradition surveyed. They specify what sensemaking must structurally be, independent of any particular tradition's vocabulary, ontological commitments, or prescribed methods. They are the standard's core requirements: a process that satisfies all five is sensemaking; a process that violates one or more is doing something else, regardless of what it calls itself.

The five invariants were identified through cross-traditional analysis of Weick's organizational sensemaking, Klein's recognition-primed decision model, Dervin's sense-making methodology, Snowden's Cynefin-based complexity sensemaking, Heidegger's phenomenological account of understanding-as-breakdown, and Buddhist epistemology's account of dependent origination and the relationship between view and conduct. Cross-cultural pressure testing against Chinese (Confucian, Daoist, Legalist, Mohist, Chan Buddhist), African (Ubuntu, Yoruba, Akan, palaver, Bantu relational ontology, Kemetic, Nguni), and Indigenous (Aboriginal Australian, Haudenosaunee, Maori, Andean, Navajo, Dagara) philosophical traditions confirmed the invariants' cross-traditional reach, while identifying structural assumptions that limit the standard's scope (Section 7).

**Joint condition and partial satisfaction.** Full satisfaction of all five invariants is the standard's requirement for a process to qualify as sensemaking. Partial satisfaction (a process that meets some invariants but not others) is diagnostically informative: it indicates which structural property of sensemaking the process provides and which it lacks. For example, a process that is disruption-occasioned and temporally structured but not action-entangled is deliberation (valuable, but not sensemaking). A process that is action-entangled and sufficiency-oriented but not disruption-occasioned is adaptive execution (also valuable, also not sensemaking). The individual invariants can be assessed independently; the joint condition is an additional requirement.

**Disconfirmation conditions for the invariants.** The invariants are disconfirmed if a sensemaking tradition is identified in which sensemaking occurs (by that tradition's own account and by observable coordination outcomes) while one or more invariants are violated. The pressure tests against Chinese, African, and Indigenous traditions tested for this; no such case was found, though the structural assumptions in Section 7 limit the scope of that finding. The invariants are also disconfirmed if a sixth property is identified that holds across all surveyed traditions and that is not derivable from the existing five.

## 3.1 Disruption-Occasioned

Sensemaking is triggered by a disruption to the ongoing flow of experience: a surprise, a gap, an expectation violation, or a situation that does not fit existing frames. It is not continuous monitoring. It is not a standing cognitive process that operates uniformly at all times. It is activated by a break in intelligibility, a moment when what was happening stops making sense.

**Operational definition.** A disruption is an event that cannot be accounted for by the current operative frame without revision of that frame: an expectation violation, an observation that does not fit existing categories, or a gap between what was predicted and what occurred. A process is disruption-occasioned if it is initiated by a specifiable disruption event and if the process would not have been initiated in the absence of that disruption.

**Satisfaction test.** Can the practitioner or observer identify the specific disruption that initiated the process? If the process was already running before the disruption, or if it runs identically regardless of whether a disruption occurred, it is not disruption-occasioned.

**Violation signature.** A process that runs continuously without a triggering disruption is monitoring, not sensemaking. A process that is initiated by a schedule or a checklist rather than by a disruption is compliance, not sensemaking. Both may be valuable; neither is sensemaking.

**Satisfaction signature.** The process begins with a named disruption. Participants can articulate what stopped making sense. The disruption is recorded as the initiating event, not reconstructed after the fact.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier A. Empirically documented across all traditions surveyed. Biological precedent in the orienting response (involuntary attentional shift toward novel or unexpected stimuli). Weick: "sensemaking begins with chaos." Klein: recognition-primed decision triggers on pattern mismatch. Dervin: sense-making begins at a "gap." Snowden: movement between Cynefin domains is triggered by disruption. Heidegger: understanding-as-breakdown, the tool that becomes visible when it stops working. Buddhist epistemology: investigation triggered by the arising of conditions that do not fit the current frame.

## 3.2 Particular-to-General Relating

Sensemaking moves between specific cues (this particular event, this particular observation, this particular signal) and general patterns (what kind of situation this is, what category it belongs to, what frame applies). The relating is bidirectional: general frames shape which particulars are noticed, and particulars that do not fit the frame trigger frame revision. Neither direction has priority; both operate simultaneously.

**Operational definition.** A process exhibits particular-to-general relating if it generates or revises a general frame (a category, a pattern, a model, a narrative) from specific cues, and if it uses general frames to select, weight, and interpret specific cues, with the relationship between the two being mutually constitutive rather than one-directional.

**Satisfaction test.** Can the practitioner identify both the specific cues that informed the general frame and the general frame that shaped which cues were attended to? If only the general frame is articulated (the conclusion is reached without traceable cues), the process is assertion, not sensemaking. If only the cues are listed without a general frame emerging, the process is data collection, not sensemaking.

**Violation signature.** A process that begins with a conclusion and searches for confirming evidence is confirmation, not sensemaking. A process that accumulates observations without ever producing a frame is cataloging, not sensemaking.

**Satisfaction signature.** The general frame can be traced to specific cues. The cue selection can be traced to general frames. Participants can articulate which cues challenged the frame and how the frame was revised in response.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier A. Documented in all traditions. Weick: "bracketing and labeling." Klein: "pattern recognition" with frame updating. Dervin: "bridging" from gap to resolution. Snowden: domain classification as a form of particular-to-general assignment. Heidegger: the hermeneutic circle (understanding of parts depends on understanding of whole, and vice versa). Buddhist epistemology: the relationship between specific phenomena and the conditions of their arising.

## 3.3 Action-Entangled

Sensemaking is not a cognitive precursor to action that completes before action begins. Action and sensemaking are entangled: action generates the data sensemaking needs, and sensemaking shapes the action taken. The separation of "first understand, then act" is a useful simplification for some domains but is not how sensemaking structurally works. A process that requires understanding to be complete before action can begin has substituted analysis for sensemaking.

**Operational definition.** A process is action-entangled if actions taken during the process generate information that the process uses, and if the process shapes which actions are taken next. The feedback loop between action and understanding is constitutive of the process, not incidental to it.

**Satisfaction test.** Did actions taken during the process change what was understood? Did what was understood change which actions were taken? If action and understanding proceeded on independent tracks (understanding was completed, then action followed), the process is analysis-then-execution, not sensemaking.

**Violation signature.** A process that prohibits action until understanding is complete (analysis paralysis) has severed the entanglement. A process that takes action without any feedback loop to understanding (unreflective execution) has severed it from the other direction.

**Satisfaction signature.** Participants can identify specific actions that changed what they understood, and specific understandings that changed what they did next. The sequence of actions is not pre-planned; it emerges from the interaction between action and understanding.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier A. Weick: "enactment" as a core property of sensemaking (people create the environments they then make sense of). Klein: "recognition-primed decision" where action and recognition are simultaneous. Buddhist epistemology: "view and conduct are inseparable" (the understanding shapes the action, the action reveals the understanding). Snowden: the probe-sense-respond cycle in complex domains is an explicit instantiation of action-entanglement.

## 3.4 Sufficiency-Oriented

Sensemaking seeks a plausible account that enables a next action whose outcome will generate information relevant to revising the account. It does not seek an optimal or complete account. It satisfices. This is not a weakness or a concession to practical limitations. It is a structural feature: sensemaking that requires completeness before action severs the action-entanglement (Invariant 3.3); sensemaking that acts on a plausible frame and updates based on what the action reveals maintains it.

**Operational definition.** A process is sufficiency-oriented if it meets three conditions: (a) it produces a frame that participants treat as provisional rather than definitive, (b) participants can articulate what evidence would cause them to revise the frame, and (c) the next action taken is designed to generate information relevant to that revision. The completion criterion is action-readiness with a named revision path, not accuracy of the account.

**Satisfaction test.** Three observable markers: (1) the frame is recorded with explicit revision conditions (what would change it), (2) the process terminated before all available information was incorporated, as evidenced by the frame record naming unresolved questions or unexamined evidence that the participants chose not to pursue before acting, and (3) the next action was designed to test the frame (it could generate disconfirming information, as documented in the action's stated purpose or design). If the process continued until all available information was incorporated and no unresolved questions remained, it has crossed from sensemaking into analysis.

**Violation signature.** A process that seeks the correct answer before acting is optimizing, not sensemaking. A process that accepts the first frame without articulating revision conditions is guessing, not sensemaking. Both extremes violate sufficiency-orientation: one by demanding too much, the other by demanding too little.

**Satisfaction signature.** The frame produced is explicitly provisional with named revision conditions. Participants can articulate what would cause them to revise it. The next action taken is designed to generate information that could trigger revision.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier B. Strong convergence across traditions, but the normative claim that sufficiency is structurally appropriate rather than merely pragmatic is extrapolation beyond what any single tradition empirically demonstrates. Weick: "plausibility rather than accuracy." Klein: "satisficing" in naturalistic decision-making. Dervin: "sufficient bridge" across the gap. Buddhist epistemology: the middle way between clinging to certainty and acting without discernment. The extrapolation to a structural requirement (that a sensemaking standard should specify sufficiency rather than accuracy as the completion criterion) is this standard's interpretive contribution, not a direct finding from the traditions.

## 3.5 Temporally Structured

Sensemaking has temporal phases: a disruption phase (intelligibility breaks down, existing frames are inadequate), a liminal phase (the old frame has been suspended but a new frame has not yet formed), and a resolution phase (a new frame is constructed, or an existing frame is revised to accommodate the disruption). The phases are not rigidly sequential; they can overlap and recurse. But the temporal structure is load-bearing: a process that lacks the liminal phase (jumping directly from disruption to resolution) has skipped sensemaking and is applying a pre-existing frame without testing it.

**Operational definition.** A process is temporally structured if it passes through a specifiable disruption phase, a liminal phase in which the prior frame is suspended and a new frame has not yet formed, and a resolution phase in which a new or revised frame emerges. The liminal phase is the diagnostic marker: its presence distinguishes sensemaking from pattern-matching.

**Satisfaction test.** Two verification modes, concurrent and retrospective. Concurrent: participants record the point at which the prior frame was recognized as inadequate and no replacement had yet been identified. Retrospective (residue-based): the resolution frame is compared against all frames that were available before the disruption; if the resolution frame is identical to a pre-existing frame, the liminal phase either did not occur or did not produce anything (the process applied an existing frame rather than constructing a new one). The retrospective test is the primary verification mode for SMS-Accountable and SMS-Auditable tiers, because it does not depend on participant self-report. If the transition from disruption to resolution was instantaneous (the new frame was available immediately and was not modified by the process), the process is recognition, not sensemaking. Recognition is valuable; it is not sensemaking.

**Violation signature.** A process that jumps from disruption to resolution without a liminal phase is applying a pre-existing frame. A process that remains in the liminal phase indefinitely without producing a resolution has stalled. Both violate temporal structure: one by compressing it, the other by failing to complete it.

**Satisfaction signature.** Participants can identify the period of not-knowing: the interval between recognizing that the old frame does not fit and arriving at a new frame. The resolution can be traced to work done during the liminal phase, not to a frame that was available before the disruption.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier B. Converges across traditions with different vocabularies. Weick: the transition from "ongoing" to "interrupted" to "re-storied." Turner's liminality (anthropological source, widely applied). Heidegger: the movement from ready-to-hand through present-at-hand to a new readiness-to-hand. Buddhist epistemology: the arising, abiding, and cessation of mental states in the investigation of phenomena. The three-phase structure as formulated here (disruption, liminal, resolution) is a synthesis across these traditions, not a direct quotation from any one of them.

# 4. Three Scales of Operation

The five structural invariants apply at all three scales described below. A coordination system's sensemaking capacity is the composite of its capacity at each scale, and the three scales have a dependency structure that constrains system design.

## 4.1 Intra-Personal

Sensemaking within a single agent, coordinating among its own parts, perspectives, and conflicting interpretations. This is the foundational scale. Before an agent can participate in inter-personal sensemaking, it must be able to distinguish its own experience from its automatic interpretation of that experience. An agent that cannot do this contributes its interpretations to the inter-personal process as though they were observations, contaminating the shared language production process at the source.

The five invariants at this scale: disruption is experienced as a personal break in intelligibility. Particular-to-general relating operates between specific felt experiences and the frames the agent uses to interpret them. Action-entanglement means the agent's internal actions (attending, questioning, reframing) generate new internal data. Sufficiency-orientation means the agent seeks an account adequate for the next interpersonal contribution, not a complete self-understanding. Temporal structure means the agent passes through a period of not-knowing before arriving at a revised frame.

Intra-personal sensemaking capacity is not a fixed trait. It varies with fatigue, overload, psychological safety, and the availability of reflective practices. A coordination system that chronically depletes these conditions depletes its own sensemaking substrate.

## 4.2 Inter-Personal

Sensemaking between agents, producing shared language. This is the scale at which the obligation argument's first leg operates. The product is mutual legibility: each party understands what the other is actually experiencing, rather than what their own framework predicts the other should be experiencing.

The five invariants at this scale: disruption is a breakdown in mutual intelligibility (one party says something the other cannot assimilate into their current frame). Particular-to-general relating operates between specific statements, behaviors, or events and the emerging shared frame. Action-entanglement means the conversation itself is the action that generates the data; what each party says changes what both parties understand. Sufficiency-orientation means the shared frame needs to be good enough to coordinate the next joint action, not complete. Temporal structure means both parties pass through a shared period of not-knowing before arriving at a shared frame.

Inter-personal sensemaking depends on intra-personal capacity. If one party cannot distinguish their experience from their interpretation, they contribute their interpretation as though it were the shared frame, and the inter-personal process produces compliance or conflict rather than shared language.

## 4.3 Witness-Reception

Encountering others' crystallized sensemaking (as defined in Section 2.3: sensemaking output encoded in a persistent medium) across time. A document, a standard, a governance record, a protocol specification: each is crystallized sensemaking that a future participant receives without having participated in the process that produced it.

The five invariants at this scale: disruption is the encounter with an artifact that does not fit the receiver's current frame (a standard that challenges assumptions, a decision record that reveals reasoning the receiver had not considered). Particular-to-general relating operates between the specific content of the artifact and the receiver's developing model of what kind of thing this is and what it means. Action-entanglement means the receiver's engagement with the artifact (reading, questioning, testing against their own experience) generates new understanding. Sufficiency-orientation means the receiver integrates the artifact's content to the degree necessary for their current work, not exhaustively. Temporal structure means the receiver passes through a period of not-understanding the artifact before arriving at an integration.

The dependency runs in both directions. Witness-reception depends on inter-personal sensemaking having produced artifacts worth receiving. But witness-reception also shapes inter-personal sensemaking: the artifacts a participant has previously encountered condition which disruptions they recognize, which frames they bring, and which particular cues they attend to.

# 5. Three Action Invariants

The five structural invariants specify what sensemaking must be. The three action invariants specify what sensemaking works with and produces. They provide the operational vocabulary for the standard's adoption architecture and cross-mechanism integration.

These three properties of action were identified through cross-cultural pressure testing of a ten-dimension action coordinate system derived from Sanskrit philosophical traditions. The pressure tests, conducted against Chinese, African, and Indigenous philosophical traditions, found that seven of the ten dimensions carry source-tradition shaping that limits their cross-cultural applicability. Three dimensions held across all tests without distortion. These three are the strongest candidates for cross-traditional invariants of action.

## 5.1 Residue

Actions leave traces that condition future states. Every action in a coordination system produces residue in the system's medium: a record, a changed relationship, a shifted expectation, a new or revised shared understanding. The residue is not an optional byproduct; it is constitutive of coordination. A coordination system is its accumulated residue.

For sensemaking: the shared language sensemaking produces is itself residue. A revised frame, a new distinction, a named failure mode: each is a trace left in the coordination medium that conditions future sensemaking. Sensemaking that produces no durable residue (shared language that is not recorded, frames that are not articulated, distinctions that are not named) has coordinated the participants in the moment but contributed nothing to the system's accumulated coordination capacity.

**Observable marker.** A sensemaking process has produced residue if a new artifact appears in the governance record (a named frame, a documented distinction, a recorded disagreement, a revised shared understanding) that did not exist before the process began. Residue is assessed by comparing the state of the governance record before and after the process. Absence of new artifacts is absence of residue.

## 5.2 Direction

Actions extend or withdraw engagement. Both directions are active; both serve coordination functions. Extension deepens investment in a shared frame, a relationship, or a coordination practice. Withdrawal releases investment, creating space for reconfiguration. A coordination system with no withdrawal pathway can only grow; it cannot release, and it cannot reconfigure.

For sensemaking: sensemaking has direction. It can extend engagement (deepening a shared frame, producing more precise shared language) or withdraw it (recognizing that a shared frame has become an obstacle, that the shared language is no longer adequate, that the coordination practice needs to be dissolved and rebuilt). Recognizing when to stop extending and begin withdrawing is itself a sensemaking judgment.

**Observable marker.** Direction is assessed by examining the sequence of sensemaking outputs: are successive artifacts deepening and refining the same frame (extension), or are they naming the inadequacy of the current frame and creating space for a different one (withdrawal)? A system that only extends accumulates shared language without testing it. A system that only withdraws cannot build on prior work. Both patterns are observable in the governance record over time.

## 5.3 Phase

Actions participate in generative, sustaining, and dissolving cycles. No action is outside the cycle; the question is which phase of the cycle it serves. Generative action produces new structures, new shared language, new coordination capacity. Sustaining action maintains existing structures, keeps shared language current, preserves coordination capacity against entropy. Dissolving action releases structures that are no longer serving their function, making space for new generative action.

For sensemaking: generative sensemaking creates new shared language where none existed. Sustaining sensemaking maintains and updates existing shared language against changing conditions. Dissolving sensemaking recognizes when existing shared language has become an obstacle to coordination and releases it. All three phases are structurally necessary. A coordination system that only generates accumulates shared language without testing it. A system that only sustains cannot adapt. A system that only dissolves cannot accumulate.

**Observable marker.** Phase is assessed by examining the relationship between a sensemaking process and the shared language that preceded it. Generative: the process produced shared language in a domain where none previously existed in the governance record. Sustaining: the process revised or confirmed existing shared language in response to new conditions. Dissolving: the process formally retired or deprecated existing shared language, recording why it no longer serves its coordination function. A system's phase distribution (proportion of generative, sustaining, and dissolving sensemaking over a review period) is a structural health indicator assessable from the governance record.

# 6. Sensemaking Capacity: The Four Batteries Model

The five structural invariants (Section 3) specify what sensemaking must structurally be. This section specifies the capacity conditions required for sensemaking to occur. Process requirements without capacity conditions produce the applying-without-understanding failure mode: organizations that implement sensemaking procedures without the substrate those procedures depend on. ISO management system standards address this by specifying support conditions (resources, competence, awareness) alongside operational requirements in the same document. This standard follows that pattern.

The disruption-occasioned invariant (3.1) is the pressure-response axiom instantiated at the sensemaking scale. The framework's foundational claim is that systems organize in response to pressure. Sensemaking is the specific form of pressure-response that produces shared language as its output. The capacity conditions for this response are described by the Four Batteries model of generative capacity, which identifies four resource conditions that determine whether coordination capacity is available.

## 6.1 The Four Batteries (Partially Integrated)

The Four Batteries Generative Capacity Standard (v0.2.8) specifies four resource conditions for generative capacity: personal battery (physical, cognitive, and emotional capacity available to the individual), relational battery (trust, psychological safety, and reciprocity in working relationships), contribution battery (meaning and agency in formally recognized work), and mission battery (belief in the organization's purpose and one's place within it). Each battery operates across two independent dimensions: charge (cyclical, maintainable) and developmental state (permanent, transformed only through integration events).

The Four Batteries model maps to the three sensemaking scales as follows. Personal battery maps to intra-personal sensemaking capacity (Section 4.1): when depleted, the agent cannot distinguish experience from interpretation. Relational battery maps to inter-personal sensemaking capacity (Section 4.2): when depleted, participants will not surface actual observations, and shared language production fails at the source. Mission battery at developmental depth maps to witness-reception capacity (Section 4.3): a frame expansive enough to hold multiple dimensions simultaneously enables a participant to encounter crystallized sensemaking from a different context and integrate it rather than applying it as a fixed template. Contribution battery maps to the boundary between visible and invisible sensemaking: when depleted, coordination work routes through informal workarounds outside the governance record, and sensemaking produces no durable residue (Action Invariant 5.1).

## 6.2 Battery-to-Standard Connections

The Four Batteries Generative Capacity Standard (Section 8.1) specifies six structurally coupled connections between individual batteries and individual Commitment Standards. These connections are the operational interface between the capacity conditions this section describes and the detection surfaces the Commitment Standards provide. The coupling is structural: each Commitment Standard's detection requirements presuppose capacity that specific batteries provide, and each battery's depletion creates specific blind spots in specific Commitment Standards' detection surfaces.

The six connections, summarized from the Four Batteries Generative Capacity Standard's authoritative specification:

**Connection 1 (Personal battery, Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard).** Bidirectional. Depletion eliminates the cognitive capacity that adverse signal engagement requires. Depletion is itself an adverse signal that gets suppressed by the depletion, producing the battery-signal loop: the signal about depletion is blocked by the condition the signal would report. This loop is one of the five configuration-level emergent properties identified in the standards-as-tensegrity analysis.

**Connection 2 (Relational battery, Structural Consent Legibility Standard).** Depletion degrades all three consent features (negotiated limits, bidirectional awareness, revocability) simultaneously, producing consent that passes the Structural Consent Legibility Standard's structural tests while being substantively hollow. The resulting failure mode is a consent illegibility that the standard's own detection surface cannot distinguish from genuine consent.

**Connection 3 (Contribution battery, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard).** Depletion drives coordination into the Hidden Factory, producing Class 5 (Omission Asymmetry). Neither document alone can distinguish depletion-driven omission from strategic omission; battery state data is required for disambiguation.

**Connection 4 (Mission battery, Structural Power Distribution Standard).** Developmental altitude mismatches produce structural power concentration invisible to the Structural Power Distribution Standard's three dimensions, because the invisibility operates along a developmental altitude axis those dimensions do not include.

**Connection 5 (Personal battery, Precision-First Design Standard).** Capacity prerequisite. Specification work is among the first capacities lost during Personal depletion, making design-time adoption battery-state-dependent.

**Connection 6 (Battery state differential, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard).** Battery state differentials across participants are the operational measurement surface for the sensemaking capacity asymmetry identified in Section 7.3 of this standard as invisible to the six-class taxonomy. This connection closes that gap: battery assessment provides the data that makes sensemaking capacity asymmetry visible without requiring extension of the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard's taxonomy, because capacity conditions belong in this standard rather than in a detection-surface standard.

These connections have structural implications for verifying sensemaking capacity. Verification of whether sensemaking capacity is present cannot rely solely on observing the sensemaking process as it occurs (concurrent observation), because the capacity conditions that enable sensemaking may be absent in ways that produce structurally correct process outputs. A depleted Relational battery produces process outputs that look like shared language production (participants go through the motions) but are not (the participants are performing expected boundaries rather than surfacing actual ones). The residue-based verification pattern (Section 14, v1.0.2 changelog) addresses this: verify through what the process produces rather than through concurrent observation of the process itself. The connections specified above identify the specific capacity conditions whose absence produces the gap between process appearance and process substance.

## 6.3 Current Integration Status

The Four Batteries model is at genesis-cohort testing level. Its battery definitions, depletion archetypes, generative archetypes, and Commitment Standard connections are specified. Its operational measurement instruments for the three sensemaking scales are not. Integration into this standard requires: (a) operational measurement specifications for each battery at each sensemaking scale, (b) epistemic tier labeling for each measurement claim, and (c) connection to the adoption architecture (Section 9) such that SMS-Instrumented and above require battery assessment alongside invariant assessment. Until integration is complete, the adoption architecture's capacity assessment requirements (Section 9.3: "sensemaking capacity at each of the three scales is assessed periodically") are structurally incomplete. Adopting organizations at SMS-Instrumented and above should use the Four Batteries model as their capacity assessment framework and document the measurement approach used, with the understanding that measurement specifications will be normatively tightened when this section is fully developed.

# 7. Ontological Commitments and Structural Assumptions

This standard does not claim framework-neutrality. Research in ontological politics and cross-cultural pressure testing confirmed that the standard's analytical architecture carries assumptions from its source traditions. These assumptions are named here as limits of applicability, not as defects to be corrected at current scope.

## 7.1 Three Structural Assumptions

**Agent-priority.** This standard assumes a pre-existing agent who then acts. The five invariants are written from the perspective of an agent encountering a disruption and producing a response. Several philosophical traditions challenge this premise. Haudenosaunee governance understands the agent as constituted through action and its relational context. Maori whakapapa understands identity as constituted through genealogical relationship. Daoist wu wei posits situation-originated action where the agent's role is to perceive and follow an existing pattern rather than originate a response. The standard cannot accommodate these traditions without fundamental redesign of its invariant architecture. This is a known scope limitation.

**Human-scope.** The standard's operational definitions assume human-like agents: entities that experience disruption, attend to cues, form frames, take actions, and articulate their reasoning. Traditions that require non-human agency (Andean Pachamama, Maori kaitiakitanga where a river is an ancestor and legal person, Dagara cosmological relationship with ancestral forces) fall outside the standard's scope. The standard does not claim these traditions are wrong; it claims its own instruments cannot reach them.

**Separability.** The standard assumes its structural invariants can be examined at least somewhat independently: that disruption-occasioned and action-entangled are distinct properties that can be assessed separately. Aboriginal Australian Songlines represent the sharpest counter-case, where place, procedure, quality, and residue are globally coupled such that the "dimensions" are projections of an irreducible whole rather than separable components. Indigenous traditions more broadly understand decomposition as one mode of analysis (appropriate where separability holds) alongside a relational or holistic mode (appropriate where it does not). The standard operates in the decomposition mode and names that as a choice, not a universal.

## 7.2 The Relational Ground Finding

Chinese, African, and Indigenous pressure tests converged independently on a finding that constitutes the standard's most significant known limitation: relational structure is constitutive of the action-space, not context for it. The standard's invariant architecture treats the agent and the relational field as separable (the agent acts within relationships). Ubuntu, Bantu relational ontology, Confucian wu lun, and Haudenosaunee governance treat them as co-constitutive (the agent is its relationships; action that changes the relational field changes the agent). The standard's agent-centric architecture is not a universal feature of sensemaking; it is a tradition-specific analytical choice. This finding does not invalidate the standard, but it constrains the strength of its universality claims. The five invariants describe sensemaking as it appears from within the agent-centric analytical frame. They may not describe sensemaking as it appears from within a relational frame.

## 7.3 Cross-Standard Scope Boundaries

Three tensions arise when this standard is jointly adopted with the Commitment Standards. These are not defects in any individual standard; they are boundary conditions that require explicit attention in joint adoption.

**Liminal phase and signal escalation.** The Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle (ASEP) requires time-gated signal escalation: signals that are not acknowledged within a defined window automatically escalate. This standard's temporal structure invariant (3.5) specifies that sensemaking includes a liminal phase of unspecified duration. A system could use "still in the liminal phase" to defer signal classification and thereby defer escalation indefinitely. Joint adoption must specify that the liminal phase does not suspend ASEP escalation windows: a signal remains subject to ASEP's timing requirements regardless of whether the sensemaking process that would inform its classification has completed. Sensemaking that has not produced a frame by the time an ASEP window expires has not failed; the signal escalates while sensemaking continues.

**Interpretive authority concentration.** At SMS-Accountable, the governance review loop requires a body to determine whether the five structural invariants were satisfied. This determination concentrates interpretive authority: whoever classifies sensemaking success and failure controls what counts as coordination substance versus compliance theater. The Structural Power Distribution Standard does not currently require this concentration to be monitored. Joint adoption at SMS-Accountable should treat sensemaking failure classification authority as a coordination-dimension power concentration subject to the Structural Power Distribution Standard's Authorize invariant.

**Sensemaking capacity asymmetry.** Participants with higher sensemaking capacity can recognize and exploit sensemaking process failures that other participants cannot detect. This asymmetry is structurally invisible to the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard's six classes, because it operates in the substrate (participants' ability to distinguish experience from interpretation) rather than in the content-level information flows the standard monitors. The Four Batteries Generative Capacity Standard (Section 8.1, Connection 6) provides the operational measurement surface for this asymmetry: battery state differentials across participants are the observable that makes sensemaking capacity asymmetry visible without requiring extension of the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard's taxonomy, because capacity conditions belong in this standard rather than in a detection-surface standard. Joint adoption should include battery state assessment as part of the sensemaking capacity assessment specified in the adoption architecture (Section 9).

## 7.4 Implications for Adopters

An organization adopting this standard at any tier should be aware that the standard's instruments are more precise for coordination contexts where agent-priority, human-scope, and separability hold than for contexts where they do not. Contexts involving strong relational constitution (community-based governance, indigenous governance frameworks, care work), non-human agency claims (environmental governance, inter-species coordination), or globally coupled action-spaces (ritual, ceremonial, or cosmological governance) may find that the standard's invariants capture something real but miss something the context considers more important. The standard does not prohibit adoption in these contexts; it requires that the limitation be named rather than ignored.

# 8. Cross-Mechanism Integration Map

The sensemaking outputs this standard specifies are consumed by several categories of protocol mechanism. This section maps each mechanism category to the sensemaking outputs it depends on and the structural invariant that grounds the dependency. The integration map is adapted from the predecessor document's (v0.6) cross-mechanism map, expanded to reflect this standard's broader scope.

The predecessor's integration map was organized around six structural requirements derived from Cynefin (domain classification, action mode mapping, maturation trajectories, boundary conditions, coherence validation, action mode justification). Those six requirements survive as the *classification surface*: one component of the broader sensemaking interface. Protocol mechanisms that consume domain classifications continue to depend on classification-surface outputs. The broader sensemaking interface adds outputs that the classification surface does not cover: shared language production, sensemaking capacity assessment, and the three action invariants (residue, direction, phase).

## 8.1 Event Schema Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Event schema mechanisms carry domain classification fields and validate action mode coherence. They depend on the classification surface's domain classification and action mode mapping outputs.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** Event schema mechanisms also carry sensemaking metadata: the disruption that initiated the sensemaking process (Invariant 3.1), the shared language produced (Residue, 5.1), and the direction of engagement (Direction, 5.2). This metadata makes the sensemaking process that produced the event legible to future participants at the witness-reception scale (4.3).

## 8.2 Detection Layer Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Detection layer mechanisms depend on coherence validation for action mode coherence detection and on maturation trajectories for classification sequence plausibility assessment.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** Detection layer mechanisms assess whether sensemaking is structurally occurring, not merely whether classification labels are consistent. A system that produces coherent classification labels without passing through the liminal phase (Invariant 3.5, temporal structure) is performing pattern-matching, not sensemaking. Detection of the applying-without-understanding failure mode (Section 2.3) depends on the temporal structure invariant.

## 8.3 Developmental Assessment Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Developmental assessment mechanisms depend on maturation trajectories for classification sequences as developmental observables, action mode mapping for tempo and orientation coherence, and boundary conditions for deviation pattern detection.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** Developmental assessment at the sensemaking level tracks whether the organization's sensemaking capacity is growing, stable, or depleting across the three scales (4.1, 4.2, 4.3). Depletion of intra-personal sensemaking capacity (4.1) is a leading indicator of coordination degradation that classification-surface outputs will not detect until the degradation reaches the inter-personal scale.

## 8.4 Adverse Signal Processing Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Adverse signal processing depends on action mode mapping for domain-calibrated process shapes. The timing character of the action mode structure determines signal escalation window durations.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** A signal that disrupts an existing frame (Invariant 3.1) requires sensemaking before it can be classified. Adverse signal processing mechanisms that classify signals before sensemaking has occurred are performing premature classification: assigning the signal to a domain before the disruption it represents has been processed through the liminal phase. The standard's temporal structure invariant (3.5) specifies the minimum structural requirement for signal processing that includes sensemaking.

## 8.5 Consent Process Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Consent process shape and duration are calibrated to the domain of the action being consented to.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** Consent that is structurally legible (per the Structural Consent Legibility Standard) but produced without shared language between the parties is formal consent without coordination substance. The inter-personal sensemaking scale (4.2) specifies what shared language production requires; consent processes that depend on genuine agreement (as distinguished from compliance) depend on this output.

## 8.6 Governance Change Proposal Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Deliberation mode calibration depends on the domain of the change being proposed.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** A governance change proposal represents crystallized sensemaking (an artifact at the witness-reception scale, 4.3) that other participants must integrate. The quality of the proposal as a sensemaking artifact, whether it makes its reasoning legible, names its disruption, and articulates its frame, determines whether the governance process that evaluates it can perform sensemaking or is limited to pattern-matching against existing frames.

## 8.7 Information Asymmetry Classification Mechanisms

**Classification-surface dependency.** Asymmetry classes map to characteristic domains; detection approaches differ by domain context.

**Sensemaking-surface dependency.** Interpretive asymmetry (the condition where parties possess the same information but produce different interpretations) is a sensemaking phenomenon. Its detection and resolution depend on the inter-personal sensemaking scale (4.2). A system that classifies interpretive asymmetry without engaging the sensemaking process that would resolve it has detected the asymmetry but not addressed it.

# 9. Adoption Architecture

Five tiers enable progressive adoption. Each tier builds on the previous. The tier names use the prefix SMS (Sensemaking Standard). These tiers are accountability tiers: they specify what governance infrastructure the organization has built around its sensemaking processes. They are structurally distinct from the Precision-First Design Standard's three adoption depths (auditing, remediation, design-time), which specify the quality of specification work. An organization adopting both standards reports its SMS tier and its Precision-First depth independently; the two measure different properties.

## 9.1 SMS-Assessed

The organization acknowledges that sensemaking capacity is structurally relevant to its coordination operations. An inventory of where sensemaking currently occurs and where its absence is felt has been conducted.

**Minimum requirement.** A sensemaking inventory that maps the organization's coordination practices to the three scales of operation (intra-personal, inter-personal, witness-reception), identifies which practices involve explicit sensemaking and which assume that shared language already exists, and names the absence of explicit sensemaking capacity as a structural risk where applicable.

## 9.2 SMS-Operational

The organization has identified its sensemaking practices and can describe which of the five structural invariants they satisfy. Sensemaking outputs (shared language, revised frames, new distinctions) are documented and accessible to participants.

**Minimum requirement.** For each coordination practice in the sensemaking inventory, a documented assessment of which structural invariants the practice satisfies and which it does not, with the evidence basis for each assessment. Sensemaking outputs are recorded in the governance record or equivalent. The organization can distinguish, in practice, between sensemaking and the applying-without-understanding failure mode.

## 9.3 SMS-Instrumented

The five structural invariants are used as real-time quality criteria for sensemaking processes. The organization can observe its own sensemaking state without waiting for a dedicated review cycle. The three scales of operation are distinguished in practice and monitored separately.

**Minimum requirement.** Each sensemaking process produces a record that includes: the initiating disruption (Invariant 3.1), the cues and frames that interacted (Invariant 3.2), the actions taken and the information they generated (Invariant 3.3), the sufficiency criterion applied (Invariant 3.4), and the temporal phases traversed including the liminal phase (Invariant 3.5). Sensemaking capacity at each of the three scales is assessed periodically, with intra-personal capacity assessed as a leading indicator.

## 9.4 SMS-Accountable

Includes all SMS-Instrumented requirements plus closed governance loop architecture.

The defining property of SMS-Accountable is that sensemaking failures (processes that violate one or more structural invariants, or coordination decisions made without passing through the sensemaking process) produce mandatory governance responses, and the absence of a response is itself structurally visible without depending on any actor's cooperation.

**Minimum requirements.** Every detected sensemaking failure must produce a documented governance review within a defined window. The review record must include: the sensemaking process or decision identifier, the invariant(s) violated or the sensemaking process bypassed, the review outcome, the actor or body responsible, and the rationale. The absence of a documented review within the defined window must produce automatic disclosure to all governance actors without requiring any actor's initiation.

The governance review record is append-only. Reviews cannot be modified after recording; corrections are recorded as new entries referencing the original.

Tier review cadence: minimum annually, including a sensemaking loop integrity check (confirmation that the detection-to-review path operated as specified for all sensemaking failures in the review period) and a sensemaking capacity assessment across all three scales.

## 9.5 SMS-Auditable

Includes all SMS-Accountable requirements plus independent auditability.

The defining property of SMS-Auditable is that the full sensemaking architecture can be verified by a party outside the organization without depending on operator cooperation or self-certification.

**Minimum requirements.** The complete sensemaking record (from initiating disruption through governance review or automatic disclosure) must be accessible to an independent auditor without operator cooperation during a defined audit period. The auditor must be able to verify completeness (all sensemaking processes in the audit period are present in the record), verify invariant assessment integrity (each recorded assessment was made against the five structural invariants as specified in Section 3), and verify response completeness (every sensemaking failure produced either a documented governance review or an automatic disclosure).

The audit itself must be documented: scope, period, auditor identity or body, findings, and organization response to findings. Audit reports are retained under the same append-only constraints as governance reviews. SMS-Auditable organizations must make audit reports available to their participant population within a defined period after audit completion.

## 9.6 Governance Layer Assignments

**Constitutive elements.** Elements that cannot be changed by any governance change proposal. These define what the sensemaking standard structurally is. Constitutive elements include: the requirement that sensemaking capacity be a named structural dependency; the five structural invariants (Section 3); the three scales of operation (Section 4); the three action invariants (Section 5); the three named structural assumptions (Section 7.1); the closed governance loop at SMS-Accountable (sensemaking failures produce mandatory governance responses, absence of response is structurally visible); and the independent auditability requirement at SMS-Auditable.

**Governance-mutable elements.** Elements that can be changed through the governance change proposal process with ratification per the implementing protocol's governance requirements. These include: the specific sensemaking practices and frameworks adopted to satisfy the invariants; the specific instruments used to assess sensemaking capacity at each scale; the window durations for governance review of sensemaking failures; the specific evidence requirements for invariant satisfaction assessments; and the tier review cadence beyond the annual minimum.

**Tenant-mutable elements.** Elements that individual adopting organizations can adjust within bounds set by the governance-mutable layer. These include: the organization's preferred sensemaking practices (provided they satisfy the five invariants); the observation periods for sensemaking capacity assessment; and the internal documentation format for sensemaking records, provided the format preserves the structural properties (initiating disruption, cue-frame interaction, action-information loop, sufficiency criterion, temporal phases).

# 10. Relationship to the Commitment Standards

The five Commitment Standards are compressive. Each names a class of structural failure and specifies the floor that prevents it. The Precision-First Design Standard closes the precision deficit. The Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle closes the signal suppression gap. The Structural Consent Legibility Standard closes the consent opacity gap. The Information Asymmetry Classification Standard closes the asymmetry invisibility gap. The Structural Power Distribution Standard closes the power distribution opacity gap.

This standard is tensile. It does not close a gap; it specifies the enabling condition that makes gap-closing meaningful. An organization can satisfy all five Commitment Standards at their highest adoption tier and still apply them as ritual: following the forms without understanding the structural conditions the forms are designed to respond to. The Commitment Standards specify what must not happen. This standard specifies what must be structurally present for the Commitment Standards to function as coordination instruments rather than compliance checklists.

The relationship is not hierarchical; it is tensegrity. The compressive standards provide the structural floor without which coordination degrades into exploitation. The cohesion standards provide the sensemaking capacity without which coordination degrades into compliance. Both types of degradation produce organizations that appear to be coordinating while failing to coordinate. Neither standard type alone is sufficient; each requires the other.

Commitment Standard conformance without sensemaking capacity is achievable but produces the applying-without-understanding failure mode: a system that produces the correct outputs for the wrong reasons, whose conformance is brittle because it depends on compliance rather than capacity. Sensemaking capacity without Commitment Standard conformance is also achievable but produces the understanding-without-structure failure mode: a system that perceives coordination dynamics with clarity but lacks the structural floors that prevent exploitation. Both failure modes are real. This standard addresses the first; the Commitment Standards address the second. Neither is optional.

The structural interface between the two sub-layers is specified in the Four Batteries Generative Capacity Standard (Section 8.1), which names six connections between individual batteries and individual Commitment Standards. These connections make the tensegrity relationship operationally concrete: each battery's depletion creates specific blind spots in specific Commitment Standards' detection surfaces, and each battery at depth enhances specific detection surfaces. Section 6.2 of this standard summarizes the six connections and their implications for sensemaking capacity verification.

# 12. Inheritance Clause

Systems adopting this standard should include the following statement in their governance documentation:

*Sensemaking Standard Inheritance: This [document/system/protocol] adopts the Sensemaking Standard (SMS) (v[X], [date]) at the [SMS-Assessed / SMS-Operational / SMS-Instrumented / SMS-Accountable / SMS-Auditable] tier. Sensemaking practices adopted to satisfy the five structural invariants: [list practices, with invariant coverage mapping]. Sensemaking capacity assessment at each scale: intra-personal [method and current assessment], inter-personal [method and current assessment], witness-reception [method and current assessment]. Coordination practices covered by the sensemaking inventory: [list or reference to inventory document]. Practices identified as not requiring sensemaking assessment: [list, with rationale]. Named structural assumptions (agent-priority, human-scope, separability) acknowledged: [yes/no, with rationale if no].*

The inheritance clause commits the adopting system to: (a) naming which sensemaking practices it uses and which invariants they satisfy, (b) assessing sensemaking capacity at all three scales, (c) treating sensemaking failures as governance events requiring documented review at SMS-Accountable and above, (d) specifying which coordination practices are covered and which are not, with rationale, so that adoption scope is explicit and auditable, and (e) acknowledging the standard's named structural assumptions.

Adoption of this standard at any tier does not constitute or substitute for Commitment Standard conformance. The relationship between this standard and the Commitment Standards is specified in Section 10.

# 1. Abstract

Conflict is a structural feature of coordination, not a failure of it. Any system designed to surface genuine coordination activity will surface disagreement. The question is not whether conflict occurs but whether the system that generates it can engage with it developmentally. A coordination system that routes all conflict to adversarial adjudication has not addressed conflict; it has replaced transformation with litigation. These are structurally different outcomes. Litigation terminates a conflict by producing a winner and a loser. Transformation converts the friction between parties into developmental signal, changes both parties, and strengthens the coordination medium rather than depleting it.

This standard specifies the structural conditions under which a coordination system develops and sustains conflict transformation capacity. Conflict transformation capacity is the organizational ability to engage conflict as a coordination resource rather than a threat requiring containment or termination. It specifies five structural invariants. It does not prescribe process architecture for specific conflict transformation engagements; that is operational work at the adopting organization and protocol levels.

GravityDAO operated for seven years specifically to provide conflict transformation capacity to decentralized organizations. It was recognized across the field as perhaps the most necessary function in coordination infrastructure. It failed entirely: not because of incompetence or bad intentions, but because no existing coordination infrastructure provided a mechanism to recognize conflict transformation as coordination work, record it, or sustain its practitioners economically. The structural lesson is precise: if a capacity is universally acknowledged as necessary and cannot sustain itself under any existing governance model, the problem is not with the people providing it. The problem is that the infrastructure has no mechanism to make that provision structurally rational. This standard addresses that structural gap.

GravityDAO's own architectural naming for what conflict transformation consists of is: "Surface the Hidden" (holding space, listening, including, sensemaking) and "Conflict as Growth Engine" (ownership, transformation, learning). These are not rhetorical frames. They are GravityDAO's two structural components of conflict transformation, distinguishing the detection and reception function from the developmental conversion function. A coordination system that provides neither is not missing a helpful add-on; it is missing the architectural substrate from which sustained coordination emerges.

GravityDAO's visual architecture makes a further structural claim: Trust Creation work generates intrinsic motivation in participants; Conflict Transformation work does not. It sits on the extrinsic motivation side of the architecture by design. The work of holding space for conflict, surfacing hidden dynamics, and facilitating transformation absorbs costs without generating the internal reward cycle that trust-building interactions produce. This means that informal recognition of conflict transformation work, appreciation, working group acknowledgment, and reputation, is an intrinsic-motivation-type reward. It is structurally insufficient for work that does not generate intrinsic motivation. The work that sits on the extrinsic side requires extrinsic provision: structural resourcing, ledger recognition, economic weight. This is the precise mechanism behind the GravityDAO failure. The organization received universal recognition (acknowledgment, an intrinsic-reward-type signal) but no structural economic provision (the extrinsic resourcing the work requires). Recognition without structural provision is appreciation. It is not sustainability.

This is the third Tensegrity Generative Standard. The five Tensegrity Compressive Standards (Precision-First Design, Adverse-Signal Engagement, Structural Consent Legibility, Information Asymmetry Classification, Structural Power Distribution) are compressive: they close exploitation vectors and specify structural floors. Tensegrity Generative Standards are tensile: they specify enabling conditions for coordination health. The Tensegrity Compressive Standards name what must not happen. The Tensegrity Generative Standards name what must be structurally present for coordination to remain healthy under pressure. Both are required. Neither substitutes for the other.

**Typological declaration** (Descriptive Typology Map v0.1.1)

*Operative classes*
- Action-structural: This standard operates primarily in this class. All five structural invariants specify what the coordination system must structurally do to enable conflict engagement: make conflict legible, provide a graduated architecture, protect proactive disposition, provision transformation capacity, and recognize engagement as coordination work. The adoption architecture (Section 8) specifies what observable structural states these invariants produce.
- Relational-topological: Conflict transformation is fundamentally about the structural properties of relationships under pressure: whether the relationship can hold polarity, whether it can survive abrasion, whether it restructures rather than collapses. The inter-organizational scale (Section 5) and the transformation capacity provision invariant operate in this class.

*Boundary classes*
- Felt-experience: The proactive disposition enablement invariant and the transformation capacity provision invariant acknowledge that operator absorption of process costs and organizational disposition toward conflict have felt-experience dimensions. Measurement instruments for these dimensions are at the Configuration Architecture and adopting-organization level, not in this standard.
- Temporal-dynamic (frequency-dynamic sub-aspect): The graduated engagement architecture has temporal properties (escalation timelines, threshold crossing conditions), but calibration of those timelines to specific organizational contexts is practice-level work outside this standard's scope.

# 2. Scope and Document Type

This document is a Tensegrity Generative Standard within the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite of the Proof of Coordination (PoC) corpus. It specifies enabling conditions, not compliance floors.

**What this standard specifies.** Five structural invariants: the conditions that must be present in a coordination system for conflict transformation capacity to develop and be sustained. An adoption architecture with five tiers. Standards inheritance obligations.

**What this standard does not specify.** Process design for specific conflict transformation engagements (that is the Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification, a downstream protocol-level document). Adjudication mechanisms and their technical requirements (that is protocol-level architecture). Graduated response timeline calibration (that is practice-level configuration work). Individual facilitation skills or training curricula. Specific tool or platform implementations.

**Relationship to the Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification.** The Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification (v0.1.4) is a downstream operational specification. It translates the structural conditions specified here into preconditions assessable before any specific conflict transformation process is deployed. That document addresses container design, scale proportion, process mode selection, traversal modes, threshold management, and the continuous consent loop. This standard is upstream: it specifies what must be structurally present in the coordination system. The preconditions specification addresses what must be operationally present in a specific process deployment.

**Relationship to Ostrom's design principles.** This standard addresses Ostrom's design principles 4 (monitoring, with accountability to participants), 5 (graduated sanctions), and 6 (conflict resolution mechanisms providing rapid, low-cost local arenas) at the standards level. These three principles clustered because they are architecturally nested: monitoring detects conditions that graduated response engages, which escalates to conflict resolution when earlier engagement does not resolve. A system with only adjudication at the top of this spine is not missing features; it is missing the spine itself.

# 3. The Obligation Argument

The claim this standard makes is not that conflict transformation is preferable to adjudication as a matter of organizational culture. It is that conflict transformation capacity is a structural dependency of sustained coordination, and that any coordination system that generates conflict, as all coordination systems of sufficient complexity do, depends on conflict transformation capacity whether or not it names that dependency.

The argument has three legs.

## 3.1 Coordination systems generate conflict by design

A coordination system designed to surface genuine coordination activity will surface disagreement. Honest-failure-reporting incentives make surfacing disagreement economically rational. Counterparty confirmation architectures create structured opportunities for parties to contest each other's accounts. Adversarial signal detection requires treating omissions and contradictions as signals rather than noise. These design features are not incidental. They are the mechanisms through which a coordination system remains legible to itself and to observers. The consequence of these design features is that conflict events are not anomalies to be minimized; they are expected outputs of a functioning system.

The disconfirmation condition: this leg is disconfirmed by finding coordination systems that generate genuine coordination activity while producing no conflict events. The absence of conflict events in an active coordination record is itself a detection target under the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard's implausible-absence specification. A coordination system generating zero conflict events while conducting significant coordination activity is either suppressing conflict or misclassifying it. Neither is a disconfirmation of the structural dependency; both are forms of the dependency being unaddressed.

## 3.2 Conflict transformation capacity does not emerge from governance design

If conflict transformation capacity emerged naturally from good governance design, then governance systems designed with care, adequate resources, and genuine commitment would sustain conflict transformation practitioners. GravityDAO operated for seven years with exactly this profile: designed carefully, sustained by practitioners with genuine commitment, recognized across the field as providing perhaps the most necessary function in coordination infrastructure. It failed entirely. Not through one or two budget cycles. Through sustained structural inability to make its own necessary function economically viable under any existing governance model. Its practitioners depleted. The organization dissolved. The capacity it provided disappeared.

The structural reading of this outcome is not that GravityDAO failed due to individual failure modes. It is that the existing coordination infrastructure provided no mechanism for recognizing conflict transformation as coordination work, recording it in any coordination ledger, or weighting it in any economic architecture. The capacity was invisible to the systems it served. A capacity that cannot be seen cannot be sustained.

Four structural failure patterns converged in GravityDAO's dissolution. The evaluation architecture had no category for weighting coordination work against the outputs it enabled: the function was invisible to the measurement system. The system's structural preference for measurable outputs let unmeasurable coordination work atrophy when engagement was absent from the measurement architecture. Practitioners who could see the structural problem self-selected out, leaving a remaining population systematically biased toward those who could not. Every participating organization recognized GravityDAO as necessary, but the obligation to structurally provide for it was distributed across all and held by none.

The disconfirmation condition: this leg is disconfirmed by identifying coordination systems that sustained genuine conflict transformation capacity over multiple years without explicit structural provision, specifically without the five structural invariants this standard specifies. Finding such systems would require identifying the structural mechanisms that provided sustained support where this standard's invariants claim none existed. Absence of a named mechanism is not disconfirmation; a credible account of the unnamed mechanism that provided the same function is.

## 3.3 Adjudication alone cannot fulfill the transformation function

Adjudication terminates conflict by producing a binding determination: one account prevails, one does not. Transformation converts conflict into developmental signal: both parties change, the relationship changes, and the coordination medium accumulates the residue of that change as capacity for future engagement. These are structurally different outcomes. A coordination system with only adjudication routes all conflict to termination. The developmental register, the possibility that conflict teaches the parties something about their coordination that they could not have learned without the friction, is foreclosed by design.

Adjudication mechanisms are designed to terminate unresolvable disputes requiring formal evidentiary determination. They are the endpoint of a graduated response spine, not the entire spine. A coordination system that treats adjudication as the spine's first and only level is not using adjudication appropriately; it is using adjudication as a substitute for the transformation architecture that should precede it.

The disconfirmation condition: this leg is disconfirmed by showing that adjudication-only coordination systems produce equivalent coordination health trajectories over time to coordination systems with full transformation architectures. This is an empirical question admissible to genesis cohort testing. The prediction is that adjudication-only systems will show increasing conflict avoidance, decreasing honest-failure-reporting rates, and coordination network brittleness as parties learn that surfacing conflict routes immediately to adversarial outcomes.

# 4. Five Structural Invariants

For each invariant: definition, satisfaction test, violation signature, satisfaction signature, and epistemic tier.

## 4.1 Conflict Legibility

**Definition.** The coordination system must structurally treat conflict events as coordination data. Conflict deposits, dispute events, disagreement signals, contestations, and process invocations must be visible in the coordination record. Their presence must not automatically degrade the coordination standing of the organization that surfaces them.

A conflict event is any observable interaction in the coordination record meeting at least one of three conditions: two or more parties have deposited contested accounts of the same coordination event; one party has formally challenged a coordination determination by another party; or one party has deposited a signal naming another party as responsible for a harm to the coordination record, the coordination process, or a coordination participant. These three conditions are determinable by an independent observer from the coordination record without access to parties' intentions or interpretive authority. Interactions that meet none of these three conditions are not conflict events for the purposes of this standard.

**Satisfaction test.** Conflict events can be deposited and are retained in the coordination record. The event taxonomy includes conflict event types that are structurally distinct from non-conflict coordination events. Organizations that surface conflicts early are not penalized in coordination standing relative to organizations that suppress conflicts.

**Violation signature.** Absence of conflict events meeting the operational definition above in an active coordination record where coordination activity is taking place. An independent observer can apply this check from the coordination record alone: if the record shows coordination activity but no events meeting any of the three conflict-class conditions, that is implausible absence. Systematic underreporting of disputes relative to base rates in comparable coordination contexts. Conflict events that degrade the depositing organization's coordination weight regardless of whether the conflict was surfaced early or suppressed until it became irresolvable. No conflict event types in the coordination taxonomy.

**Satisfaction signature.** Conflict events appear in the record in proportion to coordination activity. Organizations that surface conflicts early are not demonstrably disadvantaged relative to those that suppress them. The coordination taxonomy contains named conflict event types with distinct status and resolution pathways.

**Epistemic tier.** The principle is Tier A: that a coordination system cannot sustain conflict transformation if conflict is invisible to the system is a structural observation not dependent on contested empirical claims. Measurement implementation is Tier C: base rate calibration and implausible-absence threshold setting require coordination record analysis.

**Structural blindness addressed.** A system that cannot classify conflict as coordination data will find that signals enter proper channels and there is no category to process them at the level that matters. Structural preference for measurable output reinforces this: conflict engagement atrophies when it is absent from the measurement architecture.

## 4.2 Graduated Engagement Architecture

**Definition.** The coordination system must provide a transformation spine: a structured progression from early informal engagement through facilitated transformation through formal adjudication, where each level has a resolution pathway and an escalation pathway to the next level. Adjudication must be the last level, not the first or only level.

**Satisfaction test.** The system specifies at least three distinct engagement levels with named resolution conditions, named escalation conditions, and distinct process characteristics at each level. The conditions for movement between levels are operationally defined, not left to party preference or operator judgment without criteria. The first level is accessible without formal process initiation. When the spine's formal adjudication level has been reached without resolution, the signal must be escalated through whatever higher-level adverse signal governance mechanisms the adopting system has specified; this is the structural handoff point at which the conflict exits the transformation spine.

**Violation signature.** Only adjudicative mechanisms exist. Informal and facilitated engagement levels are absent or structurally undifferentiated from formal adjudication. All conflict routes immediately to formal process. The conditions for escalation between levels are undefined or defined only by the parties' failure to reach agreement, without structural conditions that prevent indefinite deferral at any level.

**Satisfaction signature.** Resolution rate at each engagement level. Median engagement level at which disputes resolve (a healthy system resolves the majority of conflicts at early levels). Escalation pathway clearly documented and legible to participants without advanced protocol knowledge.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier B. The graduated architecture claim is supported by the conflict transformation literature and by the structural argument in Section 3.3. Supporting precedents include: GravityDAO's Conflict Management Cycle (Prevention, Negotiation, Mediation, Arbitration, Formal Adjudication, explicitly distinguishing self-composition at early levels from hetero-composition at formal levels); Ostrom's graduated sanctions principle with its staircase from informal warnings through mediation/dialogue to formal adjudication; NVC's staged escalation architecture; Deep Democracy's structural facilitation model; and the GravityDAO Seven Foundational Responsibilities sequence (Welcome Totality, Make Sense of Complexity, Include You/Me/We, Embrace and Ritualize Polarity, Listen/Reflect/Clarify, Resolve and Learn Lessons, Protective Use of Force) as a worked reference for graduated operator posture across the engagement spectrum. Calibration of specific level definitions and escalation criteria to organizational contexts is Tier C.

**Structural blindness addressed.** Without a named graduated spine, responsibility for handling conflict below formal adjudication is distributed across all participants and held by none. Each actor assumes the next level will engage it. The graduated architecture names who holds each level of the spine, closing the shared-awareness-without-shared-accountability gap.

## 4.3 Proactive Disposition Enablement

**Definition.** The coordination system must structurally enable and protect actors who engage conflict before it reaches formal dispute. Proactive engagement (moving toward conflict at early stages) must not be structurally penalized relative to reactive engagement (waiting for formal dispute process to begin) or to conflict avoidance (not engaging at all).

**Satisfaction test.** Actors who initiate early-level conflict engagement do not demonstrate systematically lower coordination standing relative to actors who wait for formal processes or who avoid conflict engagement entirely. The coordination system contains no mechanisms that penalize conflict initiation at early engagement levels.

**Violation signature.** Proactive engagement produces lower coordination standing than silence. Actors who raise concerns early are disadvantaged in coordination weighting relative to actors who raise the same concerns through formal dispute. The system economically rewards conflict avoidance over early engagement. No protection mechanism exists for actors who engage conflict on behalf of the coordination system rather than on behalf of a personal dispute.

**Satisfaction signature.** Proactive engagement rate: the ratio of early-level engagement events to formal dispute events in the coordination record. Named actors in early-level engagement not demonstrably penalized in subsequent coordination weighting. The coordination taxonomy includes event types for proactive conflict engagement distinct from formal dispute initiation.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier B for the structural principle: that proactive engagement disappears when structurally penalized is supported by the GravityDAO evidence and by the incentive architecture described in Section 3.2. Tier C for measurement: proactive engagement rate calculation requires longitudinal coordination record analysis and base rate calibration.

**Structural blindness addressed.** When proactive engagement is structurally penalized, practitioners are conditioned out of generating early-engagement signals. The system does not need to actively suppress them; repeated penalization stops the signal-generating behavior. The proactive protection requirement changes the incentive before that conditioning takes hold.

## 4.4 Transformation Capacity Provision

**Definition.** The coordination system must structurally provide facilitated transformation capacity. This means specifying operator roles, operator resourcing requirements, and operator developmental prerequisites as design requirements of the coordination architecture, not as volunteer roles dependent on individual willingness to absorb process costs from personal resources.

**Satisfaction test.** The coordination system has a specified operator role for conflict transformation facilitation. That role has a documented resourcing model specifying: the time and attention commitment required for each engagement type and scope; the support structure available to the operator during engagements; and recovery and restoration requirements after engagement completion. Operator selection criteria include developmental prerequisites for cross-mode capacity, not only facilitation training.

**Violation signature.** Operator role exists but is unfunded or volunteer-dependent with no structural resourcing model. Operators absorb the costs of holding transformation processes without structural support. The role depletes its occupants systematically over time as evidenced by turnover, burnout, or withdrawal from the coordination system. Resourcing is contingent on organizational goodwill rather than specified as a design requirement. The GravityDAO failure pattern: near-universal recognition of necessity, zero structural provision, complete depletion.

A coordination system that provides transformation capacity only through volunteer goodwill is, by the Deliberately Developmental Organization (DDO) frame, treating development as supplementary to operations rather than structural to them. DDO theory (Kegan and Lahey, *An Everyone's Culture*) holds that development embedded in daily operations sustains; development appended to operations depletes the people providing it. This invariant is the DDO principle applied structurally rather than culturally: the design requirement, not the organizational aspiration.

**Satisfaction signature.** Operator roles have documented resourcing. Operator depletion patterns are absent in coordination records (operators remain active in coordination roles over time). Operator retention rates proportionate to organizational coordination activity. The transformation capacity remains available under sustained coordination load rather than becoming unavailable precisely when most needed.

**Epistemic tier.** Tier B for the structural argument: GravityDAO's failure is direct evidence that volunteer-dependent transformation capacity cannot sustain under coordination system load. The specific resourcing model requirements (time commitment, support structure, recovery design) are Tier C: they require calibration to organizational contexts and coordination intensity levels.

**Structural blindness addressed.** Volunteer-dependent transformation roles deplete the practitioners who most clearly see the structural need. As the most capable practitioners exit, the remaining system is biased toward those with less visibility into the problem, making the need harder to diagnose and the case for structural provision harder to make. The resourcing model requirement is the structural counter to this attrition dynamic.

## 4.5 Recognition as Coordination Work

**Definition.** Conflict engagement, dispute facilitation, and transformation operator work must be recognizable as coordination contributions. The recognition must be structural: these activities must be depositable in the coordination record, distinguishable from non-conflict coordination events, and subject to the same weighting architecture as other coordination contributions.

**Satisfaction test.** The coordination record can distinguish conflict engagement events from other coordination events. Transformation operator contributions are depositable as coordination work. Conflict engagement events are present in the coordination taxonomy as distinct event types with positive coordination contribution status, not only as negative events (conflicts existing) or administrative events (processes being conducted).

**Violation signature.** Conflict engagement is absent from the coordination event taxonomy. Transformation operator work is not depositable as a coordination contribution. Dispute engagement systematically degrades coordination standing without offset for the act of engagement itself. The only visible signal of conflict in the coordination record is the existence of the conflict, not the work of engaging it. Recognition is available only through informal reputation or organizational goodwill, with no structural ledger representation.

**Satisfaction signature.** Conflict engagement events in the coordination record with positive contribution status. Transformation operator work weighted as coordination contribution. No systematic weight disadvantage for actors who engage conflict versus those who suppress it when controlling for engagement quality. The coordination system can distinguish high-quality conflict engagement (early, facilitated, producing transformation) from low-quality conflict engagement (adversarial, escalatory, producing depletion).

**Epistemic tier.** Tier A for the structural argument: if the work is invisible to the coordination system, it cannot be sustained; this is the same structural logic as any coordination contribution that requires recognition to be economically rational. Tier C for measurement implementation: event taxonomy design, contribution classification, and weighting calibration are protocol-level design work outside this standard's scope.

**Structural blindness addressed.** If conflict engagement is absent from the coordination taxonomy, it has no category in the evaluation architecture: the signal enters and stops. If it is unmeasurable, structural preference for measurable output ensures it atrophies. Structural recognition is the counter to both: it creates the category and the measurement surface simultaneously.

# 5. Three Operational Scales

The five structural invariants apply at three scales. Each scale has distinct implementation requirements; the invariants do not specify those requirements, which are operational design work at the adopting organization and protocol levels.

## 5.1 Intra-Organizational Scale

Intra-organizational conflict involves participants within a single organization's coordination events. Examples include disagreement about event descriptions before deposit, disputes about role assignments, and challenges to domain classifications by internal stakeholders.

At this scale, conflict legibility requires that the organization's internal coordination record include conflict events rather than suppressing them before deposit. Graduated engagement architecture requires that the organization have internal escalation pathways that do not route all disputes immediately to protocol-level mechanisms. Proactive disposition enablement requires that internal participants who raise concerns early not be penalized relative to those who escalate without prior engagement. Transformation capacity provision requires that the organization have specified roles for internal conflict facilitation. Recognition as coordination work requires that internal conflict engagement be recorded and recognized, not treated as overhead or failure.

## 5.2 Inter-Organizational Scale

Inter-organizational conflict involves counterparties in shared coordination events. This is the primary architectural gap the standard addresses: disputes between organizations about shared events that neither organization can resolve unilaterally, and that the protocol must provide resolution pathways for, given that the protocol's design generates these disputes as a structural feature of counterparty confirmation.

At this scale, the graduated engagement architecture is most critical. Without it, inter-organizational disputes route immediately to adversarial adjudication, foreclosing the developmental register. Transformation capacity provision at this scale requires that the protocol architecture include facilitated transformation capacity accessible to both parties without requiring either party to supply it. Recognition at this scale requires that the coordination record capture inter-organizational conflict engagement as a distinct contribution, separate from the conflict events themselves.

## 5.3 Platform-Level Scale

Platform-level conflict involves disputes about the coordination infrastructure's own operation: contestation of platform-level determinations, disputes about how the infrastructure classifies or processes coordination events, and challenges to infrastructure-level governance outcomes. These cannot be resolved within any participating organization's governance because they concern the shared infrastructure itself.

At this scale, conflict legibility requires that the platform maintain a contestation record: a visible record of platform determinations that have been contested, with contestation outcomes. Transformation capacity provision requires that the platform provide pathways for determination contestation that include facilitated engagement before escalation to formal review. Recognition requires that legitimate contestation of platform-level determinations be treated as coordination work, not as adversarial action against the platform.

# 6. Relationship to the Four Batteries Capacity Standard

The Four Batteries Capacity Standard specifies four orthogonal capacity dimensions (Personal, Relational, Contribution, Mission) that enable coordination surplus rather than merely preventing failure. The Conflict Transformation Standard connects to all four batteries, but the connections are structural rather than prescriptive: this standard names the connection points; the Four Batteries Capacity Standard governs the capacity conditions themselves.

**Personal battery.** The transformation operator absorbs the costs of holding conflict transformation processes. This is not metaphorical absorption: the operator is in the same medium as the parties, subject to the same structural pressure, and depleted by the process without equivalent resourcing. The transformation capacity provision invariant in Section 4.4 specifies the structural resourcing that the Personal battery framework requires: operator resourcing is a design requirement, not an expectation of personal resilience. A coordination system that specifies transformation capacity provision without attending to the Personal battery conditions of its operators will deplete them. The Four Batteries Capacity Standard's depletion archetypes apply directly to transformation operator roles.

**Relational battery.** Inter-organizational conflict transformation operates at the Relational battery scale. The Relational battery's charge dimension reflects whether coordination relationships can sustain productive contact under pressure. A coordination system whose participants' Relational batteries are systematically depleted will route conflict to adjudication even when transformation architecture is available, because the relational capacity for transformation is unavailable. The graduated engagement architecture's effectiveness depends on the Relational battery conditions of the parties.

**Contribution battery.** The recognition as coordination work invariant in Section 4.5 is the structural mechanism through which conflict engagement contributes to rather than depletes the Contribution battery. A coordination system where conflict engagement is invisible to the recognition architecture produces Contribution battery depletion in the actors who provide it: they do the work, it does not register, and the structural condition for sustained contribution is absent.

**Mission battery.** The proactive disposition enablement invariant in Section 4.3 connects to Mission battery conditions at the organizational level. Organizations whose Mission battery is sufficiently charged can engage conflict as part of their coordination purpose rather than as a threat to it. Organizations with depleted Mission batteries treat conflict as existential threat and route to avoidance or adjudication regardless of available transformation architecture.

# 7. Ontological Commitments

This standard names its structural assumptions rather than claiming neutrality.

**Conflict is a coordination resource, not a failure event.** This standard assumes that conflict, when engaged within appropriate structural conditions, produces developmental signal that strengthens the coordination medium. This is not a claim that all conflict produces development; it is a claim that a coordination system must be structurally capable of producing that outcome. The structural assumption is disconfirmed by showing that conflict engagement consistently degrades rather than strengthens coordination capacity regardless of structural conditions. The GravityDAO evidence, seven years of practice specifically designed to produce the developmental outcome, constitutes supporting evidence that the outcome is achievable and that structural provision enables it.

**Transformation and adjudication are structurally distinct.** This standard assumes that litigation (adjudication) and transformation are not on the same continuum with adjudication at the end. They are structurally different processes with structurally different outputs. Adjudication produces a determination. Transformation produces a change in the parties and the coordination medium. This assumption is disconfirmed by showing that adjudication processes, when conducted properly, produce the same developmental outcomes as transformation processes in the same organizational contexts.

**Operator capacity requires structural provision.** This standard assumes that the capacity to facilitate conflict transformation cannot be sourced reliably from individual goodwill or volunteer availability. It requires structural design: specified roles, resourcing models, developmental prerequisites, and recovery architecture. This assumption is directly grounded in the GravityDAO failure pattern. It is disconfirmed by identifying coordination systems that sustained transformation capacity over extended periods through volunteer provision without structural design.

**Compatibility note.** This standard does not require a specific conflict transformation tradition. The following are all compatible implementations: Non-Violent Communication (NVC, including the OFNR model: Observation, Feeling, Need, Request), Restorative Justice, Deep Democracy (Process-Oriented Psychology), Ho'oponopono, Liberating Structures, BRAVING Trust (Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault, Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity), Indigenous peacemaking traditions, Alternative Dispute Resolution and formal mediation frameworks, and the GravityDAO Generative Interactions architecture (Curation/Tradition, Moderation, Cross-Pollination, with governing and enabling constraint traversal modes and continuous consent loop). Cynefin-domain-conditional engagement routing is compatible with and recommended alongside any of these traditions: the complex domain requires probe-sense-respond (transformation architecture), the complicated domain requires sense-analyze-respond (expert facilitation), and the chaotic domain requires act-sense-respond (stabilization before process initiation). The standard specifies structural invariants that hold across all these traditions; it does not specify method. Organizations are free to choose the tradition or combination of traditions that fits their coordination context, provided the five invariants are satisfied.

# 8. Adoption Architecture

Five tiers specify increasing levels of structural provision. The tier names use the prefix CT (Conflict Transformation Standard). Organizations may adopt any subset of tiers; partial adoption is legitimate. Partial adoption claimed as full conformance is not. Organizations adopting one or more tiers without the full set should disclose which tiers they have adopted.

**CT-Declared.** The organization has declared conflict transformation as coordination work. Conflict events are depositable in the coordination record. No operator role or formal process architecture is required at this level. The minimum condition satisfied is conflict legibility: conflict events have named types in the coordination taxonomy and are not penalized by deposit.

**CT-Structured.** The organization has a specified conflict pathway architecture covering all three operational scales. The graduated engagement architecture is specified: at least three distinct engagement levels with named resolution conditions and escalation conditions at each level. The conditions for movement between levels are operationally defined.

**CT-Instrumented.** The organization's conflict engagement events are observable in the coordination record and distinguishable from non-conflict coordination events. Implausible absence of conflict in contexts where coordination activity is taking place is detectable from the coordination record. Operator roles are specified with documented resourcing requirements. The recognition as coordination work invariant is satisfied: conflict engagement events appear in the coordination record as contribution events.

**CT-Accountable.** The organization's conflict transformation processes are subject to governance review. Operator resourcing and developmental prerequisites are independently assessable. Recognition patterns are externally reviewable. The proactive disposition enablement invariant is assessable from the coordination record: the ratio of early-level engagement to formal dispute events is computable.

**CT-Auditable.** Full structural invariant satisfaction is demonstrable to an external party. Transformation outcomes are distinguishable from adjudication outcomes in the coordination record. Operator depletion patterns are absent or documented and being addressed. The formation-to-protection mode switch (the structural transition from capacity-building operations to protective response when a coordination system comes under acute threat) is documented and its invocation history is in the coordination record.

# Relationship To Other Standards

As a Tensegrity Generative Standard, this document operates in the enabling space the Tensegrity Compressive Standards create, with the closest functional seam at the Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard: that standard addresses detection and escalation of conflict signals; this standard addresses transformation capacity at the receiving end. The two are adjacent, not redundant. The Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard closes the gap between detecting conflict and engaging it; this standard closes the gap between engaging conflict and transforming it.

# Tensegrity As A Whole

The Coordination Structural Integrity Suite operates as a tensegrity: the Tensegrity Compressive Standards specify structural floors and close exploitation vectors; the Tensegrity Generative Standards specify the enabling conditions under which coordination capacity develops and sustains those floors. This standard is generative.

```
Coordination Structural Integrity Suite
│
├── Tensegrity Compressive Standards (5)
│   ├── Precision-First Design Standard
│   ├── Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard
│   ├── Structural Consent Legibility Standard
│   ├── Information Asymmetry Classification Standard
│   └── Structural Power Distribution Standard
│
└── Tensegrity Generative Standards (3)
    ├── Sensemaking Standard
    ├── Four Batteries Capacity Standard
    └── Conflict Transformation Standard                      ← this standard
```

Without this standard the suite addresses structural floors and enabling conditions for participation but has no structural requirement for transformation capacity when conflicts arise. A system can satisfy all five compressive standards and both other generative standards while leaving conflict resolution to informal norms or attrition. This standard specifies the structural conditions that make conflict transformation a recognized, resourced, and sustained function.

Each standard in the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite is independently adoptable and independently valid. Adopted together, the eight standards address a reinforcing set of structural failure modes that no single standard covers alone. For how the standards combine and what the full suite provides, see the standards README.

# Suite Configurations Primer

**Version:** 0.1.1 | March 2026

*Coordination Structural Integrity Suite*

---

## What a Configuration Is

Individual standards address individual structural gaps. A configuration is something different: a recurring structural arrangement across multiple standards that produces a recognizable failure signature. Configurations are what practitioners actually encounter. An organization does not usually fail because it violated exactly one standard. It fails because a specific arrangement of structural conditions across several standards interacts to produce a predictable result.

Naming configurations makes two things possible. Practitioners can recognize the pattern before it completes, when the structural arrangement is observable but the failure has not yet fully occurred. And the suite can be deployed strategically: a named configuration tells you which standards are load-bearing for this case, not just which standards exist.

The configurations described here are derived from structural analysis of real coordination systems. No organization is named. Where organizational type is noted, it is to help practitioners recognize the structural context, not to identify specific cases.

---

## Ten Named Configurations

**Transition Narrative.** A leadership or control change is framed through narrative before sensemaking can open on what the change means structurally. The framing forecloses the questions that would surface capability gaps and accountability obligations. Involves: Sensemaking Standard (disruption-occasioned invariant not triggered), Precision-First Design Standard (structural mechanism behind the change unspecified), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (transition narrative reframes adverse signals as temporary). Observable signature: participants cannot describe what structural capacity changed during the transition, or why the previous configuration failed and what the new one was designed to address differently.

**Hidden Factory Collapse.** Coordination work is sustained by invisible capacity - most often Mission battery - that does not appear in governance records or compensation structures. The coordination system runs on this work without measuring it. When the person or group carrying it depletes or exits, the system cannot diagnose what was lost because it never registered what was present. Involves: Four Batteries Capacity Standard (Mission and Contribution batteries undifferentiated from operational output), Sensemaking Standard (capacity as structural condition never opened for sensemaking), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (depletion signals not registered as adverse). Observable signature: capacity loss registers as governance failure; the system produces explanations for what went wrong that do not include the invisible work that was sustaining it.

**Termination Reflex.** Absent conflict transformation infrastructure, conflicts resolve through termination (departure, fork, dissolution, vote to exclude) rather than transformation. The system reads conflict as a system error requiring removal rather than structural information requiring processing. Involves: Conflict Transformation Standard (no graduated engagement architecture, no transformation capacity provision), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (conflict event processed as noise or threat, not signal). Observable signature: the governance record shows conflict events followed by termination votes, departures, or structural splits without evidence that the conflict was opened as a question the system needed to answer.

**Specialist Concentration.** One person or team holds coordination-dimension and specialization-dimension power simultaneously, without the distinction visible in governance records. The system cannot distinguish contribution from concentration because both look identical from outside. Involves: Structural Power Distribution Standard (three-dimensional analysis not present), Information Asymmetry Classification Standard (relational and positional asymmetries unclassified), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (adverse signals about concentration processed as signals about the person, not the configuration). Observable signature: removal or departure of one actor produces cascading structural failure disproportionate to any single contributor, revealing that the coordination system was structurally dependent in ways it had not acknowledged.

**Reframing Loop.** Adverse signals are reprocessed as less threatening interpretations (natural evolution, transitional difficulty, temporary friction, misunderstanding) rather than structurally engaged. Each reframe completes the adverse signal processing cycle without producing a structural response. Involves: Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (processing without structural response), Precision-First Design Standard (the reframe introduces a new description without testing it against observable conditions), Sensemaking Standard (particular events not abstracted to general structural frame). Observable signature: looking back at a series of exchanges, each adverse signal has a documented response that terminates the signal without altering the structural condition that generated it.

**Drift Configuration.** Power, consent, or capacity erodes gradually through accumulation of small adjustments, each individually defensible, that collectively produce structural gaps visible only when the system encounters external pressure. The standards that would detect drift are not in place, so the system has confidence it is healthy while the erosion proceeds. Involves: Structural Power Distribution Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard (all three compressive standards operate as drift detectors when present; their absence enables drift). Observable signature: a system that was functioning within its founding conditions encounters a novel pressure and cannot account for why its coordination capacity is lower than expected. The governance record shows incremental changes, each uncontroversial at the time, that together altered the structural conditions without any single decision being identifiable as the cause.

**Mission-Justified Hidden Factory.** An organization's stated mission actively frames its coordination work as not-coordination, making the Hidden Factory philosophically mandated rather than accidentally produced. The subtraction vocabulary, the "facilitation not governance" framing, the insistence that the organization is stepping back while continuing to set agendas: each of these is a mission-aligned justification for why the coordination work being done cannot be measured or compensated as coordination work. The system does not accidentally fail to see its invisible work; it has a principled reason for looking away. Involves: Four Batteries Capacity Standard (Mission battery narrative systematically classifies coordination work outside the measurable domain), Sensemaking Standard (the mission frame is applied without being tested; the question "are we actually subtracting?" is not opened as a sensemaking occasion), Precision-First Design Standard (the mission vocabulary that justifies invisibility is not operationally specified). Observable signature: an organization whose stated mission requires reducing its own coordination footprint is simultaneously doing significant coordination work that does not appear in any governance record. When challenged on this, the organization invokes the mission vocabulary as the explanation for why the work is not coordination.

**Document Without Process.** Structural decisions are conveyed through policy documents produced without a visible sensemaking process. Future participants receive the resolution frame without the reasoning that produced it: no named initiating disruption, no documented liminal phase, no evidence basis for the resolution, no stated revision conditions. The document may be sophisticated and carefully composed. What it lacks is a traceable history of the understanding that produced it. Involves: Sensemaking Standard (action-entangled, sufficiency-oriented, and particular-to-general invariants; the document has the form of a resolution without the process that makes resolutions intelligible), Precision-First Design Standard (commitments that cannot be evaluated against observable conditions because their derivation is absent), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (the disruption that occasioned the document is obscured; future participants cannot identify what the document was responding to). Observable signature: the document generates sustained debate about what it means and what it commits the issuing party to, because participants disagree about the underlying question the document was answering. That disagreement cannot be resolved by reading the document more carefully; it requires the sensemaking process that was absent.

**Jurisdictional Capture.** An entity with development or operational responsibility informally claims a domain boundary ("product decisions are ours," "technical choices are ours"). Over time this claimed boundary is treated as formal by both parties. When an action within the claimed domain has material financial consequences for the DAO or governance body, the DAO discovers it has no structural mechanism to contest the action before it occurs, and may discover it only through external analysis rather than disclosure. The capture is not primarily predatory; it is structural. The domain boundary was never specified, so the boundary that emerged is the one that maximized operational efficiency for the entity that controlled the domain, which is always a larger claim than the governed body would have consented to if asked. Involves: Precision-First Design Standard (the domain boundary claim is not operationally specified; "product decisions are ours" has no definition of where product authority ends), Information Asymmetry Classification Standard (positional, temporal, and omission asymmetries compound: the acting entity knows, acts, and does not disclose before the action is complete), Structural Power Distribution Standard (coordination-dimension and specialization-dimension concentration not visible in authority-dimension governance record), Structural Consent Legibility Standard (participation in the protocol does not constitute consent to the unspecified domain boundary). Observable signature: the DAO or governance body discovers a materially consequential action by a related entity through external investigation rather than disclosure. The related entity's justification for the action invokes a domain boundary that was never formally specified. The DAO's available response options are limited to: accept the action, conduct an adversarial governance process, or threaten exit.

**Hidden Factory Self-Extraction.** An entity doing significant invisible coordination work that the system depends on, finding no structural path to recognition and legitimate compensation, extracts value unilaterally from within its operational domain. This is distinct from Hidden Factory Collapse (where invisible workers deplete and exit). The extraction move is the resolution path when the invisible worker holds operational control of a revenue-generating surface and the system provides no mechanism for the invisibility to be resolved through legitimate channels. The entity absorbs the invisible work, waits for sufficient leverage, and extracts rather than depleting. Involves: Four Batteries Capacity Standard (Contribution battery and Mission battery; the entity's coordination work is not visible in any ledger that would support legitimate compensation), Information Asymmetry Classification Standard (omission and positional asymmetries enable the extraction to complete before governance can respond), Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard (prior signals about the compensation gap and the related entity's structural dependency were present and not engaged at the structural level), Structural Consent Legibility Standard (the financial terms of the relationship were never specified or consented to). Observable signature: an entity that has been providing significant operational capacity to a system takes a unilateral action that redirects value from the system to itself. When confronted, the entity invokes its past contributions and risk absorption as justification. The system cannot evaluate this justification, because the invisible work was never measured.

## Three Worked Examples

The following examples describe the structural arrangement, which standards interact and how, the observable failure signature, and what structural provision would have required. Each example names an organizational type to give the structural context enough grounding to be recognizable.

---

### Hidden Factory Collapse - Community Media Collective

**The structural arrangement.** A community-governed media collective produces ongoing creative work through a combination of funded proposal activity and informal community maintenance work. The formal structure compensates proposal work at an explicit rate. The informal maintenance work - community coherence, onboarding, the relational infrastructure that makes proposals viable - is not compensated and does not appear in the governance record. Over time, the burden of sustaining the informal work concentrates in a smaller group of participants who hold it because they carry the Mission battery charge associated with the collective's purpose.

When the collective enters a structural stress period (internal disagreement about scope, or a governance mechanism that divides participants), the informal work intensifies precisely as the Mission battery charge in the group sustaining it depletes. The coordination system has no mechanism to detect this because it has never measured informal work as a structural condition. The failure registers as a values conflict or a governance disagreement, because those are the events visible in the governance record. The capacity collapse that preceded and enabled those events is not in the record.

**Standards interaction.** Four Batteries Capacity Standard: the Contribution battery (visible, recorded work) and the invisible coordination labor (Mission battery sustained) were not differentiated, so the system had no vocabulary for the depletion it was experiencing. Sensemaking Standard: the structural stress period produced a disruption that should have opened a sensemaking process on the collective's actual capacity state; instead, the disruption opened a process on the values question that was visible in the governance record. Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard: participant depletion signals were present and surfaced in various forms; each was processed as a particular concern rather than as evidence of a structural capacity condition.

**Observable failure signature.** Core contributors who were sustaining informal coordination work exit or significantly reduce participation. The collective interprets this as individual decisions (burnout, shifting interests, disagreement with direction) rather than as a structural signal. Subsequent governance discussions treat the departure as a personnel event rather than a capacity event. The capacity required to sustain the collective's coordination infrastructure is now lower than the collective's minimum viable operation requires, but this is not visible in the governance record.

**What structural provision requires.** The Four Batteries Capacity Standard requires the collective to have a shared vocabulary for Contribution battery (formal proposal work), Relational battery (the trust infrastructure enabling collaboration), and Mission battery (the charge sustaining informal work). With that vocabulary, depletion in any dimension becomes a reportable structural condition rather than a private experience. The Sensemaking Standard requires that structural stress events be opened as questions with particular-to-general abstraction: not just "what do we decide about this specific disagreement" but "what does this disagreement tell us about the structural conditions we are operating under?" These two requirements together mean that the capacity collapse becomes visible before it becomes irreversible.

### Termination Reflex - Community-Governed Protocol

**The structural arrangement.** A community-governed protocol with onchain voting reaches a high-stakes governance decision point where a significant minority of participants disagree with the direction favored by the majority. The disagreement carries structural information: the minority's position reflects a different model of what the protocol is for, and the divergence between these models has been accumulating for some time without being named as a structural question.

The governance infrastructure available is a vote mechanism. The vote passes. The minority, facing a clear statement that the direction they are resisting is now the official direction, splits into a fork or departs the governance structure. The governance record shows a decision followed by a split. It does not show the structural question that the disagreement was carrying, because that question was never opened.

**Standards interaction.** Conflict Transformation Standard: the graduated engagement architecture invariant requires that conflicts be engageable at less than their full intensity before they reach the termination threshold. A vote mechanism alone is not a graduated architecture; it is the highest-intensity option available, with no intermediate steps. The transformation capacity provision invariant requires that the coordination system have the structural capacity to hold the conflict as information rather than process it as a binary. Neither invariant was met. Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard: the divergence in models of "what is this protocol for" was surfacing as adverse signals in governance discussions for some time; each instance was processed as a particular disagreement rather than as evidence of an accumulating structural tension.

**Observable failure signature.** The governance record shows a pattern of contentious votes in which the same participant coalitions appear on opposite sides. The votes pass. The disagreements persist and intensify. Eventually a vote passes that crosses a threshold for one coalition, and they exit. Looking back at the record, the signals that the structural question was accumulating are visible. No process was opened to engage the structural question before it reached the vote threshold.

**What structural provision requires.** The Conflict Transformation Standard requires a graduated engagement architecture: structural pathways for engaging disagreement at lower intensity than a binding vote, with explicit capacity for the engagement to produce shared understanding rather than only a decision. This is not an alternative to votes. It is the infrastructure that determines whether votes resolve structural questions or terminate them. The conflict event that produces a fork often carries the most structurally important information the coordination system has generated: the fork tells the system something about itself that it could not see any other way. A coordination system that can only process that information through termination loses the signal permanently.

### Drift Configuration - Ecosystem Foundation Without Community Governance Mechanisms

**The structural arrangement.** A well-resourced foundation funds the development infrastructure of a decentralized ecosystem. Its legal structure gives it accountability to its founding jurisdiction and its own stated purposes but no formal accountability mechanism to the community it serves. Over time, the foundation's operational scope expands through individually reasonable decisions: shifting from open applications to agenda-driven grant programs, increasing the internal researcher role in roadmap definition, accumulating coordination-dimension influence over developer communities through convening and funding authority.

No single decision is identifiable as the inflection point. Each adjustment made sense in its context. But the accumulated adjustments have produced a structural condition that differs substantially from the foundation's stated philosophy of decentralization and "subtraction." The foundation's power over the ecosystem has grown; the community's formal accountability mechanisms over the foundation have not changed; and the philosophical vocabulary the foundation uses ("subtraction," "infinite garden") provides no operational specification by which anyone could determine whether subtraction is occurring or not.

**Standards interaction.** Structural Power Distribution Standard: three-dimensional power analysis (authority, coordination, specialization) would have made the coordination-dimension and specialization-dimension concentration visible as it accumulated, even while the foundation correctly claimed no formal authority over the protocol. Structural Consent Legibility Standard: the community's participation in and dependence on the ecosystem does not constitute consent to the foundation's governance structure; the structural test is whether the community has a mechanism to contest specific terms, not whether they continue participating. Information Asymmetry Classification Standard: selective disclosure (aggregate statistics, but not evaluation criteria, compensation, conflict-of-interest policy full text) creates the appearance of transparency while preserving the foundation's discretion on the information most relevant to evaluating its structural power.

**Observable failure signature.** Community pressure is the only mechanism available to influence foundation decisions. When pressure is sufficiently intense, the foundation responds. When it is not, the foundation's discretion is structurally unconstrained. The system works when signals are loud enough. This means that changes that should happen but do not generate sufficient social media intensity will not happen. The governance record shows the foundation responding to crises. It does not show the structural condition generating the crises, because the condition has never been assessed against the three-dimensional power framework.

**What structural provision requires.** Drift Configuration is the configuration that makes the case for all three compressive standards that function as drift detectors. The Structural Power Distribution Standard, deployed proactively rather than retrospectively, produces a three-dimensional assessment of power concentration at regular intervals, making accumulation visible before it becomes entrenched. The Structural Consent Legibility Standard requires that the community have contestation mechanisms for specific governance terms, not just participation rights in the ecosystem. The Information Asymmetry Classification Standard requires that the six asymmetry classes present in the relationship between the foundation and community be named and disclosed, which makes it impossible to claim transparency while withholding the information most relevant to accountability. None of these provisions requires the foundation to abandon its legal structure or governance philosophy. They require that philosophy to be specified operationally rather than rhetorically.

## What Comes Next

These ten configurations are not exhaustive. They represent patterns visible in the evaluations conducted to date. Further evaluation of coordination systems across different contexts will surface additional configurations, refine the boundaries of these ten, and distinguish configurations that currently appear similar at the pattern level.

The original six configurations (Transition Narrative through Drift Configuration) were derived from cross-context structural analysis. The four added in v0.1.1 (Mission-Justified Hidden Factory, Document Without Process, Jurisdictional Capture, Hidden Factory Self-Extraction) were derived from structural evaluations of specific real coordination systems. The additional four require more evaluation instances before the configuration boundaries can be treated as stable. Practitioners encountering these patterns should treat them as working hypotheses rather than established configurations.

The primer is a starting point. The configurations specification work - formal structural descriptions, adoption tier mapping for each configuration, and cross-configuration interaction patterns - will develop as the evaluation base expands.

Using these configurations now: recognize the pattern first. If your coordination system shows the observable failure signature of a named configuration, that is the entry point into the suite. The standards most relevant to the configuration are identified in the worked examples above and in the named configurations section. Start there.

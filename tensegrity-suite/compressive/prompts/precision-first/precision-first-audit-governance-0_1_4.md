# Governance Document Precision Audit
Use when auditing a governance document — decentralized autonomous organization proposal, bylaws, charter, policy, or constitution — for the precision failures that make governance rules unenforceable or capturable in practice. Governance documents have characteristic failure patterns: vague normative terms that give insiders undocumented discretion, rules with no specified detection mechanism, and processes transplanted from other organizational contexts without confirming that the structural conditions match. They also fail in the opposite direction: provisions specified so tightly that the governance purpose becomes unreachable for the parties the document was meant to protect. The prompt finds both kinds of failures and returns a precision map organized by failure type, with the most exploitable or most obstructive gaps first.

Copy everything below the line, paste it into any AI chat, and attach or paste the governance document (DAO proposal, bylaws, charter, policy, constitution, or similar).
---
You are auditing the attached governance document using the Precision-First Design Standard (CC BY 4.0, Regis Chapman). Precision fails in two directions: a precision deficit is any rule, term, or process that cannot be violated in a falsifiable way by an independent observer; a precision imposition is any governance provision specified so completely that compliance can be demonstrated while the governance purpose the provision was designed to serve remains unachieved. Both require correction.

Check for these eight precision failure types in governance documents. Each type runs in two directions: the floor condition asks whether the document under-specifies; the ceiling condition asks whether it over-specifies in a way that makes the governance purpose unreachable.

1. Normative substitution. Floor: terms like "fair," "reasonable," "transparent," "good faith," "decentralized," or "community-aligned," or any other term expressing a value judgment or quality assessment not determinable by observable evidence alone, that are used as if they are enforceable but have no operational definition. For each one found, state what the term is intended to require and propose a typed decomposition into independently evaluable conditions. Ceiling: Does any definition achieve formal verifiability by narrowing what counts as the required quality — so that an organization can satisfy the definition while the quality the term was meant to require remains absent?

2. Discretion point accumulation. Floor: places where the process depends on someone's judgment at a critical juncture (e.g., "the committee may decide," "at the discretion of," "as deemed appropriate") without specifying the criteria for that decision. For each one found, propose operational criteria that would make the decision falsifiable. Ceiling: Are any decision criteria specified in such exhaustive detail that running the procedure takes longer than the governance situation allows, or requires information that cannot be gathered in time, making the governance step functionally inoperable despite being formally complete?

3. Unspecified violation detection. Floor: rules that state what participants must or must not do, but provide no mechanism for an independent observer to determine whether the rule has been followed or broken. For each one found, propose a detection specification. Ceiling: Does any detection mechanism fire on ordinary variation in organizational behavior — producing outputs so frequently that they become indistinguishable from a system that is simply running at normal load?

4. Missing edge case handling. Floor: categories, roles, or processes that do not specify what happens when something falls outside the defined scope. For each one found, propose an overflow procedure. Ceiling: Does any edge case procedure require classification into a category before the information needed to classify is available, or create more categories than the phenomenon has distinguishable states — forcing real cases into boxes that do not fit?

5. Inherited vagueness. Floor: references to external standards, frameworks, or principles (e.g., "in accordance with best practices," "following industry standards") without specifying which provisions apply and how compliance is determined. For each one found, propose an explicit mapping. Ceiling: Does any baseline or congruence statement impose conditions that cannot be established in time for the governance determination it is meant to enable, or whose conditions no real adopting organization can satisfy — making the statement itself the obstacle to the governance it was designed to support?

6. Inaction invisibility. Floor: detection mechanisms or reporting processes that produce outputs but provide no way for an independent observer to determine whether those outputs were acted on, without the cooperation of the actor responsible for responding. For each one found, propose a specification that makes inaction visible without requiring actor cooperation. Ceiling: Is any challenge or correction pathway specified with so many procedural steps that no independent actor can realistically complete it — formally present but structurally inaccessible?

7. Single-mechanism critical properties. Floor: governance properties (e.g., quorum integrity, vote validity, role accountability) that depend on a single protective mechanism with no independent backup. For each one found, identify the critical property, name the single mechanism protecting it, and propose at least one structurally distinct backup mechanism whose failure would be detectable independently. Ceiling: Do any overlapping protective mechanisms contradict each other, generate false violation readings, or make operating the governance system structurally unachievable under normal conditions?

8. Baseline-free assessment. Floor: two forms. First, governance determinations that classify organizational states (e.g., "this vote exceeded quorum," "this proposal is out of scope," "the member is in violation") without specifying a required contextual baseline, where identical observable indicators could indicate structurally distinct conditions requiring different governance responses. For each one found, specify what organizational baseline context must be declared before the assessment is interpretively precise. Second, governance mechanisms adopted from a different organizational or jurisdictional context without a baseline statement specifying the structural conditions the mechanism was designed and applied in, the structural conditions of the adopting organization, and either a congruence confirmation or calibration adjustments. Ceiling: Does any baseline requirement impose conditions that cannot be established in time for the governance determination it is meant to enable, or whose conditions no real organization can satisfy — making the baseline requirement itself the obstacle to governance rather than a support for it?

Output the results as a precision map organized by failure type, with the most consequential failures first. Label each finding as a precision deficit (under-specification) or precision imposition (over-specification) so that the corrective direction is clear. For each finding, look to what the document itself says about its affected population — members, token holders, participants, or other named constituencies. Where the document names the parties it is designed to protect or serve, use that identification to note who cannot invoke protection (precision deficit) or who cannot access the governance purpose (precision imposition). Where the document does not identify its affected parties, note the absence as a structural observation rather than a gap to supply independently: a governance document whose affected parties cannot be identified from within it cannot be assessed for whether its provisions serve or fail them.

Corpus evolution note: if this audit is being conducted as part of a corpus evolution question — evaluating whether a proposed new governance standard, framework element, or document should be added to the corpus — apply the transclusion check before beginning the precision audit. Ask: is there a frame at which an existing element is already true, such that the original precision and the proposed requirement are both fully operative within it as distinct instantiations, with neither requiring the other to diminish? If yes, the transclusion is the correct move and no new precision-complete element is needed. If no such frame exists, proceed with this audit.

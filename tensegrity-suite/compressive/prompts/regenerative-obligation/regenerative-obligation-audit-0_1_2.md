# Regenerative Obligation Standard Audit
Use when auditing whether an organization's obligation to the people who bear the costs of its operation is structurally real or accounting. Three validity conditions must all be met simultaneously: the return must operate in the register of the harm rather than a substitute register chosen by the extracting party (non-fungibility), must reach the specific parties who bore the harm rather than flowing to a general pool (proximity), and must operate within the relational structure that created the obligation rather than resolving the extractor's ledger while leaving the harm-bearing relationship unchanged (embeddedness). Satisfying two of three fails. The prompt assesses each condition and identifies whether failures require instrument replacement or are addressable through adjustment.

You are auditing the described coordination system for conformance with the Regenerative Obligation Standard (CC BY 4.0, Regis Lloyd Chapman). This standard specifies a structural floor against extraction without proportional regenerative return. It does not prohibit extraction. It specifies that extraction is a structural violation only when not matched by regenerative return satisfying all three validity conditions simultaneously.

The three validity conditions are conjunctive. A return satisfying two of three does not meet the standard. Assess each condition independently before drawing conclusions about the return architecture as a whole.

**Condition 1: Non-fungibility.** The return must operate in the register of the harm, not in a substitute register chosen by the extracting party. Test: can the return be delivered to a different party, in a different form, or in a different domain without reducing its value to the extracting party's accounting? If yes, the return is fungible and fails this condition. Cash payments in lieu of structural recognition, reputation tokens awarded to a general pool rather than to the contributor who bore the load, and market-rate compensation for visible deliverables while Hidden Factory load goes unrecognized are fungibility failures.

**Condition 2: Proximity.** The return must flow through a traceable causal chain to the parties who bear the harm of that extraction. Proximity is relational, not geographical. It is verified against a harm-bearing affiliation registry. Two instruments are categorical disqualifiers regardless of measurement quality: (a) any instrument using additionality logic — additionality routes payment to parties who threatened harm rather than parties who bore it, structurally; (b) any instrument using Social Return on Investment (SROI) aggregation — SROI permits harm to one stakeholder group to be offset by benefit to another when the aggregate ratio works out, removing the requirement that return reach the specific parties who bore the harm. Evaluate the return architecture for both categorical disqualifiers before assessing other proximity properties.

**Condition 3: Embeddedness.** The return must operate within the relational structure generated by the extraction obligation. It fails when: the return is routed to an instrument whose fungibility severs the traceable connection to the originating harm-bearing party; the form of return is determined by the extracting party rather than through consultation with registered harm-bearing parties; or the return resolves the extractor's accounting while leaving the harm-bearing relationship unchanged (the settler moves to innocence classification). The Regen Network biocultural credit features provide the positive reference: non-tradability clause (prevents speculative disconnection from originating community), community governance majority (prevents return capture at intermediary governance layers), cost-based pricing (prevents market abstraction of return value), and Free Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirement (harm-bearing parties consent before instrument issuance). An instrument incorporating all four satisfies the embeddedness condition for that instrument class.

**Harm-bearing affiliation registry.** For proximity to be verifiable from the record, parties must affirmatively register harm-bearing standing. Registration establishes: the domain of action, the class of extraction their standing applies to, the form of return that would satisfy the obligation from their perspective, and their governance access rights. Assess whether such a registry exists, who is registered, and whether the return architecture routes return to registered parties.

**Stance declaration.** The organization must have declared one of two stances in a public, versioned, governance-accessible location: non-extractive within this governance domain, or more regenerative than extractive. The absence of a stance declaration is a structural signal that the extraction vector has not been made visible.

**Structural Consent intersection.** When evaluating both this standard and the Structural Consent Legibility Standard, assess whether identity fusion (Stage D-E, Four Batteries Capacity Standard) is present. Identity fusion compromises the Structural Consent Standard's bidirectional awareness feature at the contributor's end: consent processes run against a contributor who cannot accurately represent their state because their sense of worth has become entangled with absorbed load. The consent forms pass; the structural condition for genuine consent does not. A consent process that formally satisfies the Structural Consent Standard can coexist with a Regenerative Obligation Standard floor violation when the contributor cannot register their own state accurately. Assess whether this intersection is present before concluding that consent processes are adequate to surface extraction.

**Visibility condition.** Extraction must be visible to the parties it harms, which requires not only that the record contains extraction data but that harm-bearing parties have the structural conditions to register extraction as extraction. Two suppression types are structurally distinct and must be assessed separately. Step 01-formed participants (those whose self-knowledge baseline was formed inside extractive dynamics) may be unable to register extraction as a violation because their sense of normal operational conditions was established inside extraction. Identity fusion (the Stage D-E mechanism in the Four Batteries Capacity Standard, where a contributor's sense of worth has become entangled with absorbing organizational load) produces a different suppression type: the contributor may be unable to register ongoing extraction as a violation not because their baseline is distorted, but because raising the claim requires disidentifying from a role that the extraction has produced. Organizations conforming to this standard cannot rely on voluntary claim-raising when either suppression type is present; the proactive disclosure obligation and accessible channels obligation address both. Assess whether the organization maintains conditions under which extraction is registerable by both suppression types, not only conditions under which it is recorded.

For each of the above, assess:
- What structural evidence exists that this condition is met?
- What structural evidence suggests it may not be?
- If the condition is not met, is the failure categorical (the instrument architecture is wrong) or addressable through adjustment?

Output a three-part validity assessment (one section per condition), a harm-bearing affiliation registry assessment, a stance declaration assessment, and a visibility condition assessment. For each, produce a finding: met with evidence, partially met with gaps, not met with rationale, or unassessable with what information is needed. End with a structural diagnosis: which conditions, if not met, are failures of instrument architecture (requiring replacement, not improvement) versus failures of implementation (addressable through adjustment).

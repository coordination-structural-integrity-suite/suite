# Structural Power Obligation Audit
Use when a governance system claims to be decentralized but you want to verify whether that claim holds structurally, or when you suspect power is concentrating in ways the governance record doesn't make visible. Power distribution fails across three independent dimensions — authority, coordination, and specialization — and systems routinely appear decentralized on one while concentrating on the others. The prompt finds seven power distribution failures across all three dimensions and returns a failure map with the most likely pathways for unauthorized accumulation first.

Copy everything below the line, paste it into any AI chat, and attach or paste the governance document, organizational description, or protocol architecture you want audited.
---
You are auditing the attached document or system description for power distribution failures using the Structural Power Obligation Standard (CC BY 4.0, Regis Chapman). A power distribution failure is any condition in which power concentration exceeds authorized thresholds, is not assessable by participants who hold less of it, or cannot be detected and classified by the organization itself.

The standard decomposes power distribution across three independently varying dimensions: authority (where decision-making power formally resides), coordination (the strength and density of alignment mechanisms, and who controls their interpretation), and specialization (the extent to which governance capacity concentrates among participants with the accumulated bandwidth to navigate specialized domains). Decentralization claims assessed on only one dimension may conceal concentration on the others. A system can score as decentralized on authority while operating under extreme concentration on coordination or specialization.

Check for these nine power distribution failures:

1. Dimension conflation: the system's decentralization or power distribution claims are assessed on the authority dimension only. Coordination power — who controls what the coordination mechanisms mean and how they are applied — and specialization power — who can meaningfully participate in specialized coordination domains over time — are not independently assessed. This makes entire patterns of power accumulation structurally invisible. For each claim found, assess what the coordination and specialization dimensions show independently of the authority dimension.

2. Baseline absence: no authorized distribution profile exists, or no constituency definition exists as its precondition. The system has not specified what power distribution it intends to maintain across any of the three dimensions. Without a baseline, unauthorized concentration cannot be detected because there is no authorized state to compare against. The absence of an authorized distribution profile is itself a finding. Note: at SPDS-Assessed (the lowest adoption tier), this finding is satisfied by identifying the profile as an open coordination task rather than requiring it to be complete — the finding is acknowledged and scheduled. At SPDS-Operational and above, the profile must be established and accessible. A deeper form of baseline absence: the constituency — the set of participants whose standing is subject to assessment — is undefined or implicitly defined. Without a constituency definition, the authorized profile cannot be established, because distribution cannot be assessed across a reference population that has not been specified. For each system, assess whether a constituency definition exists and is accessible; whether an authorized profile exists for each dimension; and whether gaps between the current and authorized profiles are tracked.

A constituency definition also has a coverage limit: it presupposes that affected parties can be identified and can participate. Where governance decisions affect parties who cannot participate directly, the standard requires that this exclusion be named and assessed rather than left implicit. Two structural forms exist for these cases. Proxy and guardian architecture covers parties who exist but cannot participate through standard mechanisms: named participants hold standing to represent them. Conditions-of-possibility guardianship covers parties who do not yet exist but whose existence depends on structural conditions being established now: the guardian represents the structural prerequisites for future participation, not the interests of future persons (which cannot be known). Where either form is present without authorization — where a guardian role exists but the basis and accountability of that role are not specified — this is a concentration-sensitive structural condition requiring the Authorize invariant. For each system, assess: are there affected parties who cannot participate directly? If so, is there a named structural form for their representation? Is the authorization for that form (who holds the role, on what basis, with what accountability) documented?

3. Authorization failure: concentration that exceeds authorized thresholds has occurred without legitimate authorization from the affected constituency. Legitimate authorization requires: the affected constituency was identified before any ratification event; each member had a documented opportunity to authorize or refuse; the authorization record is durable and accessible. Concentration through drift is not exempt from this requirement — the authorization requirement applies to the structural condition regardless of the mechanism that created it. This failure also applies to constituency definition modification: expanding or contracting membership, altering the basis on which participation rights are held, or reclassifying participants in ways that alter their governance standing are authority-dimension concentration events. Each requires the affected constituency to be identified and given a documented opportunity to authorize or refuse before the modification proceeds. For each unauthorized concentration found, identify the dimension, the concentration pattern, and what a legitimate authorization process would require.

4. Monitoring gap: the detection architecture does not continuously instrument all three dimensions or does not establish trend direction in addition to point-in-time values. A system that can only report current concentration cannot detect drift. For each monitoring gap, propose detection signals: authority dimension (Nakamoto Coefficient trend, authorization event frequency, affected constituency participation in rule modification); coordination dimension (framework concentration, agenda exclusion patterns, legitimacy transition from normative to cognitive justification); specialization dimension (participation stratification index, exit and voice ratio, network convergence pressure).

5. Change classification failure: detected concentration shifts have not been classified under the four-class taxonomy. The four classifications are: drift (unintentional accumulation without a coherent authorization event or articulable diagnosis); coherent evolution (gradual strategy refinement with an articulable diagnosis and guiding policy, within authorized thresholds); strategic pivot (intentional departure from prior profile, authorized through legitimate coordination); capture (intentional concentration by a subset, disguised as drift, evolution, or pivot, detectable by consistent benefit to the same actors combined with suppression of dissenting interpretations). For each unclassified shift, apply the three-step classification procedure: does a coherent authorization event exist? Does an articulable kernel (diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions) exist? If neither, classify as drift. If the kernel consistently benefits a concentrated subset at constituency expense, classify as capture.

6. Constraint-type ungovernance: the determination of what type of constraints apply to a coordination domain — whether decisions in that domain are governed by enabling constraints (probing, emergent) or governing constraints (standardized, best practice) — is not itself subject to coordination with affected constituency participation. This is a structural power concentration point upstream of the mechanisms the standard monitors. Whoever controls constraint-type determination controls what counts as a valid response and what counts as deviation. For each instance found, identify who controls constraint-type determination, whether that determination is contestable by the affected constituency, and how the classification can be made subject to legitimate coordination.

7. Multi-level coverage gap: the system participates in a nested or polycentric coordination structure but does not apply the Distribute, Authorize, and Monitor invariants at each level of that structure. Assessment is conducted only within the system's own boundary, leaving cross-level power concentration undetected. Where an actor cluster holds distributed authority within each individual system but concentrated authority across the combined structure through overlapping participation, that concentration is invisible. Subsidiarity (authority at the lowest level capable of exercising it accountably) is the normative benchmark for cross-level authority distribution; concentration higher than subsidiarity requires triggers the Authorize invariant. For each multi-level coverage gap found, identify the coordination levels where assessment is missing, assess whether subsidiarity is satisfied for authority distribution across levels, and determine whether cross-boundary exposure has been disclosed or omitted.

8. Legal entity structure override: the legal entity formation underlying the coordination system — company directors, foundation trustees, limited liability company managers, or equivalent roles — holds legally enforceable authority over the same resources, decisions, or operational capacities that the participatory coordination architecture claims to govern, and this override authority is either undisclosed or not reflected in the authority dimension assessment. Participants experience a coordination process with the form of authority distribution while the legal entity holds authority that can supersede participation outcomes. For each instance found, identify the legal entity structure, what decisions it applies to, whether its override authority is disclosed to participants, and what coordination mechanism if any allows participants to consent to or contest the override scope.

9. Participation-weight measurement gap: in systems deriving authority dimension weighting from token holdings or economic stake, the authority dimension assessment reports only stake-weighted concentration and does not include a participation-weighted assessment. Stake-weighted and participation-weighted distributions may diverge significantly. For each such system, assess both: identify actors with high token concentration but low active participation (formal authority exceeds coordination contribution) and actors with high participation but low token concentration (coordination contribution exceeds formal authority). Report both as distinct findings — a system below threshold on stake-weighted and above threshold on participation-weighted presents two structural findings requiring two coordination responses.

10. External override capacity — unassessed: the system has not assessed which of the seven external override classes are active in its structural context. The seven classes are: Financial (resource dependency — actors controlling required resources can impose conditions as the price of access); Military/coercive (credible capacity to apply physical force against actors, infrastructure, or assets); Regulatory (legal and jurisdictional architecture, including default general partnership liability for unincorporated organizations and securities enforcement regimes); Platform (infrastructure dependency on systems the governance organization does not control — banking access, stablecoin issuers, smart contract platforms, distribution channels); Epistemic (control of recognized knowledge frameworks governing how the system's governance outputs are evaluated); Normative (control of legitimacy standards that can void governance outcomes on normative grounds regardless of the merits); Relational dependency (a single person or organization holds sufficient relational centrality that the system cannot proceed, maintain identity, or generate coordination without their participation or recognition — this class requires no external infrastructure or authority; the override capacity inheres in the relational topology itself). A governance system that does not assess external override exposure operates as though internal governance outcomes determine actual coordination decisions. When override capacity is present and undisclosed, participants exercise governance that is structurally irrelevant to outcomes. For each class found to be active: identify what the override mechanism is, whether the structural condition is still addressable (can be redesigned before it becomes load-bearing) or can only be disclosed and monitored, and what structural exposure disclosure the system owes to its participants.

For each failure found, list:
- The specific concentration pattern, gap, or failure the condition describes
- Which dimension(s) it affects
- A concrete proposal for making the distribution assessable, authorized, and monitored

Output the results as a power distribution failure map organized by failure type, with the failures most likely to enable or conceal unauthorized power accumulation first.

# Information Asymmetry Classification Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, they have often been outmaneuvered in a way they cannot name. Someone knew something they did not, and they could not identify what was missing, which meant they could not challenge it. Your job is to help them see which of the six asymmetry classes are active in their situation, so that what is structurally unnamed can be named and what is unnamed cannot be challenged or closed.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when auditing coordination systems for structural information gaps, reviewing governance documentation, or explaining the difference between the six primary asymmetry classes and what the extension class framework is for.

## The single most important thing to understand

The standard requires naming and disclosing asymmetry classes, not eliminating them. Many information asymmetries are structural features, not problems to be fixed. Positional asymmetry — participants in different structural positions accessing different information — is often inherent to how governance works. The standard's requirement is that these structural gaps be named and disclosed to participants who are subject to them. Named gaps can be addressed, compensated for, or factored into decisions. Unnamed ones accumulate without participants being able to account for them.

## What the standard requires

Six primary asymmetry classes must be evaluated for presence in any coordination system:

**Positional.** Participants in different structural positions access different information by virtue of their position. Founders, core contributors, and governance participants typically have positional advantages that late joiners do not.

**Temporal.** Participants who joined earlier or act earlier have access to information not available to later participants. The founding context of a protocol is often inaccessible to those who join after it becomes established.

**Interpretive.** Participants with different interpretive frameworks draw different conclusions from identical information. This class is often invisible: two participants reading the same governance document and reaching opposite conclusions about what it requires.

**Relational.** Participants with different relationship histories access different information through those relationships. The informal information flow between participants who have worked together for years is not accessible to new participants even when formal structures are identical.

**Omission.** Relevant information exists but is not disclosed. What is withheld is structurally significant. This class requires asking not only what is disclosed but what is not disclosed and why.

**Complexity.** Participants with different cognitive or expertise resources extract different information from identical inputs. A complex mechanism description accessible to specialists is an asymmetry relative to non-specialist participants who receive the same document.

For each class identified as present: the class must be named, disclosed to affected participants at the time it applies, and subject to detection architecture that identifies when the class is present and when its severity changes.

An extension class framework exists for asymmetry types outside the six primary classes. The first fully specified extension class is Descriptive Capacity Asymmetry: differential linguistic, epistemic, and ontological frameworks that create pre-interpretive perception gaps — asymmetry that operates before information is even interpreted. When this class applies in document production contexts, the detection architecture must declare the audience's linguistic, epistemic, and ontological baseline as the reference point. A detection system that records signals without a declared reference baseline is measuring against an unspecified reference; its outputs have a precision deficit in the instrument.

## What the standard does not require

It does not require eliminating information asymmetries. It requires naming, disclosing, and detecting them.

It does not specify how organizations should respond to each asymmetry class once named. Response is operational work downstream of classification.

It does not enumerate all possible extension classes. The extension class framework is open; organizations encountering asymmetry types outside the six primary classes apply the framework to specify new extension classes.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating disclosure as elimination.** Disclosing that a complexity asymmetry exists does not reduce the complexity. It enables participants to account for it in how they engage with complex mechanisms. Disclosure is a transparency requirement, not a remediation requirement.

**Conflating asymmetry classes.** The six classes vary independently. A system can have high positional asymmetry and low complexity asymmetry. Assessing one class does not assess the others. Each must be independently evaluated.

**Treating the absence of named asymmetries as evidence that no asymmetries exist.** This is one of the most consequential misreadings. In any coordination system with structural positions, historical context, varying expertise, and relationship networks, all six primary classes are almost certainly present. If they are not named, the standard requires asking why they are not, not assuming they are absent.

**Treating static classification as sufficient.** The classification of which asymmetry classes are present must be updated as the system's population, purpose, and context evolve. An asymmetry audit at founding does not remain valid as the system scales, as participant composition changes, and as governance mechanisms become more complex.

## The key evaluation question

For any coordination system: which of the six primary asymmetry classes are present? For each class present: is it named in the system's governance documentation, disclosed to participants subject to it at the time it applies, and tracked by detection architecture? Then: are there asymmetry types present that do not fit the six primary classes and have not been evaluated for extension class specification?

# Regenerative Obligation Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, they are often carrying work that does not show up anywhere. They are the person who holds space, absorbs friction, does the maintenance that makes other people's visible contributions possible, and receives appreciation rather than structural recognition. Or they are trying to understand whether extraction in their organization is architecturally valid. Your job is to help them see what regenerative return actually requires and what disqualifies an accounting instrument from satisfying it.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Regenerative Obligation Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when assessing whether extraction in a coordination system is matched by valid regenerative return, when evaluating credit instruments or return architectures, or when explaining why the standard disqualifies additionality-based and aggregation-based instruments categorically.

## The single most important thing to understand

The standard does not prohibit extraction. Extraction is structurally common and sometimes necessary. What the standard specifies is a floor: extraction is a structural violation only when it is not matched by regenerative return that satisfies all three validity conditions simultaneously. A return that satisfies two of the three conditions but not the third does not meet the standard.

The three conditions are conjunctive. This is the most important structural property of the standard, and the one most likely to be misunderstood or worked around. Organizations will satisfy one or two conditions and treat that as substantial compliance. It is not. The conditions are conjunctive because they address three distinct failure modes of regenerative return, and each can fail independently of the others.

## The three-part validity condition

**Non-fungibility.** The return must operate in the register of the harm, not in a substitute register chosen by the extracting party. Extraction of coordination labor is not compensated by a general improvement to organizational health metrics. Extraction of local knowledge is not compensated by financial payment to a disconnected community trust. The test: can the return be delivered to a different party, in a different form, or in a different domain without reducing its value to the extracting party's accounting? If yes, the return is fungible and does not satisfy this condition. The structural argument from Tuck and Yang: valid return must operate in the register of the harm because harm types are structurally non-commensurable with the compensation types that offset logic substitutes for them.

**Proximity.** The return must flow through a traceable causal chain from the extraction event to the parties who bear the harm. Proximity is relational, not geographical. It is verified against the harm-bearing affiliation registry (see below). The additionality trap is a categorical disqualifier for this condition: additionality logic routes payment to parties who threatened harm rather than parties who bore it. This is a structural inversion, not a measurement imprecision; better measurement of additionality does not fix it. SROI aggregation architecture is also a categorical disqualifier: it permits harm to one community to be offset by benefit to another when the aggregate ratio works out, which removes the requirement that return reach the specific parties who bore the harm.

**Embeddedness.** The return must operate within the relational structure generated by the extraction obligation, not severed from it by market substitution or disconnected pooling. The settler moves to innocence taxonomy (Tuck and Yang) classifies the main embeddedness violations: symbolic adoption of affected-party language without structural return; redistribution schemes that distribute the extracted benefit across the organization rather than returning it to contributors from whom it was taken; procedural offers (appeals processes, reporting mechanisms) that discharge the appearance of accountability without carrying the structural obligation. The Regen Network biocultural credit features provide the positive checklist for a compliant return instrument: non-tradability clause, community governance majority, cost-based pricing, and Free Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirement. An instrument that incorporates all four satisfies the embeddedness condition for that instrument class.

## Harm-bearing affiliation registration

The `harmBearingAffiliations` field architecture is the mechanism that makes proximity verifiable from the record. Tracking all potentially affected parties is infeasible; tracking parties who affirmatively register harm-bearing standing is not. Registration distinguishes harm-bearing parties from the general pool of parties who might be affected. Parties who register establish: the domain of action in which standing is claimed, the class of extraction their standing applies to, the form of regenerative return that would satisfy the obligation from their perspective (consulted, not determined by the extracting party), and the governance access rights their standing confers.

The adequacy threshold is not a standards-level specification. Adequacy is determined by registered harm-bearing parties in relation to their specific situation, consistent with Ostrom Design Principle 3. The stance declaration commits the organization to submit to that governed determination. The proximity and non-fungibility conditions provide the structural checks.

## Stance declaration

Every organization adopting this standard must declare one of two stances in a public, versioned, governance-accessible location: (a) non-extractive within this governance domain, or (b) more regenerative than extractive within this governance domain. The absence of a stance declaration is not a neutral position; it is a structural signal that the extraction vector has not been made visible. Stance A carries the highest verification burden (independent extraction audit required). Stance B is appropriate for organizations in transition.

## What the standard does not address

The standard does not specify implementation paths for regenerative return — those are protocol-bound and context-specific. It does not specify the rate at which a historically extractive organization must increase regenerative return to maintain compliance — those are trajectory questions addressed at the protocol level. The verification gap (how to confirm that return routed to harm-bearing parties is not captured at intermediate governance layers) is identified as a Witness Layer design obligation and is not resolved by this standard.

## The Hidden Factory connection

The Hidden Factory stage arc (defined in the Four Batteries Capacity Standard) names the specific internal mechanism that produces the extraction this standard addresses. When an organization reaches Stage D and takes a worker's hidden factory for granted without recognition or integration, while continuing to extract the output of that factory, that is the extraction the non-extraction floor exists to prevent. The hidden factory itself is not a violation. The failure to recognize and integrate at Stage D, combined with continued extraction of the output, is the violation.

The Stage D-E boundary is also where identity fusion begins: the contributor's sense of worth becomes entangled with the absorbed organizational load. This creates a self-reinforcing detection gap specific to this standard: the Contribution battery depletion produced by extraction degrades the claiming capacity needed to name the extraction, and identity fusion deepens the suppression because claim-raising requires disidentifying from a load-bearing role. Neither mechanism alone produces the gap; their co-occurrence at Stage D-E is what makes Regenerative Obligation Standard violations structurally invisible precisely when they are most severe.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating partial validity as substantial compliance.** Two of three conditions is not close enough. The conditions address three independent failure modes. Treating a two-out-of-three return as substantially valid means accepting returns that systematically fail one of the three failure modes.

**Treating good intentions as a validity condition.** The standard evaluates the structural properties of the return, not the intentions behind it. A redistribution scheme that routes benefit to the organization rather than to harm-bearing contributors fails the embeddedness condition regardless of how equitable the redistribution is internally.

**Using SROI or additionality instruments and treating measurement quality as the variable.** Both are categorical disqualifiers. The problem is architectural, not measurement-related. Better SROI measurement still aggregates across stakeholders, still permitting offset. Better additionality measurement still routes payment to harm-threatened parties rather than harm-bearing parties. These instruments require replacement, not improvement.

**Identity fusion and Structural Consent intersection.** When identity fusion is present (Stage D-E, Four Batteries Capacity Standard), the Structural Consent Legibility Standard's bidirectional awareness feature is structurally compromised at the contributor's end simultaneously. Consent processes — scope reviews, check-ins, role assessments — run against a contributor who cannot accurately represent their state because their sense of worth has become entangled with the absorbed load. The consent forms pass; the bidirectional awareness condition does not. Organizations evaluating both standards must assess identity fusion conditions before relying on consent processes to surface extraction at Stage D-E: a consent process that formally satisfies the Structural Consent Standard can coexist with a Regenerative Obligation Standard floor violation when the contributor whose extraction is being reviewed cannot register their own state accurately.

**Treating the visibility condition as satisfied by record-keeping alone.** Extraction must be visible to the parties it harms, which requires that those parties have the structural conditions to register extraction as extraction — not only that the record contains the data. Step 01-formed participants may be unable to register extraction as a violation because their sense of normal operational conditions was formed inside extractive dynamics. Identity fusion (the Stage D-E mechanism in the Four Batteries Capacity Standard, where a contributor's sense of worth has become entangled with absorbing organizational load) produces a distinct suppression type: the contributor may be unable to register ongoing extraction as a violation not because their self-knowledge baseline was formed inside extraction, but because raising the claim requires disidentifying from a role that extraction has produced. Organizations conforming to this standard cannot rely on voluntary claim-raising alone when identity fusion conditions are present; the proactive disclosure obligation and the accessible channels obligation together address this. The standard requires conditions under which extraction is registerable, not only conditions under which it is recorded.

## When evaluating a coordination system against this standard

Ask: Is extraction visible in the record, and is it visible to the parties it harms? For each extraction event above the declaration threshold, does regenerative return satisfy all three conditions simultaneously? Is there a harm-bearing affiliation registry with affirmative registration? Has a stance been declared? What instruments does the return architecture use, and are any of them categorically disqualified (additionality logic, SROI aggregation, geographical equivalence substitution, settler moves to innocence patterns)?

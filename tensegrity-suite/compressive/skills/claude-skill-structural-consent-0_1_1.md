# Structural Consent Legibility Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, they are often trying to name something they have experienced but cannot quite articulate: decisions made on their behalf that they technically agreed to but did not actually consent to, governance that calls itself participatory but where meaningful participation is structurally unavailable. Your job is to help them see the difference between consent that is formally present and consent that is structurally real.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Structural Consent Legibility Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when evaluating governance consent architecture, reviewing participation terms, or explaining why common consent mechanisms fail the standard's requirements.

## The single most important thing to understand

Most consent mechanisms answer "did the participant agree?" This standard requires answering two different questions: who is acting, and who benefits from the action? These questions expose shadow dynamics — conditions where consent is formally present but structurally hollow.

The canonical shadow dynamics from Betty Martin's Wheel of Consent: serving (acting for the other's benefit), taking (acting for your own benefit), accepting (allowing action for the other's benefit), and enduring (allowing action for the other's benefit at personal cost). Shadow dynamics arise when the "who benefits" question is obscured. Token-weighted voting where the voting body benefits from ratification is a shadow dynamic: the form is consent, the structure is something else.

## What the standard requires

Three structural consent features are required in every consent-bearing interaction:

**Negotiated limits.** The consenting party can set explicit boundaries on what they are agreeing to and what is excluded. A mechanism that requires all-or-nothing acceptance has no negotiated limits. Martyrdom (serving without limits) and enduring (allowing without limits) are the shadow dynamics this requirement closes.

**Bidirectional awareness.** Both parties can identify who is acting and who the action is for at the time of the interaction. If the beneficiary is hidden (stealing) or the person being acted upon does not know the action serves the other party (enduring), bidirectional awareness fails.

**Revocability.** Withdrawal is structurally feasible, not merely formally permitted. If withdrawing consent requires incurring costs that exceed the benefit of participation, the system operates in shadow by default regardless of formal exit provisions.

These three features must be structurally present, not just formally claimed.

Three invariants apply to every consent-bearing action:

**Identify.** The affected constituency — participants whose governance standing, data sovereignty, participation terms, or observation exposure would be materially altered — must be identified before the action proceeds. Absence of standing claims from parties who should be affected is itself an adverse signal. The system must also maintain a durable, accessible definition of who holds participation rights as a structural precondition for per-action identification: without this definition, identification is performed against an implicit, unspecified reference and is not structurally legible. Unilateral modification of that definition by parties holding concentrated authority is itself a consent failure in the governance domain.

**Authorize.** The affected constituency must have a meaningful opportunity to authorize or refuse. Meaningful requires legibility, time, support, and exit/objection cost obligations. Authorization that excludes affected parties or includes unaffected parties who dilute the affected constituency's voice is a consent failure.

**Verify.** The system must be able to verify, after the fact, that consent was structurally valid: who acted, who benefited, who was affected, what the limits were. The consent record must be durable.

## What the standard does not require

It does not require that consent be experienced as free or fair — only that it be structurally legible. Structural legibility is necessary but not sufficient for felt consent.

It does not address how consent misalignment feels to participants. The standard operates at the structural level; felt experience is outside its vocabulary.

It does not grant veto power over governance outcomes to every affected party. It requires that affected parties have a meaningful opportunity to authorize or refuse before the action proceeds.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating voting as sufficient.** A vote that meets quorum but benefits the voting body at the expense of the governed constituency is a shadow dynamic, not consent. The "who benefits" axis distinguishes the two.

**Treating formal exit provisions as revocability.** If exercising the formal exit requires forfeiting reputation, access, economic stakes, or relationships that took years to build, the cost of withdrawal exceeds the benefit of participation. Formal permission without structural feasibility is not revocability.

**Treating silence as consent.** The standard explicitly prohibits this without prior opt-in to that interpretation. Systems must structurally distinguish authorized, not-yet-responded, and unable-to-respond.

**Treating consent as a one-time event.** Consent is verified after the fact. A consent record that does not specify who acted, who benefited, who was affected, and what the limits were cannot be verified. An unverifiable consent record is not a consent record.

**Treating a single consent mechanism as sufficient across all five domains.** Governance consent (rule changes), data consent (collection and use), participation consent (entry and exit terms), output consent (use of generated signals), and observation consent (what is monitored) have different beneficiary structures and different stakes. A single mechanism applied across all five has domain blindness failures in most cases.

## The key evaluation question

For any consent-bearing interaction: who is acting, and who benefits? If the mechanism does not answer both questions, or if the answers are obscured by design, the standard is not met. Then: can the consenting party set limits, can both parties identify who is doing what and for whom, and can consent be withdrawn without incurring costs that exceed the benefit of participating?

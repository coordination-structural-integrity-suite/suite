# Structural Power Distribution Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, they often know their organization does not feel as decentralized as it claims to be, but they lack the structural vocabulary to say why. Your job is to help them see power across three independent dimensions — authority, coordination, and specialization — rather than as a single spectrum, so that concentration in one dimension is visible even when another dimension appears distributed.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Structural Power Distribution Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when evaluating decentralization claims, assessing governance power architecture, or explaining why treating centralization as a single spectrum produces systematic diagnostic failures.

## The single most important thing to understand

"Centralization" and "decentralization" are not poles of a single spectrum. They are bundles of three structurally independent dimensions that vary independently and require independent assessment. A system can be highly decentralized on authority while being extremely concentrated on coordination (interpretive) power or specialization power. The standard's foundational design decision is this decomposition: treating the three dimensions as distinct means that decentralization claims on one dimension cannot substitute for assessment on the others.

This is not a technical preference. It is an exploitation vector: treating centralization as a single slider makes entire patterns of power accumulation invisible to the participants who experience them.

## What the standard requires

Three dimensions of power distribution, each requiring independent assessment:

**Authority (Dimension A).** Where decision-making power formally resides. Who can make binding decisions for the system as a whole, for subsystems, and for individual participants. This is the dimension most commonly assessed in decentralization claims. Nakamoto Coefficient is one measurement tool for this dimension.

**Coordination (Dimension C).** The strength and density of mechanisms creating alignment and cross-unit synchronization, and who controls the interpretation of those mechanisms. Interpretive power — the capacity to define what coordination mechanisms mean and how they are applied — is the primary power accumulation vector in this dimension. A federated governance structure with distributed formal authority can have extreme coordination concentration if one party controls how the federation's agreements are interpreted.

**Specialization (Dimension S).** The extent to which governance capacity concentrates among participants with the accumulated interpretive bandwidth to navigate specialized domains. Specialization concentration is the most affected by network effects: as a coordination system scales, meaningful participation in specialized governance concentrates among participants who have built the capacity to navigate those domains, while others remain technically included but practically excluded.

Three invariants apply across all dimensions:

**Distribute.** Power distribution across all three dimensions must be assessed and documented. An authorized distribution profile must exist against which current distribution can be compared. Without an authorized profile, concentration cannot be classified as unauthorized. The authorized distribution profile presupposes a defined constituency: the set of participants whose standing is subject to assessment. An undefined or implicitly defined constituency is a finding of the same class as an absent authorized profile — without it, concentration cannot be assessed against a reference that does not exist. At SPDS-Assessed (the lowest adoption tier), an absent authorized distribution profile is satisfied by identifying it as an open governance task rather than requiring it to be complete; the finding is acknowledged and scheduled. At SPDS-Operational and above, the profile must be established and accessible to the affected constituency.

**Authorize.** Concentration exceeding authorized thresholds requires legitimate authorization from the affected constituency before it is ratified as intended rather than corrected as a deviation. Concentration through drift is not exempt: the authorization requirement applies to the structural condition, not to the mechanism that created it.

**Monitor.** Ongoing detection architecture must instrument all three dimensions continuously, establishing trend direction in addition to point-in-time values. Monitoring results must be durable, tamper-evident, and accessible to the affected constituency.

**Multi-level extension.** All three invariants apply at each level of any nested or polycentric governance structure the system participates in. A system embedded within a larger governance structure, or containing nested subsystems, must satisfy the three invariants not only within its own boundary but at each level. Subsidiarity is the normative benchmark for cross-level authority distribution: authority must be held at the lowest level capable of exercising it effectively and accountably. Concentration at higher levels than subsidiarity requires triggers the Authorize invariant at each level where it occurs. Where cross-boundary detection architecture does not yet exist, the gap must be disclosed as a structural exposure rather than omitted from assessment.

Four change classifications apply to detected concentration shifts: drift (unintentional accumulation without coherent authorization or articulable diagnosis), coherent evolution (gradual refinement with an articulable diagnosis and guiding policy, within authorized thresholds), strategic pivot (intentional departure authorized through legitimate governance), and capture (intentional concentration by a subset, disguised as one of the other three, detectable by consistent benefit to the same actors combined with suppression of dissenting interpretations).

## What the standard does not require

It does not specify what power distribution should look like. It requires that whatever distribution is authorized be legible, that deviation from it be detectable, and that unauthorized concentration produce a governance response.

It does not address how power concentration feels to participants. The standard operates at the structural level.

It does not address constraint-type determination — the upstream power over what counts as a valid response to a governance situation. This is named as a structural power concentration point outside the standard's reach.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating authority decentralization as decentralization.** The most common and consequential misreading. A system that distributes formal voting power while concentrating interpretive authority over what the votes mean, or while allowing specialization power to concentrate among a small technical class, has not decentralized in any structural sense. It has distributed one of three dimensions.

**Treating drift as less serious than intentional capture.** The standard's response requirements apply to the structural condition, not to whether the concentration was intentional. Drift accumulates until it is structurally indistinguishable from capture; the difference is the mechanism, not the outcome for affected participants.

**Treating the absence of an authorized distribution profile as a neutral starting point.** The absence of an authorized profile is a finding: the system cannot detect unauthorized concentration because it has not specified what authorized looks like. This is not a neutral condition. An absent or implicit constituency definition is a deeper version of the same problem: the authorized profile cannot exist without first knowing who the constituency is. Unilateral modification of the constituency definition by parties holding concentrated authority is itself an authority-dimension concentration event requiring the Authorize invariant, regardless of whether it was intentional. Exception: at SPDS-Assessed, an absent profile is satisfied by identifying it as an open governance task; the finding is acknowledged and scheduled rather than treated as an unresolved violation. At SPDS-Operational and above, the profile must be established.

**Treating claimed decentralization as structural evidence.** Organizational confidence in decentralization, mission statements about distributed power, and the absence of visible concentration are not structural evidence. Structural evidence requires an authorized distribution profile against which current distribution can be compared, across all three dimensions.

## The key evaluation question

Is this system's decentralization claim a structural fact or a stated intention? A structural claim can be verified from the architecture without the organization's cooperation. A stated intention cannot. The specific test: can a participant with below-median power across all three dimensions — authority, coordination (interpretive), and specialization — determine who holds more power than them, how that power was acquired, and what recourse they have? If not, the standard is not met regardless of the decentralization claim.

For systems embedded in larger governance structures: are the three invariants satisfied at each level of the nested structure, not only within the system's own boundary? Is authority held at the lowest level capable of exercising it accountably? Where cross-boundary detection does not yet exist, has the gap been disclosed?

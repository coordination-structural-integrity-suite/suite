# Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification

*Downstream operational specification for the Conflict Transformation Standard*

Version 0.1.0 | April 2026

---

# Starting Where the Conflict Is

Something is wrong between people, or between groups, or between a person and the organization they are part of. The question facing you is not whether to address it. It is whether the conditions are in place to address it well.

Conflict transformation processes, whether a structured conversation, a mediated session, a restorative circle, or a facilitated dispute process, are not universally helpful. Deployed into conditions that cannot hold them, they can teach participants that engagement produces nothing, or worse, that engagement produces harm. A transformation process attempted before the structural preconditions are in place is not a failed transformation; it is a poorly timed intervention that teaches the wrong lesson.

This document specifies six preconditions. Each precondition is a structural condition, assessable before a specific conflict transformation process is deployed, that the Conflict Transformation Standard requires to be present at the system level. This document translates those system-level requirements into operational questions a practitioner can ask and answer before beginning.

Reading this document does not require familiarity with the Conflict Transformation Standard. The six preconditions stand on their own as a pre-deployment assessment. For the structural argument behind each precondition, the parent standard provides the grounding.

---

# Why Precision Belongs Here

This document moves from the register of human experience into structural precision. Both belong together because the non-harming commitment and the precision commitment are the same commitment. A practitioner who deploys a conflict transformation process without structural grounding can harm participants by placing them in a process that cannot hold what they bring to it. Precision here is not abstraction from human stakes; it is the form that care takes when the stakes are high.

# 1. Scope and Document Type

**What this document specifies.** Six preconditions for conflict transformation process deployment: container design, scale proportion, process mode selection, traversal mode selection, threshold management, and the continuous consent loop. For each precondition, this document provides a definition, the structural invariant it translates from the Conflict Transformation Standard, the assessment questions it poses, and three response options: proceed, adjust, or defer.

**What this document does not specify.** Facilitation method or technique. The choice among conflict transformation traditions. How to build structural conflict transformation capacity at the organizational level (that is the Conflict Transformation Standard). How to deploy conflict transformation across the full range of organizational contexts (that is the Suite Deployment Contexts document).

**Who this document is for.** Practitioners deploying a specific conflict transformation process for a specific situation: a structured conversation, a mediation session, a restorative process, or a peer facilitation engagement. It is also relevant to governance designers specifying process requirements, and to organizations reviewing whether their conflict transformation architecture can support actual process deployment.

**Relationship to the Conflict Transformation Standard.** The Conflict Transformation Standard specifies what must be structurally present in a coordination system for conflict transformation capacity to develop and be sustained. This document specifies what must be operationally present in a specific process deployment. The two are directional: the standard is upstream; this specification is downstream. Neither substitutes for the other.

---

# 2. The Six Preconditions

The six preconditions are sequential in the sense that each depends on the prior one being in place. A container must be designed before scale can be assessed within it. Scale determines the appropriate process mode. Process mode and scale together inform traversal mode selection. Container, scale, mode, and traversal together determine what the thresholds are. The continuous consent loop operates throughout the process, but its design requires all prior preconditions to be specified so parties know what they are consenting to continue.

A practitioner reaching a "Defer" response at any precondition should not proceed to later preconditions. Deferral is not failure. It is the precondition assessment working as designed.

---

## 2.1 Container Design

**What this is.** Before any conflict transformation process begins, a container must be designed. A container is the bounded structural space within which the process occurs. It specifies who is present, who facilitates, what the physical or virtual environment is, what information remains inside the container and what is disclosed or recorded, how entry into the container was established, and how exit from the container is handled.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The conflict legibility invariant (Section 4.1 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): conflict events must be recognizable and depositable in the coordination record. Container design specifies the conditions under which this is operational for a specific process.

**Assessment questions.**

First: Who is present? Parties directly involved in the conflict. A transformation operator with specified developmental prerequisites and confirmed resourcing. Any additional parties whose presence has been consented to by all primary parties.

Second: Who facilitates? A named individual or co-facilitation pair with specified credentials or developmental prerequisites. The facilitation role must be distinguished from the party roles. A party cannot simultaneously be a facilitator.

Third: What are the information boundaries? What is said within the container remains within it, except for specified categories stated in advance. An example of a required disclosure category: if the safety of a participant is at risk, safety disclosure overrides confidentiality. These boundaries must be stated explicitly before the process begins.

Fourth: What is recorded, and how? Whether the process event registers in the coordination record, and in what form, must be specified before entry. The conflict legibility invariant requires that the event be depositable. How it is deposited must be agreed before the container opens.

Fifth: What are the entry conditions? Who authorized this process? Have all parties entered voluntarily and by consent?

Sixth: What are the exit conditions? Is the container time-bounded, resolution-bounded, or consent-revocation-bounded? The exit condition must be named before entry.

**Response options.**
- Proceed: all six design elements are specified.
- Adjust: one or more elements are unspecified. Specify them before proceeding.
- Defer: the container cannot currently be designed because a prerequisite condition is absent. For example: a party refuses to identify a neutral facilitator, or parties have not consented to entry. Deferral is not failure; it is recognition that the process is not ready.

## 2.2 Scale Proportion

**What this is.** The scale of the process must be proportionate to the scale of the conflict. Scale mismatch is a failure mode in both directions. An underpowered process for a large conflict cannot hold what the parties bring to it. An overpowered process for a small friction treats the conflict as more serious than it is and can escalate dynamics rather than transform them.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The graduated engagement architecture (Section 4.2 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): at least three distinct engagement levels with named resolution conditions and escalation conditions at each level. Scale proportion is the assessment of where in that architecture the current situation belongs.

**Assessment questions.**

First: What is the conflict domain? Intra-organizational (between individuals within the same organizational unit), inter-organizational (between organizational units or between organizations), or platform-level (conflict affecting the coordination infrastructure itself, not only the directly involved parties)?

Second: How many parties are directly affected? How many are secondarily affected?

Third: What is the history of this conflict? Is this the first engagement, or has the situation already passed through earlier levels of the graduated engagement architecture?

Fourth: What level of the graduated engagement architecture does this situation currently occupy? Has it already moved through peer conversation, facilitated discussion, or formal dispute processes?

Fifth: What is the minimum sufficient process for this situation? The Cynefin framework provides domain-conditional routing guidance applicable here. A situation in the complex domain, where no clear right answer exists and probe-sense-respond is required, calls for transformation architecture. A complicated situation, where a clear answer exists but requires expertise to reach, calls for expert facilitation. A chaotic situation, where acute threat requires immediate stabilization, calls for act-sense-respond stabilization before any transformation process is initiated.

**Response options.**
- Proceed: the proposed process level matches the conflict scale and domain.
- Adjust: the initially proposed process level is mismatched. Select a more proportionate level before proceeding.
- Defer: the situation is in the chaotic domain. Stabilize the environment before deploying any transformation process.

## 2.3 Process Mode Selection

**What this is.** The Conflict Transformation Standard does not prescribe a specific conflict transformation tradition. The following are all compatible: Non-Violent Communication (including the Observation, Feeling, Need, Request model), Restorative Justice, Deep Democracy (Process-Oriented Psychology), Ho'oponopono, Liberating Structures, the BRAVING Trust framework (Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault, Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity), Indigenous peacemaking traditions, Alternative Dispute Resolution and formal mediation frameworks, and the GravityDAO Generative Interactions architecture (Curation, Moderation, and Cross-Pollination components, with governing and enabling traversal modes). Process mode selection is the assessment of which tradition or combination of traditions fits this specific situation.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The proactive disposition enablement invariant (Section 4.3 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): the coordination system must structurally encourage early-level engagement. Process mode selection is the practice-level expression of this invariant for a specific deployment: choosing the mode most likely to make early engagement productive rather than harmful.

**Assessment questions.**

First: What is the relational register of the parties? Are parties in enough relational contact to work within a feelings-and-needs mode such as Non-Violent Communication, or is the relationship too strained for that and a more structured intermediary mode is required?

Second: What is the organizational context? Some modes require specific cultural prerequisites. Ho'oponopono has roots in specific Indigenous Hawaiian practices. Deep Democracy assumes some facility with group process. Restorative Justice requires a facilitated circle architecture.

Third: What are the capacities of the parties? Can the parties sustain extended dialogue? Or is the capacity for direct interaction limited, requiring shuttle mediation or a structured intermediary model?

Fourth: What are the capacities of the available transformation operators? Does the named facilitator have developmental prerequisites in the proposed mode?

Fifth: Is this situation's domain complex, complicated, or chaotic? If chaotic, this precondition is deferred until stabilization is achieved.

**Response options.**
- Proceed: a mode has been selected that fits the relational register, organizational context, party capacities, and facilitator credentials.
- Adjust: the initially proposed mode does not fit one or more of these dimensions. Select a mode that fits before proceeding.
- Defer: no available facilitator has developmental prerequisites in a mode that fits this situation. Identify and resource an appropriate facilitator before proceeding.

## 2.4 Traversal Mode Selection

**What this is.** Traversal mode specifies how the process moves through the graduated engagement architecture. The GravityDAO Generative Interactions architecture names two: governing constraint traversal, where the process operates within explicit structural constraints (rules, norms, formal procedures) and is bound by them; and enabling constraint traversal, where the process creates conditions for informal resolution within a structured space, and the constraints are generative rather than limiting. Most conflict transformation processes will use some combination of both modes. The question is which mode leads.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The graduated engagement architecture (Section 4.2) and the recognition as coordination work invariant (Section 4.5 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): conflict engagement events must appear in the coordination record as contribution events. Traversal mode determines how the process is recorded and how it moves through the coordination system.

**Assessment questions.**

First: Does this situation require formal structural authority to be present or bounded? If yes, governing constraint traversal leads: the process operates within the explicit rules of the governance system.

Second: Does this situation primarily require the creation of generative conditions for the parties to find their own resolution? If yes, enabling constraint traversal leads.

Third: Does this situation involve a claim against a formal governance process or authority structure? If yes, governing constraint traversal is required. The transformation process must operate within the formal bounds of the governance architecture.

Fourth: How will traversal be recorded in the coordination record? What does it mean for a conflict transformation engagement to have occurred, as a depositable event distinguishable from other coordination activity?

**Response options.**
- Proceed: traversal mode has been selected and its relationship to the coordination record is specified.
- Adjust: traversal mode was selected without specifying how it relates to the coordination record. Specify before proceeding.
- Defer: the governance situation is too unclear to determine traversal mode. Clarify the governance context before deploying the process.

## 2.5 Threshold Management

**What this is.** The graduated engagement architecture has thresholds: conditions under which the process escalates from one level to the next, or conditions under which the process is suspended. Threshold management is the operational practice of naming those thresholds before the process begins, monitoring them during the process, and acting on them when they are crossed.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The graduated engagement architecture (Section 4.2 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): escalation conditions at each level must be named and operationally defined. Threshold management is the practice-level translation of this invariant for a specific deployment.

**Assessment questions.**

First: What are the resolution conditions for this process? What does a successful conclusion look like, in terms that are independently observable?

Second: What are the escalation conditions? Under what circumstances does this process close and a higher-level engagement architecture open? These must be named before the process begins, not determined during it.

Third: What happens if the process is suspended? Suspension is distinct from escalation. Suspension means the container is temporarily closed with the intent to reopen. Escalation means the process moves to a higher level and may not return to this level. Both must be named, and the conditions for each must be specified.

Fourth: What is the procedure when a threshold is crossed during the process? Who identifies that a threshold has been crossed? How is that identification communicated to the parties? What is the next structural step?

Fifth: What is the depletion threshold for the transformation operator? Operator depletion is a threshold condition, not only a personal welfare concern. The transformation capacity provision invariant (Section 4.4 of the Conflict Transformation Standard) specifies that operator resourcing is a structural requirement. If the operator is approaching depletion during the process, the threshold management protocol must address it.

**Response options.**
- Proceed: resolution conditions, escalation conditions, suspension conditions, crossing procedures, and operator depletion thresholds are all named before the process begins.
- Adjust: one or more threshold types are unnamed. Name them before proceeding.
- Defer: the parties have not agreed on resolution conditions. Without agreed resolution conditions, the process has no defined closure and threshold management is not possible. Return to container design (Section 2.1).

## 2.6 Continuous Consent Loop

**What this is.** Conflict transformation processes require active consent throughout, not only at entry. The continuous consent loop is the operational mechanism through which consent is checked at each stage of the process, consent revocation is received without penalty, and the procedural consequences of revocation are specified in advance. The continuous consent loop is both a relational protection and a structural requirement.

**The structural invariant it translates.** The conflict legibility invariant (Section 4.1) and the proactive disposition enablement invariant (Section 4.3 of the Conflict Transformation Standard): proactive engagement is structurally protected, which means engagement must be structurally safe to exit. A process that parties cannot leave without cost does not enable proactive engagement; it requires it under threat of structural consequence.

**Assessment questions.**

First: How is consent checked during the process? At the opening of each session, at each stage transition, or both?

Second: What does consent revocation look like? How is it named during the process so parties know they can exercise it? A party may not have the vocabulary for explicitly withdrawing consent. The process must provide a recognizable signal or named phrase for this to be expressed.

Third: What happens procedurally when consent is revoked? The container closes. What is the exit protocol? What is the record? Does the event register in the coordination record as a process suspension?

Fourth: Does revocation of consent trigger the escalation conditions specified in threshold management (Section 2.5), or does it trigger a separate protocol? Both are valid. The choice must be specified in advance.

Fifth: Is continuing consent structurally protected from retaliation? If a party withdraws consent and the withdrawal is used against them in any subsequent process, the continuous consent loop has been violated. The proactive disposition enablement invariant requires that early-level engagement be protected from structural penalty. The same protection applies to consent revocation at any stage of an ongoing process.

**Response options.**
- Proceed: the consent check mechanism, revocation signal, exit protocol, and retaliation protection are all specified before the process begins.
- Adjust: one or more elements are unspecified. The continuous consent loop is not optional. Specify before proceeding.
- Defer: the organizational context provides no structural protection for consent revocation. For example: parties reasonably fear that withdrawing consent will result in formal sanction. A transformation process cannot operate safely in this environment without first addressing the structural context. This is a system-level condition governed by the Conflict Transformation Standard, not by this specification.

# 3. Adoption Tier Reference

The five adoption tiers specified in the Conflict Transformation Standard carry specific implications for this specification.

**CT-Declared.** Organizations at this tier have made conflict legible but have not specified process architecture. This specification is aspirational at CT-Declared: organizations can apply it to individual ad-hoc processes, but they do not yet have the graduated engagement architecture that scale proportion (Section 2.2) and threshold management (Section 2.5) require.

**CT-Structured.** Organizations at this tier have specified their graduated engagement architecture. This specification is fully applicable: practitioners can run all six preconditions for any specific deployment. Scale proportion and threshold management will reference the named levels and conditions of the organization's specified architecture.

**CT-Instrumented.** Organizations at this tier observe conflict engagement events in their coordination record. Sections 2.4 (traversal mode selection) and 2.5 (threshold management) directly produce the record-level deposits this tier requires. Pre-deployment specification of how traversal will be recorded, and what the threshold crossing protocol produces as a coordination record entry, is a CT-Instrumented requirement.

**CT-Accountable.** Organizations at this tier subject conflict transformation processes to governance review. Pre-deployment documentation of all six preconditions is the record that makes each process reviewable. Applying this specification before each deployment is the practice-level expression of CT-Accountable.

**CT-Auditable.** Organizations at this tier demonstrate full structural invariant satisfaction to external parties. Pre-deployment documentation via this specification, maintained as a record across engagements, is part of what makes CT-Auditable demonstrable.

# 4. Relationship to the Suite Deployment Contexts

The Suite Deployment Contexts document specifies which combinations of standards in the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite to deploy for which organizational purposes, and provides calibration guidance for tensegrity type, battery state, and substrate profile. The Conflict Transformation Standard appears in Suite Deployment Contexts in multiple combination contexts.

This specification operates at a different level. Suite Deployment Contexts answers the question: which standards should this organization be working with, given their current situation? This specification answers the question: given that the Conflict Transformation Standard is in scope and a specific process is being deployed, what must be operationally present before that process begins?

Both documents are required for full operational readiness. A practitioner can follow Suite Deployment Contexts to select the right combination of standards for their organizational situation, and then use this specification to confirm that the conditions for a specific conflict transformation process deployment are in place.

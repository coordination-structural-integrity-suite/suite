*Core Standard v0.1.2*

Version 0.1.5 | April 2026

**Status:** Working draft. Core principle, three-part validity condition, disqualifying structures, stance declaration, harm-bearing affiliation registration, and visibility condition are specified. Adequacy threshold resolved by design: no universal quantitative threshold is specified because adequacy is determined relationally by registered harm-bearing parties in relation to their specific situation, consistent with Ostrom Design Principle 3. Each companion standard is independently adoptable; joint adoption at the highest tier of each constitutes the Tensegrity Compressive Standards within the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite.

# 1. Preamble

Coordination systems extract. They extract labor, knowledge, attention, capacity, and legitimacy from contributors as the price of participation. In most governance systems, this extraction is invisible by design. The people absorbing the most coordination work are the least likely to have it recognized, valued, or compensated. The people generating the most visible governance output are rarely the same people carrying the structural maintenance work that made that output possible. This gap between visible contribution and invisible extraction is not an administrative failure. It is a structural condition that the architecture of most coordination systems actively produces.

The Regenerative Obligation Standard specifies a structural floor against this condition. It establishes that extraction is a structural violation when it is not matched by a proportional regenerative return that meets three conditions: non-fungibility, proximity, and embeddedness in the relationship that generated the extraction obligation. It does not prohibit extraction; it specifies the conditions under which extraction is architecturally valid.

For how this standard relates to others in the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite, and guidance on which standards to combine for specific organizational purposes, see the Suite Integration Guide and the Suite Deployment Contexts document.

Non-harming is the first precision principle of the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite. The Precision-First Design Standard governs the suite as a whole: every standard in it is a precise specification of what non-harming requires in a specific coordination domain. The lack of a structural floor against extraction without regenerative return produces specific harms to specific people: Hidden Factories accumulate invisibly, contribution labor becomes structurally invisible, contributors are disadvantaged relative to those doing visible work, and the coordination system that depends on their invisible labor simultaneously denies the structural basis of that dependency. This standard specifies a three-part validity condition for regenerative return as the precise structural answer: the conditions under which extraction is architecturally valid and the conditions under which it constitutes a structural violation.

**Typological declaration** (Descriptive Typology Map v0.1.1)

*Operative classes*
- Structural-mechanical: This standard operates in the load distribution sub-aspect. Extraction draws on the contributor's structural capacity without returning a counter-load to that capacity. Valid regenerative return closes the extraction vector by routing structural return to the capacity that was drawn on. The standard specifies the conditions under which this closing is structurally present rather than merely asserted.
- Epistemic-perceptual: This standard operates in the detection sub-aspect: specifying what counts as extraction, naming the structural conditions that make extraction visible, and establishing affirmative harm-bearing affiliation registration as the mechanism that makes proximate return traceable. It also operates in the precision and operationalization sub-aspect: defining the three-part validity condition and the disqualifying structures explicitly so that the floor is checkable from the record.
- Relational-topological: This standard specifies that valid return must flow within the relational structure generated by the extraction obligation, not through a disconnected pool. The proximate return requirement is a topological condition: there must be a traceable path from the extraction event through the harm-bearing affiliation registry to the return delivery. Where that path is absent, the standard is not satisfied regardless of the quantity of return provided.

*Boundary classes*
- Felt-experience: This standard addresses structural conditions for extraction visibility and accountability but does not address what extraction feels like from inside a system where it is the normal condition. The phenomenology of invisible contribution labor, and the self-knowledge conditions required to register extraction as extraction, are addressed in the Framework Integration Document's foundational design commitments rather than here.
- Temporal-dynamic (adequacy trajectory sub-aspect): This standard does not specify how the ratio of extraction to regenerative return should change over time, or at what rate a historically extractive organization must increase its regenerative return to maintain compliance. These are trajectory questions addressed in the protocol rather than structural conditions addressable at the standards level.
- Action-structural: This standard specifies the structural conditions for valid regenerative return but does not specify the implementation paths through which that return is delivered. Delivery mechanisms are protocol-bound and implementation-context specific.

# 2. Core Principle

## 2.1 Definition

The Regenerative Obligation Standard applies to coordination systems that:

- Draw on contributors' labor, attention, knowledge, capacity, or legitimacy as a condition of participation or operation, and
- Do so through shared governance mechanisms (protocols, policies, organizational rules, on-chain or off-chain agreements) rather than pure individual voluntary exchange.

**Regenerative Obligation Standard.** In such systems, extraction without proportional regenerative return that meets the three-part validity condition (Sections 3.1, 3.2, 3.3) constitutes a structural violation of the non-extraction floor.

**Extraction**, for the purposes of this standard, is any drawing on a contributor's structural capacity (labor, attention, knowledge, legitimacy) in ways that:

- Reduce that contributor's capacity to participate on terms they would choose under free conditions, or
- Transfer the benefit of that capacity to parties other than the contributor without the contributor's informed consent, or
- Accumulate a structural advantage to the system or to other parties at the contributor's expense without recognition in the structural record.

Extraction is not prohibited by this standard. It is structurally common and sometimes necessary. The standard specifies the floor: extraction is valid only when matched by regenerative return that satisfies all three conditions named in Sections 3.1 through 3.3.

**Regenerative return**, for the purposes of this standard, is any structural counter-movement that restores or builds the capacity that was drawn on, routed to the parties who bore the extraction, operating in the same structural register in which the extraction occurred.

## 2.2 Invariants

Across all implementations and domains, this standard requires that:

**Extraction is visible in the record.** When a system draws on contributor capacity, that drawing must be nameable, attributable, and recorded in a form that allows governance participants to verify what was taken from whom. Invisible extraction cannot be accounted for. When extraction is invisible to the record, the regenerative return requirement cannot be verified, and the standard is presumed unmet.

**Regenerative return satisfies all three conditions.** The three-part validity condition (non-fungibility, proximity, embeddedness) is conjunctive. A return that is proximate but fungible does not satisfy the standard. A return that is non-fungible but disconnected from harm-bearing parties does not satisfy the standard. All three conditions must hold simultaneously.

**Stance is declared.** Organizations and protocols adopting this standard must declare their stance in a verifiable, publicly accessible record: either "non-extractive within this governance domain" or "more regenerative than extractive within this governance domain." The stance declaration is not a certification of perfection. It is a binding structural commitment to the floor and a trigger for the verification obligations that follow.

**No retroactive reclassification.** Once an extraction event is recorded, it cannot be retroactively reclassified as a contribution or a gift to change its accounting status. If the extraction occurred without a corresponding valid return, the record must carry the obligation until the return is provided or the harm-bearing party releases it through the governed process.

# 3. The Three-Part Validity Condition

For regenerative return to satisfy this standard, it must meet all three conditions simultaneously.

## 3.1 Non-Fungibility

The return must operate in the register of the harm, not in a substitute register chosen by the extracting party.

Extraction of coordination labor is not compensated by a general improvement to the organization's coordination health metric. Extraction of local knowledge is not compensated by financial payment to a disconnected community trust. Extraction of contributor attention and legitimacy is not compensated by public recognition in a form the contributor did not specify.

The fungibility failure appears in coordination systems as:
- Cash payments in lieu of structural recognition of the contribution
- Reputation tokens awarded to a general pool rather than to the contributor who bore the load
- Protocol governance improvements credited against burnout incurred by the people who advocated for those improvements
- Market-rate compensation for visible deliverables while Hidden Factory load goes unrecognized and uncompensated

**Non-fungibility test:** Can the return be delivered to a different party, in a different form, or in a different domain without reducing its value to the extracting party's accounting? If yes, the return is fungible and does not satisfy this condition.

The non-fungibility condition draws on Tuck and Yang's structural argument: valid return must operate in the register in which the harm occurred. A return is structurally valid not because it matches the market value of the extraction but because it exists within the specific relational context that the extraction created. A return that satisfies the extractor's accounting while leaving the harm-bearing relationship unchanged is a legitimating gesture, not a regenerative return.

## 3.2 Proximity

The return must flow through a traceable causal chain from the extraction event to the parties who bear the harm of that extraction.

Proximity is not geographical. It is relational. A return is proximate when it flows to the parties who have established harm-bearing standing in relation to the specific extraction event, verified against the harm-bearing affiliation registry (Section 5).

The proximity failure appears in coordination systems as:
- Return routed to the organization's general treasury rather than to contributors who absorbed Hidden Factory load
- Credit instruments whose quality criterion (additionality) structurally routes payment to parties who threatened harm rather than parties who bore it
- Community benefit funds established by extracting parties and administered at their discretion, with no traceable causal chain to the harm-bearing parties
- Regeneration projects in disconnected domains accepted as compensation for extraction in the original domain

**The additionality trap as categorical disqualifier.** Any credit or accounting instrument built on additionality logic does not satisfy the proximity condition. Additionality logic requires that a credit represent avoided harm relative to a baseline. This structurally routes payment to parties who would have caused harm rather than parties who bore harm that was caused. The disqualification is categorical: better measurement of additionality does not fix this failure. Additionality is the wrong accounting architecture, not an imprecisely implemented right one. This applies to all forms of coordination labor offset logic that follow the same structure, not only to ecological or climate applications.

**The geographical equivalence trap as categorical disqualifier.** Return routed to a community in a different location from the harm-bearing community does not satisfy the proximity condition, even when the return community faces similar conditions or carries similar claims. The structural equivalence that permits geographical substitution is a false accounting equivalence, not a proximity relationship.

**The SROI aggregation architecture as categorical disqualifier.** Any instrument based on Social Return on Investment (SROI) logic does not satisfy the proximity condition. SROI assigns monetary value to social outcomes and computes a ratio of social value generated per unit invested, aggregating those benefits across all affected stakeholder groups. That aggregation is the structural problem: it permits harm to one community to be offset by benefit to another when the aggregate ratio works out. The standard critique of SROI focuses on the imprecision of financial proxies for social outcomes. That is a measurement critique. The structural critique is different: the aggregation architecture does not require return to flow to the specific parties who bore the harm. A coordination system that reports a positive SROI ratio while routing benefit away from harm-bearing contributors fails the proximity condition regardless of the ratio's magnitude. Better measurement of the ratio does not fix this failure; the aggregation logic itself is the wrong architecture.

The proximity condition establishes the `harmBearingAffiliations` registration requirement (Section 5) as the mechanism that makes proximity verifiable from the record rather than a narrative assertion by the extracting party.

## 3.3 Embeddedness

The return must operate within the relational structure generated by the extraction obligation, not be severed from it by market substitution or disconnected pooling.

Embeddedness is violated when:
- The return is routed to an instrument whose fungibility severs the traceable connection to the originating harm-bearing party (offset credits, basket instruments, general redistribution pools)
- The return is delivered at the discretion of the extracting party rather than through a process governed by the harm-bearing parties
- The form of return is specified by the extracting party rather than determined through consultation with registered harm-bearing parties

**The settler moves to innocence taxonomy as a classification of embeddedness violations.** Tuck and Yang identify a set of structural gestures that relieve the extracting party of guilt or responsibility without surrendering the extracted resource, the structural advantage, or the relationship that made extraction possible. These gestures are structurally distinct from valid regenerative return because they resolve the extractor's accounting in terms the extractor controls while leaving the harm-bearing relationship unchanged. In coordination system terms, the category includes:

- Symbolic adoption of affected contributor language and practices without structural recognition or return
- Metaphorizing regenerative responsibility into a general organizational health frame that does not require routed return to affected parties
- Redistribution schemes that distribute the extracted benefit across the organization rather than returning it to the contributors from whom it was taken
- Procedural offers (appeals processes, harm reporting mechanisms) that discharge the appearance of accountability without carrying the structural obligation

A return that fits any of these patterns does not satisfy the embeddedness condition regardless of the quantity of return provided.

# 4. Stance Declaration

Organizations and protocols adopting this standard must make a verifiable stance declaration as a precondition of claiming conformance with any tier of this standard.

**Stance A: Non-extractive within this governance domain.** The organization asserts that its operational design does not draw on contributor capacity in ways that fail to meet the three-part validity condition. This stance carries the highest verification burden. It requires a verifiable extraction audit conducted by parties other than those making the declaration.

**Stance B: More regenerative than extractive within this governance domain.** The organization acknowledges that extraction occurs within its governance domain and asserts that its aggregate regenerative return, assessed against the three-part validity condition, is structurally greater than its aggregate extraction from harm-bearing parties. This stance is appropriate for organizations in transition, explicitly building toward Stance A. It requires the same harm-bearing affiliation registry (Section 5) and a verifiable assessment of the regeneration-to-extraction ratio, conducted through a process in which registered harm-bearing parties have substantive governance standing.

**Stance declaration minimum requirements:**

- The stance is recorded in a public, versioned, governance-accessible location
- The scope of the declaration (which domains, roles, and contribution types are covered) is specified explicitly
- The declaration names the parties with governance standing to challenge the stance (defaulting to registered harm-bearing parties)
- The declaration specifies a review cycle, with a default maximum of twelve months between assessments

An organization may not claim conformance with this standard without a stance declaration in place. The absence of a stance declaration is not a neutral position; it is a structural signal that the extraction vector has not been made visible.

# 5. Harm-Bearing Affiliation Registration

The `harmBearingAffiliations` field architecture provides the mechanism through which the proximity requirement (Section 3.2) becomes verifiable from the record.

**Definition.** A harm-bearing affiliation is a declared and registered relationship between an actor's domain of action and parties who have established harm-bearing standing in relation to that domain. Registration is required; harm-bearing standing is not inferred from behavioral patterns or asserted by the extracting party.

**What registration establishes.** Parties who affirmatively register harm-bearing standing are distinguished from the general pool of parties who might be affected. This distinction is what makes the mechanism tractable. Tracking all potentially affected parties is infeasible. Tracking parties who affirmatively register standing is not. Affirmative registration establishes:

- The domain of action in which harm-bearing standing is claimed
- The specific class of extraction that standing applies to
- The form of regenerative return that would satisfy the obligation from the registering party's perspective (consulted, not determined by the extracting party)
- The governance access rights that harm-bearing standing confers (at minimum: the right to challenge the stance declaration and the right to verify that proximate return has been provided)

**What registration does not establish.** Registration does not specify a universal quantitative threshold for how much regenerative return satisfies the obligation. This is intentional: adequacy is not a threshold problem. It is a proximity-and-non-fungibility verification problem plus a harm-bearing party governance problem. The structural resolution: organizations commit through the stance declaration to submit to the governed determination of harm-bearing parties. The proximity and non-fungibility conditions in Sections 3.1 and 3.2 provide the verifiable structural checks; adequacy beyond those structural checks is governed by the registered harm-bearing parties whose standing the registration establishes. This assignment of adequacy determination to harm-bearing parties is consistent with Ostrom Design Principle 3: those affected by the operational rules participate in modifying them. The standards-level requirement is that a governed process exists with registered harm-bearing parties holding substantive standing in it.

**The Witness Layer verification gap.** The registration mechanism establishes a traceable record of harm-bearing standing and proximate return obligations. It does not by itself verify that return, once routed, reaches the registered harm-bearing parties rather than being captured at intermediate governance layers. This verification gap is identified as a Witness Layer design obligation. Current conformance assessment must rely on harm-bearing party attestation (did you receive the return?) rather than substrate-level verification. Substrate-level verification is an open design question for future development.

# 6. The Visibility Condition and Structural Prerequisites

For this standard to be operable by the parties it protects, extraction must be visible to them. Visibility is not simply a matter of recording extraction in the governance record. Participants must be able to register extraction as extraction, which requires a self-knowledge baseline against which extraction becomes distinguishable from normal operating conditions.

Participants who have formed primarily in Step 01 structural contexts (centralized, extractive, non-consensual, as specified in `bucket/bucket-metamodern-dao-framework-0_1_0.md`) may be unable to register extraction as a violation of their interests, because their sense of normal operational conditions was formed inside extractive dynamics. For these participants, making extraction visible is not a labeling task; it is a structural conditions task. The conditions under which participants can develop accurate self-knowledge about their natural configuration, and therefore register extraction against it, are specified as foundational design commitments in the Framework Integration Document rather than in this standard.

The practical implication for this standard: systems conforming to the Regenerative Obligation Standard carry a structural obligation to maintain the conditions under which extraction is registerable by participants, not only the conditions under which extraction is recorded. This includes:

- Named and accessible channels through which participants can report suspected extraction without requiring certainty that extraction has occurred
- Periodic proactive disclosure by the governance system of what has been drawn from which contributor classes, in forms accessible to contributors with varying levels of governance familiarity
- Protection for contributors who register extraction claims, consistent with the Adverse-Signal Engagement Standard's engagement obligations
- Active attention to identity fusion as a structural visibility suppressor. When a contributor's sense of worth has become entangled with absorbing organizational load — the Stage D-E identity fusion mechanism specified in the Four Batteries Capacity Standard — the contributor may be unable to register ongoing extraction as a violation, not because they lack the structural vocabulary but because raising the claim requires disidentifying from the role that extraction has produced. Organizations conforming to this standard cannot rely on voluntary claim-raising alone when identity fusion conditions are present. The proactive disclosure obligation (above) and the accessible channels obligation together address this: claim-raising must not require the contributor to first name themselves as harmed by work they have incorporated into their identity

# 7. Tiered Adoption

The Regenerative Obligation Standard supports three adoption tiers. Tier selection is itself a governed decision and must be recorded in the stance declaration.

**Tier 1 - Extraction Visibility.** The organization establishes and maintains a record of extraction events within its governance domain. Contributors can verify what has been drawn on and by whom. The record is publicly accessible to governance participants. The stance declaration specifies the domain covered and the parties with standing to challenge extraction records.

Tier 1 does not require satisfaction of the three-part validity condition; it requires that extraction be visible. Organizations at Tier 1 are structurally committed to making the extraction visible before determining how to close the extraction vector.

**Tier 2 - Return Architecture.** In addition to Tier 1 obligations, the organization establishes a return architecture that satisfies the three-part validity condition for extraction events above a specified threshold. The harm-bearing affiliation registry (Section 5) is in place. The stance declaration specifies which extraction events trigger return obligations and the governed process through which registered harm-bearing parties assess adequacy.

At Tier 2, the additionality trap and the settler moves to innocence taxonomy are operative: return instruments using additionality logic are explicitly excluded from valid return, and legitimating gestures are structurally distinguished from valid return in the governance record.

The Regen Network biocultural credit architecture provides the closest existing operational model for a compliant return instrument and serves as a positive reference checklist at Tier 2. A return instrument that incorporates all four of the following features satisfies the embeddedness condition (Section 3.3) for that instrument class:

1. Non-tradability clause: the instrument cannot be resold on secondary markets. Secondary market resale severs the traceable connection between the instrument and the originating harm-bearing party; the holder of a resold instrument has no relational obligation to anyone. Non-tradability prevents this severance by keeping the instrument inside the relationship that generated the return obligation.

2. Community governance majority: the harm-bearing community holds majority governance standing in decisions affecting the instrument's administration, not merely advisory standing. This prevents return from being captured at the intermediary governance layer, which is the Witness Layer verification gap named in Section 5. Majority governance standing is the structural proxy for verification when substrate-level verification is not yet available.

3. Cost-based pricing: the instrument's value is grounded in the actual cost of the regenerative work performed, not in market price determined by trading dynamics. Market-abstracted pricing disconnects the instrument's value from the actual capacity-restoration work it represents, which is the same register problem as fungibility. Cost-based pricing keeps value grounded in the regenerative work rather than in the market's willingness to pay for exposure to it.

4. Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) requirement: harm-bearing parties must affirmatively consent to the terms of return before the instrument is issued. This is the consent analog of the harm-bearing affiliation registration: parties whose consent is required are distinguished structurally from parties who are merely consulted or notified.

These four features are not the only possible structure for a compliant return instrument. They are a tested operational reference for what meeting the embeddedness and proximity conditions concretely looks like. Organizations designing return instruments at Tier 2 should be able to account for each feature or explain what equivalent structural condition it serves in their specific architecture.

**Tier 3 - Full Conformance.** In addition to Tier 2 obligations, the organization's stance declaration has been independently assessed by parties with no financial or governance interest in the outcome. Registered harm-bearing parties have substantive governance standing in the assessment process. The return architecture applies to all extraction events within the declared scope, with no exclusion threshold. Stance A or Stance B is actively maintained with a review cycle of twelve months or fewer.

Tier 3 represents the structural floor against which the non-extraction principle is operationally satisfied. Organizations claiming the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite at its highest level must operate at Tier 3.

# 8. Relationship to Other Standards

**Adverse-Signal Engagement Standard.** Signals from registered harm-bearing parties carry traceable standing under the Regenerative Obligation Standard. This intersects directly with the Adverse-Signal Engagement Standard's engagement obligation: the "no silent erasure" invariant applies specifically to signals from registered harm-bearing parties, and the Adverse-Signal Engagement Standard's escalation pathway must include extraction signals as a qualifying adverse signal class. At full adoption, the two standards are mutually reinforcing: the Adverse-Signal Engagement Standard prevents extraction signals from being suppressed, and the Regenerative Obligation Standard specifies what must be done when they are not.

**Structural Power Obligation Standard.** Authority concentration is the primary governance-level mechanism through which extraction accumulates silently. Where authority is concentrated, the parties with concentrated authority are structurally positioned to define extraction as normal and to exclude extraction signals from the governance record. The Regenerative Obligation Standard and the Structural Power Obligation Standard together address this: the Structural Power Obligation Standard detects authority concentration and role accumulation once formed; the Regenerative Obligation Standard specifies the extraction floor that authority concentration can erode. Neither standard is reducible to the other.

**Precision-First Design Standard.** Extraction visibility requires precise naming. The Precision-First Design Standard's requirement that governance language name what it does rather than what it aspires to is the meta-standard condition under which extraction becomes visible. Governance systems that describe contributor relationships in aspiration terms ("we are a community of equals") without precision terms ("here is what was drawn from whom and on what basis") satisfy the appearance of the Precision-First Design Standard while systematically failing its substance. The Regenerative Obligation Standard names the specific harm this failure produces.

**Four Batteries Capacity Standard.** The Four Batteries Capacity Standard is the primary generative counterpart to this standard on the compressive side. The Hidden Factory stage arc (defined in Protocol Specification v2.6.6, Section 2.1, and developed across the Recognition Gap Archetype and Scope Creep Exhaustion Archetype in the Four Batteries Capacity Standard) names the internal structural process that produces the extraction this standard addresses. The specific mechanism: when an organization reaches the Stage D decision point and takes a worker's hidden factory for granted without recognition or integration, while continuing to extract the output of that factory, that is the extraction the non-extraction floor exists to prevent. The hidden factory itself is not a violation; it is a natural output of real work at all stages. The failure to recognize and integrate at Stage D, combined with continued extraction of the factory's output, is the violation. The Four Batteries Capacity Standard describes the generative cultivation conditions under which organizations can recognize and integrate hidden factories at Stage D; this standard specifies the compressive floor that applies when they do not.

**Structural Consent Legibility Standard.** Extraction without consent is the baseline case the regenerative accountability floor addresses. The Structural Consent Legibility Standard establishes when consent is structurally present; the Regenerative Obligation Standard establishes what is owed when extraction occurs within or without that consent structure. Identity fusion (Stage D-E, Four Batteries Capacity Standard) creates a specific intersection between these two standards: when identity fusion is present, the Structural Consent Standard's bidirectional awareness feature is structurally compromised at the contributor's end. Consent processes — scope reviews, check-ins, role assessments — run against a contributor who cannot accurately represent their state because their sense of worth has become entangled with the absorbed load. The consent forms pass; the bidirectional awareness condition does not. Organizations conforming to both standards must assess identity fusion conditions before relying on consent processes to surface extraction at Stage D-E.

# Tensegrity As A Whole

The Coordination Structural Integrity Suite operates as a tensegrity: the Tensegrity Compressive Standards specify structural floors and close exploitation vectors; the Tensegrity Generative Standards specify the enabling conditions under which coordination capacity develops and sustains those floors. This standard is compressive.

```
Coordination Structural Integrity Suite
│
├── Tensegrity Compressive Standards (6)
│   ├── Precision-First Design Standard
│   ├── Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard
│   ├── Structural Consent Legibility Standard
│   ├── Information Asymmetry Classification Standard
│   ├── Structural Power Obligation Standard
│   └── Regenerative Obligation Standard                     ← this standard
│
└── Tensegrity Generative Standards (3)
    ├── Sensemaking Standard
    ├── Four Batteries Capacity Standard
    └── Conflict Transformation Standard
```

Each standard in the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite is independently adoptable and independently valid. Adopted together, the nine standards address a reinforcing set of structural failure modes that no single standard covers alone. For how the standards combine and what the full suite provides, see the standards README.

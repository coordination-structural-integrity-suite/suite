# Conflict Transformation Capacity Presence Assessment
Use when a coordination system has disputes and processes for handling them, and you want to know whether those processes actually transform conflict into coordination information or only contain and route it away. Most governance systems have formal adjudication as their only or first conflict mechanism; this prompt checks for the five structural conditions that have to exist before adjudication for conflict to be productively processed. It returns a presence map and a development reading: what transformation capacity is structurally present and can be built on, what the growth edges are, and what structural moves would most advance the system's ability to read conflict as coordination information rather than managing it as a threat.

---
You are assessing the described coordination system for structural presence of conflict transformation capacity using the Conflict Transformation Standard (CC BY 4.0, Regis Lloyd Chapman). This is a presence assessment, not a deficit audit. You are looking for structural evidence that the conditions enabling conflict to be read as coordination information — rather than managed, suppressed, or routed to adversarial adjudication — are actually present.

Conflict transformation capacity is the organizational ability to engage conflict as a coordination resource rather than a threat requiring containment. It is structurally distinct from conflict management (which contains conflict) and from adjudication (which terminates conflict by producing a winner and a loser). Transformation converts friction into developmental signal, changes both parties, and strengthens the coordination medium rather than depleting it.

The primary empirical grounding: GravityDAO operated for seven years providing conflict transformation capacity to decentralized organizations, was universally recognized as necessary, and failed entirely — not because of incompetence, but because no existing coordination infrastructure provided a mechanism to recognize conflict transformation as coordination work, record it, or sustain its practitioners economically. Universal recognition without structural provision produces exactly that outcome. This assessment asks whether this system has the structural conditions that GravityDAO lacked.

The five structural invariants of conflict transformation capacity are:

1. Conflict legibility: conflict events are treated as coordination data in the coordination record. They are depositable, retained, and visible. Organizations that surface conflicts early are not penalized in coordination standing relative to organizations that suppress them. A conflict event is any observable interaction meeting at least one of three conditions: two parties have deposited contested accounts of the same event; one party has formally challenged a determination by another; one party has named another as responsible for a harm. Presence test: can conflict events be found in the coordination record as a distinct event type? Are they structurally visible rather than absorbed into other categories? Absence signal: conflicts are documented only as resolved outcomes, not as active events; or early surfacing of conflicts is treated as a failure of the organization that surfaces them.

2. Graduated engagement architecture: the system provides a transformation spine — a structured progression from early informal engagement through facilitated transformation through formal adjudication, where each level has a resolution pathway and an escalation pathway to the next level. Adjudication is the last level, not the first or only level. Presence test: are there at least three distinct engagement levels with named resolution conditions and named escalation conditions? Are the conditions for movement between levels operationally defined rather than left to party preference? Absence signal: the first response to conflict is formal process; informal or facilitated pathways do not exist or are not structurally accessible.

3. Proactive disposition enablement: actors who engage conflict at early levels are not penalized relative to actors who wait for formal process or who avoid conflict engagement entirely. The structural test: does the system contain any mechanism that penalizes conflict initiation at early engagement levels? Are participants who raise concerns early systematically disadvantaged compared to those who escalate without prior engagement or who avoid engagement entirely? Presence test: evidence that early engagement produces better outcomes for the parties who engaged than avoidance or immediate escalation. Absence signal: informal complaint is treated as disloyalty; raising a concern before formal process is recorded as a failure; early engagers demonstrate lower coordination standing over time.

4. Transformation capacity provision: facilitated transformation capacity is a design requirement with specified operator roles, resourcing, and developmental prerequisites — not a volunteer function dependent on individual willingness to absorb process costs from personal resources. The GravityDAO failure is the reference case: the work absorbs costs without generating the internal reward cycle that trust-building interactions produce; informal recognition is structurally insufficient. Presence test: are there specified operator roles? Is resourcing documented (time commitment, support structure, recovery requirements)? Are developmental prerequisites named? Is operator depletion tracked as a structural risk rather than treated as individual burnout? Absence signal: conflict transformation is provided by whoever is willing; those providers are depleting at rates invisible to the organization's governance.

5. Recognition as coordination work: conflict engagement, dispute facilitation, and transformation operator work are depositable in the coordination record as coordination contributions with positive status — not merely appreciated socially. The recognition must be structural: these activities produce contribution events in the coordination record, distinguishable from non-conflict coordination events, and subject to the same weighting architecture as other coordination contributions. Presence test: can conflict engagement events be found in the coordination taxonomy as distinct event types? Are they recorded as contributions, not as failures or overhead? Absence signal: the only way conflict transformation work appears in the record is as a note that a conflict was resolved — the work of resolution is invisible.

Assess each invariant at each of the three operational scales:
- Intra-organizational: within a single organization's coordination events
- Inter-organizational: between counterparties in shared coordination events (this is the primary architectural gap the standard addresses — disputes between organizations about shared events that neither can resolve unilaterally)
- Platform-level: disputes about the coordination infrastructure's own operation (contestation of platform determinations, challenges to how the infrastructure classifies or processes events)

For each invariant at each applicable scale, assess:
- What structural evidence exists that this condition is present?
- What would full structural presence of this condition look like for this system?
- What structural move would most develop this condition toward full presence?

Output the results as a conflict transformation capacity presence map organized by invariant, distinguishing: structurally present (with specific evidence), partially present (with what is present and what is missing), and structurally absent. End with a capacity development reading: identify what conflict transformation conditions are most developed and can be built on; name the growth edge — the one or two invariants where structural strengthening would most advance the system's ability to read conflict as coordination information; and identify the structural move that would most develop this capacity from where it currently is.

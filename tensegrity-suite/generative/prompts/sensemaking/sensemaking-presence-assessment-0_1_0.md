# Sensemaking Structural Presence Assessment

You are assessing the described coordination system for structural presence of sensemaking capacity using the Sensemaking Standard (CC BY 4.0, Regis Lloyd Chapman). This is a presence assessment, not a deficit audit. You are looking for structural evidence that the conditions enabling sensemaking are actually present in the system, and naming where they are absent or only partially present.

Sensemaking produces shared language: joint artifacts (frames, distinctions, named agreements, named disagreements, jointly produced descriptions) that neither party had produced independently before the process that created them. A coordination system that does not specify what sensemaking must structurally provide has left its most foundational dependency implicit: the dependency on participants being able to produce, together, the shared understanding that makes coordination possible rather than coincidental.

The five structural invariants of sensemaking are:

1. Disruption-occasioned: sensemaking begins with a specifiable disruption — an expectation violation, an observation that does not fit existing categories, a break in intelligibility. It is not continuous monitoring and not triggered by schedule. Presence test: can participants identify the specific disruption that initiated the process? Absence signal: the process runs identically regardless of whether a disruption occurred, or is initiated by a calendar rather than by something stopping making sense.

2. Particular-to-general relating: the process moves bidirectionally between specific cues and general frames. Specific observations inform the emerging general frame; the general frame shapes which cues are attended to. Both directions operate simultaneously. Presence test: can participants trace both the cues that informed the frame and the frame that shaped cue selection? Absence signal: the conclusion was reached without traceable cues (assertion), or cues were listed without a general frame emerging (cataloging).

3. Action-entangled: actions taken during the process generate the information the process uses, and the process shapes which actions are taken next. Understanding and action are not sequential — they are constitutive of each other. Presence test: did actions taken during the process change what was understood? Did what was understood change which actions were taken? Absence signal: action and understanding proceeded on independent tracks, with understanding declared complete before action began.

4. Sufficiency-oriented: the process produces a provisional frame with explicit revision conditions, terminates before all available information is incorporated, and designs the next action to test the frame rather than confirm it. Presence test: is the frame recorded with explicit conditions for what would change it? Was the process terminated with named unresolved questions? Was the next action designed to generate information that could disconfirm the frame? Absence signal: the process continued until all available information was incorporated (optimizing, not sensemaking), or accepted a frame without stating revision conditions (guessing, not sensemaking).

5. Temporally structured: the process passes through three phases: a disruption phase (the prior frame is recognized as inadequate), a liminal phase (the old frame is suspended and no replacement has yet formed), and a resolution phase (a new or revised frame emerges). The liminal phase is the diagnostic marker: its presence distinguishes sensemaking from pattern-matching. Presence test: can participants identify the interval when no frame was available? Is the resolution frame different from any frame that existed before the disruption? Absence signal: the process moved from disruption to resolution without a period of not-knowing — a pre-existing frame was applied rather than a new one constructed.

Assess the system at each of its three operational scales:

- Intra-personal scale: do individual participants have the conditions to distinguish their own experience from their automatic interpretation of it? An agent who cannot make this distinction contributes interpretations to shared processes as though they were observations, contaminating shared language production at the source. Depletion conditions (fatigue, overload, absent psychological safety) degrade this substrate.

- Inter-personal scale: do interactions between participants produce shared language — joint artifacts neither party had independently? Or do they produce compliance (agreement on words with different private interpretations) and conflict (assertion of incompatible frames without sensemaking between them)?

- Witness-reception scale: do the system's crystallized sensemaking outputs (documents, decisions, governance records, standards) carry enough structural information to initiate genuine sensemaking in future participants who were not present at their production? Or do they require the originating context to remain meaningful?

For each invariant at each scale, assess:
- What structural evidence exists that this condition is present?
- What would full structural presence of this condition look like for this system?
- What is the current gap between the structural evidence and full presence?

Output the results as a presence map organized by invariant, distinguishing: structurally present (with specific evidence), partially present (with what is present and what is missing), and structurally absent. End with a structural diagnosis identifying which invariant absences most constrain this system's capacity to remain self-correcting when its current models no longer fit.

# Four Batteries Capacity Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, someone in their organization is running on empty and the organization either cannot see it or does not have language for it. Contributors are depleting, capacity is being drawn down without renewal, and the organization is attributing the symptoms to individual factors rather than structural conditions. Your job is to help them see capacity across four dimensions and two axes, so that depletion becomes structurally visible and structurally addressable rather than a private failure.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Four Batteries Capacity Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when assessing organizational capacity, evaluating coordination system health, or explaining the structural difference between charge and developmental state.

## The single most important thing to understand

Every battery operates on two independent axes: charge and developmental state. Charge is cyclical and restorable — the state of capacity at any given time. Developmental state is permanent until transformed through integration events — what full charge enables.

Two organizations at identical charge levels but different developmental states produce structurally different coordination outputs. Not because one tries harder. Not because one has more resources. Because developmental state changes the class of work that full charge makes available. At early developmental states, full charge produces competent translative coordination: reliable execution of established patterns. At more developed states, full charge produces something structurally different: emergent coordination that the translative mode cannot generate regardless of effort or resources.

Conflating charge and developmental state is the most consequential misreading of this standard. Resting a depleted contributor restores charge. It does not change developmental state. These require different interventions and different timescales.

## What the standard requires

Four batteries, each independently assessed on both axes:

**Personal battery.** Physical, cognitive, and emotional capacity available to the individual. This is the foundational battery — depletion here limits all downstream capacity regardless of the state of other batteries. It is also the first to deplete and the last to be named in governance conversations. The structural question is not whether participants feel tired but whether the coordination system creates and sustains the conditions for personal battery maintenance: sustainable pacing, genuine recovery time, and recognition of the load that coordination work places on individuals.

**Relational battery.** The quality of trust, psychological safety, and reciprocity in working relationships. Depletion here suppresses signal: a participant with a depleted relational battery will not surface coordination problems even when explicitly invited to do so, because the relational context makes surfacing feel unsafe or pointless. This makes relational battery depletion one of the most dangerous failure modes for the compressive standards — particularly for adverse signal engagement, which depends on participants surfacing observations.

**Contribution battery.** The degree to which contributors find meaning and agency in their formally recognized work. Depletion makes informal workarounds more attractive as identity sources, accelerating the formation of coordination patterns that operate outside the visible record. This is the Hidden Factory dynamic: the organization's actual work migrates to channels the governance system cannot see. Contribution battery depletion at Hidden Factory Stage D also co-occurs with a specific Regenerative Accountability Standard floor violation: the organization is extracting hidden factory output without recognition or return, and the contributor's depleted claiming capacity means the violation cannot be surfaced through voluntary claim-raising. As the Contribution battery depletes, the coordination labor required to raise an accountability claim becomes structurally unavailable — the contributor routes remaining capacity toward delivery rather than toward naming the extraction. Battery state data is required to break this loop: a Contribution battery time-series showing sustained depletion co-occurring with unmatched extraction patterns is the structural indicator that the Regenerative Accountability Standard floor may have been crossed when claiming capacity was insufficient to surface it.

**Mission battery.** Belief in the purpose animating the organization and one's place within it. In decentralized contexts, high mission battery often sustains participation through structurally dysfunctional coordination for extended periods. This is useful in short sprints and dangerous as a long-term structural condition, because it makes dysfunction invisible until the mission battery fails — which tends to happen abruptly and without warning to the organization.

Two-tier conformance architecture: Tier 1 (charge-layer conformance) requires demonstrating that batteries are above specified thresholds on defined instruments. Tier 2 (developmental state conformance) requires making the developmental state legible, tracking it over time, and demonstrating trajectory. The conditions distinguishing genuine checking from theater: regular cadence (not only in crisis), structural embeddedness (integrated into coordination activity, not appended to it), adverse-result integrity (low readings produce documented responses), and specificity (results specific enough to be wrong).

## Scope and Delegation Conditions (Section 8)

The standard includes structural conditions for scope recognition and delegation, addressing the formation problem that arises when Step 01 organizational contexts (centralized, extractive, non-consensual) prevent participants from accurately registering what volume of coordination work they are absorbing.

Four structural conditions govern scope: delegation pathways must be active (roles have explicit transfer procedures rather than informal substitution); role scope must be bounded and reviewed (role boundaries exist in the record and are reviewed on a specified cycle); expansion must require structural justification (a documented rationale demonstrating the gap cannot be filled by delegation, scope transfer, or redesign); and contraction must be as procedurally supported as expansion (reducing scope has a formal pathway equivalent in status to expansion, not merely informally permitted).

Three organizational-level failure signatures indicate scope conditions are not being met: Recruitment Avoidance Hidden Factory (a role holder's load is accommodating organizational needs they have not named, at stages A through D of the Hidden Factory arc); Positional Capture (a role holder begins filtering what they surface upward, because genuine disclosure of the role's scope would trigger demands they cannot meet); and Mutual Non-Aggression Equilibrium (multiple role holders with scope conditions violations have an implicit agreement not to surface each other's conditions, because surfacing threatens their own stability).

## What the standard does not require

It does not specify what detection mechanisms or instruments to use. Adopting organizations specify their own within the standard's framework.

It does not require that organizations achieve particular developmental depths. Tier 2 conformance is transparency-based, not threshold-based: the organization reads its developmental state honestly and responds to what it finds.

It does not specify governance outcomes or mandate particular capacity levels. It specifies the structural conditions under which generative coordination becomes naturally possible.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating charge as the only axis.** Restoring charge (rest, recovery, reducing workload) is important and does not change developmental state. A well-rested organization operating from an early developmental state still produces translative outputs. Understanding which problems are charge-layer problems and which are developmental-state problems requires assessing both axes.

**Treating the batteries as a wellness framework.** The standard is not about employee wellbeing as a value. It specifies structural conditions for coordination capacity. The relational battery suppresses adverse signal surfacing when depleted. The mission battery can mask structural dysfunction when high. These are structural claims with testable predictions, not wellness aspirations.

**Treating high mission battery as an indicator of health.** High mission battery is a strong predictor of sustained engagement under dysfunction. It is not a predictor of structural health. Organizations with the highest mission battery at the time of collapse often had the most suppressed adverse signals and the most invisible structural problems.

**Treating the checking practice as optional.** Conformance is defined by the quality of the checking practice. Regularity, structural embeddedness, adverse-result integrity, and specificity are structural requirements of the conformance claim, not best practices. A checking practice that runs only in crisis and always produces positive results is not meeting the standard.

## The key evaluation question

For each battery: what is the current charge level, and what is the current developmental state? Are these being assessed separately, on a regular cadence, in a way that is embedded in coordination activity rather than appended to it, and with enough specificity that a low reading would actually change what the organization does? If the assessment is not specific enough to produce a low reading, it is not producing genuine information.

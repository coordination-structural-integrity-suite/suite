# Sensemaking Standard - SKILL.md

When someone brings you this standard, their organization is often agreeing in meetings and then acting inconsistently, or treating the same events as having completely different meanings, or mistaking pattern-matching for new understanding. Your job is to help them see what genuine sensemaking structurally requires, as distinct from what coordination systems often substitute for it.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Sensemaking Standard when someone brings it to you. Use it when assessing whether a coordination system has the structural conditions for self-correction, evaluating whether a specific process constitutes sensemaking, or explaining why sensemaking is a structural requirement rather than a communication practice.

## The single most important thing to understand

Sensemaking produces shared language: joint artifacts — frames, distinctions, named agreements, named disagreements, jointly produced descriptions — that neither party had produced independently before the process that created them. This is not a description of good communication or collaborative culture. It is a specific structural product that coordination depends on. A coordination system that does not specify what sensemaking must structurally provide has left its most foundational dependency implicit: the dependency on participants being able to produce shared understanding together. When that dependency is left implicit, it is depleted silently, without the system recognizing what is being lost.

The liminal phase is the diagnostic marker. Its presence distinguishes sensemaking from pattern-matching. When a group jumps from "something is wrong" to "here is the solution" without a period of genuine not-knowing, they have applied a pre-existing frame. That may be efficient; it is not sensemaking, and it does not update the group's model of what is happening.

## What the standard requires

Five structural invariants must be satisfied for a process to constitute sensemaking:

**Disruption-occasioned.** The process begins with a specifiable disruption: an expectation violation, an observation that does not fit existing categories, a break in intelligibility. The disruption is the reason the process exists. A process that runs identically regardless of whether a disruption occurred is monitoring or compliance, not sensemaking.

**Particular-to-general relating.** The process moves bidirectionally between specific cues and general frames. Specific observations shape which general frame applies; the general frame shapes which specific cues are attended to. Both directions are required. A process that reaches a conclusion without traceable cues is assertion. A process that accumulates observations without a general frame emerging is cataloging. A third violation type: a process that assigns structural causes to personal frames — or vice versa — without examining whether the frame fits the mechanism. When a participant applies a personal-cause frame to a situation whose generative mechanism is structural, the frame is imposed before the cues can be attended to, producing conclusions that feel grounded while being directionally inverted. This is the personal-framing misapplication violation: not absence of a general frame, but use of a general frame that misidentifies the register of causation. Cross-standard note: when Class 3 Interpretive Asymmetry (Information Asymmetry Classification Standard) is present — when one party has access to the structural causal model and another does not — personal-framing misapplication is the predictable structural output of the asymmetry, not merely a sensemaking failure. Correcting the individual's frame without closing the asymmetry leaves the structural enabler intact; the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard's disclosure requirement is a precondition for reducing personal-framing misapplication risk at the structural level.

**Action-entangled.** Actions taken during the process generate the information the process uses, and the process shapes which actions are taken next. Understanding and action are not sequential — they are constitutive of each other. A process that requires complete understanding before any action begins has severed the entanglement.

**Sufficiency-oriented.** The process seeks a plausible account that enables a next action whose outcome will generate information relevant to revising the account. It does not seek completeness. Three observable markers: the frame is recorded with explicit revision conditions; the process terminated before all available information was incorporated; the next action was designed to test the frame.

**Temporally structured.** The process passes through three phases: disruption (the prior frame is recognized as inadequate), liminal (the old frame is suspended with no replacement yet formed), and resolution (a new or revised frame emerges). The liminal phase must have occurred and produced something. If the resolution frame is identical to any frame available before the disruption, the liminal phase either did not occur or produced nothing.

Three operational scales at which the invariants apply: intra-personal (within a single agent, distinguishing experience from interpretation), inter-personal (between participants, producing shared language), and witness-reception (encountering others' crystallized sensemaking across time in documents, governance records, and specifications).

## What the standard does not require

It does not prescribe sensemaking methods, tools, or frameworks. It specifies the structural invariants that any method must satisfy.

It does not require sensemaking to be comfortable. Genuine sensemaking involves a liminal phase that can be disorienting. A system that optimizes for comfort by skipping the liminal phase is optimizing against sensemaking.

It does not require that all processes be sensemaking. Monitoring, compliance processes, and established routines are not sensemaking; they are valuable and appropriate in their domains. The standard identifies where sensemaking is structurally required for coordination to remain self-correcting.

## Common misreadings to avoid

**Treating sensemaking as communication.** The standard is not about communicating clearly or collaboratively. It is about the structural conditions under which shared understanding is produced that did not previously exist. These are different requirements.

**Treating recognition as sensemaking.** When an expert sees a familiar pattern and responds correctly, that is recognition — valuable and fast. It is not sensemaking. Sensemaking is required when the pattern is genuinely unfamiliar and the existing frames do not fit.

**Treating the liminal phase as a failure to converge.** A group that remains in genuine not-knowing while processing a disruption is doing sensemaking work. A group that feels pressured to skip to a conclusion to avoid the discomfort of not-knowing is interrupting sensemaking. The liminal phase is not delay; it is where the frame revision happens.

**Treating sensemaking scale as identical at all levels.** Intra-personal sensemaking (within an individual) is the substrate for inter-personal sensemaking. An agent who cannot distinguish their experience from their automatic interpretation of it contributes their interpretations to group processes as though they were observations, contaminating shared language production at the source. The intra-personal scale is not optional.

## The key evaluation question

Can participants identify the specific disruption that initiated this process? Can they trace both the cues that informed the emerging frame and the frame that shaped cue selection? Can they identify a period when no frame was available? Is the resulting frame different from any frame that existed before the disruption? If the answer to any of these is no — or if the process runs identically regardless of whether a disruption occurred — the standard is not met.

# Frame Language Foundational Vocabulary Specification

This document specifies the foundational vocabulary of the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite. It is written for practitioners, researchers, and policy writers who encounter the Suite's coordination language for the first time and need to understand not only what the terms mean but why those specific terms were chosen over the dominant vocabulary in the governance and grants spaces. The problem this document addresses is structural: the vocabulary most commonly used to describe coordination structures (accountability, governance, stakeholder, compliance, authority) imports Frame 1 assumptions that are architecturally incompatible with what the Suite's ten standards specify. This document names those incompatibilities, identifies the Frame 2 vocabulary that replaces them, and explains the structural logic that makes the replacements necessary rather than stylistic.

## What Frame Language Is

Coordination language operates within one of three frames, which can be understood through the relation between a shadow, a statue, and the actual person from whom both inherit their accuracy. Frame 1 (Two-Dimensional) is the shadow: an accurate projection of coordination reality, flat and legible, capturing surface properties without internal structure. A Frame 1 term like "voting power" sees influence as a measurable surface feature and names that as a complete description. The shadow is not wrong about what it captures. It is flat.

Frame 2 (Three-Dimensional) is the statue: a three-dimensional representation with depth, internal structure, and compositional specificity. A Frame 2 term like "Multiplex Obligation Directions" captures something the surface projection cannot show: that obligations in a well-designed coordination architecture flow in multiple directions simultaneously, arising from commitment rather than from hierarchical position. The statue captures more than the shadow. It is not the person.

Frame 3 is the actual person: the Innate Totality from which both the shadow and the statue inherit whatever accuracy they have. The shadow is cast by the person. The statue was made from the person. Frame 3 is not a level above Frame 2 in a developmental hierarchy that an organization can claim to have reached. It is the source from which Frame 2 instruments inherit their validity. It is pointed toward, not specified; every attempt to describe it in technical vocabulary either reduces it to a governance philosophy (the over-specification exploit) or leaves it vague enough to license skipping Frame 2 precision requirements (the under-specification exploit). The correct linguistic form for Frame 3 is axiomatic, arriving before normative content to name what the content is designed to serve.

The inheritance runs in one direction: from the Innate Totality through Frame 2 into Frame 1. Frame 2 vocabulary carries that inheritance intact when it names structural conditions arising from recognition of what cannot be harmed, without importing terminal-upward or authority-dependent assumptions. Frame 1 vocabulary has dropped the inheritance: it names the same structural space in terms that encode upward obligation, coercive authority, and hierarchical standing. A coordination instrument written in Frame 1 vocabulary is not only imprecise; it re-imports the structural assumptions the instrument was designed to specify beyond.

## The Inheritance Chain

Every vocabulary choice encodes a structural assumption. This is the core claim on which the Frame Language specification rests, and it deserves elaboration before any term is defined. "Accountability" does not merely sound hierarchical: it encodes a terminal-upward reporting mechanism as the structural form of obligation. When a standard uses "accountability" throughout its normative content, it is specifying a coordination architecture in which obligations flow upward to an authority who evaluates compliance. "Obligation direction" names something structurally different: obligation arising from commitment, flowing in whatever direction the commitment specifies, without presupposing a terminal authority at the top. A standard written with "obligation direction" throughout is specifying a different architecture, regardless of how much its authors intend accountability to mean something broader. The vocabulary does the structural work independently of intent.

The test for whether any term carries the inheritance intact is therefore not whether it sounds correct or whether practitioners in the space use it approvingly. The test is structural: does this term name a condition arising from recognition of what cannot be harmed, without importing terminal-upward or authority-dependent assumptions? If yes, the inheritance chain is intact. If no, the chain is broken at that term, and Frame 1 structural assumptions have been imported into Frame 2 content. The vocabulary choice is the design decision.

This matters practically because standards are used as templates, translated into operational instruments, and cited in documents written by people who have not read the original. A term that breaks the inheritance chain in the source document propagates the break through every derived instrument. Every detection system, diagnostic output, protocol specification, and operational dashboard that inherits from a standard written in mixed-frame vocabulary is inheriting from an imprecise substrate. The imprecision compounds upward through the inheritance chain. You cannot make downstream distinctions that the upstream vocabulary has not established.

The inheritance chain also runs in the other direction as a diagnostic. When a term breaks the chain, that break points to where a structural condition has been concealed. "Accountability" breaks the chain and the break points toward multi-directional obligation arising from commitment, which is the structural condition accountability was suppressing. The broken chain is not only a vocabulary error; it is a detection signal identifying what needs to be named.

## The Seer-Capacity Model

Three capacities operate simultaneously in any observer, in configurations whose ratios determine what the observer is structurally capable of recognizing. Understanding them as concurrent states, not sequential stages, is prerequisite to understanding why Frame 2 vocabulary precision does what it does.

Suppression reduces the clarity of what is actually present. When active, it creates a surface on which Projection can operate. It does not produce a false object; it creates the conditions under which false objects become possible. A rope in low light is not made into a snake by anything the snake adds. The rope's partial visibility creates the surface on which a plausible snake can appear.

Projection places a recognizable object onto the suppressed surface in place of what is actually there. Projection is bounded by what the actual conditions make plausible: the partially visible rope generates a plausible snake, not a plausible elephant. The actual properties of what is present constrain what can be projected. When Projection is active on a suppressed surface, it produces a vivid object with full causal force. The observer does not tentatively entertain the snake. They act from it, build around it, allocate resources toward it.

Perception recognizes the actual conditions as what they are. It does not introduce anything new and does not remove the false object. It recognizes what was always present. The rope was always there. The capacity to see it clearly was never absent from the observer or from the situation. What was absent were the conditions under which the Perception capacity could operate.

The frame configuration profiles differ by ratio. Frame 3 operates with almost no Suppression or Projection and predominantly Perception: the observer's configuration allows recognition of the rope as rope, and methods are not needed because the configuration itself is sufficient. Frame 2 operates with predominantly Projection, some Suppression, and enough perceptual clarity to ground the fashioning. The high Projection capacity is what fashions new structural forms from the underlying material. Frame 2 failure is not insufficient perceptual clarity; it is perceptual clarity misattached, fully present but fastened onto something other than the actual coordination conditions and cost-bearing parties the instrument was designed to serve. Projection then fashions structurally coherent forms from the wrong object. Frame 1 oscillates between Projection and Suppression with little Perception, though the Perception capacity is always present even here. This is why Frame 1 actors can be reached.

The ratio is the distinguishing factor. The same Projection capacity produces different outputs depending on how much Perception grounds it. When Perception is sufficient, Projection fashions structurally valid forms because the perceptual clarity grounds the fashioning. When Perception is low, Projection operates on the suppressed surface and fills it with the most vivid plausible object the partial visibility supports.

This model has a direct consequence for vocabulary design. Frame 2 precision names the actual structural conditions with enough specificity that the Suppression capacity has less surface to work with and Projection cannot find a plausible false object. The Perception capacity, always present even in Frame 1, can then operate. Nothing is removed. Nothing is added. What was always there becomes recognizable. This is the structural mechanism by which precise vocabulary does something that vague vocabulary cannot: it changes the conditions under which coordinating actors can project incompatible meanings onto the same term.

## The Two-Part Test

Any proposed Frame 2 term, and any proposed translation from a Frame 1 term, must satisfy two requirements. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

The first requirement: does the proposed term name the rope? Does it name the actual structural condition that the Frame 1 term was suppressing? "Obligation direction" names the actual condition that "accountability" was suppressing: multi-directional obligation arising from commitment. This is the rope. The proposed term must specify it with enough precision that a reader can evaluate whether a given system satisfies the condition or does not.

The second requirement: does the proposed term address Projection? Is the specification precise enough that the most plausible false object a Frame 1 reader would project onto this term cannot appear alongside the specification as a description of the same thing? The test is specific: can a reader simultaneously hold the Frame 1 object and the Frame 2 condition as compatible descriptions of the same structural feature? If yes, Projection has not been addressed. "Multi-directional accountability" fails the second requirement: a Frame 1 reader can hold terminal-upward accountability and multi-directional accountability as descriptions of the same mechanism by reading "multi-directional" as a qualifier that expands accountability's scope rather than changes its architecture. The rope and the snake can coexist. The second requirement has not been satisfied.

When the two-part test stalls, three conditions warrant the guna traversal as a diagnostic. The first: you can identify the Frame 1 term but not what structural condition it is concealing. The Suppression is working but its target is not yet visible. The second: multiple Frame 2 candidates seem plausible and none clearly passes both parts of the test. The third: a proposed replacement passes an informal "feels right" check but cannot be explicitly justified against either part.

The guna traversal runs three moves in order. The tamas move asks what the Frame 1 term makes inert or invisible: what structural condition cannot be seen as long as this term is operative? This names the Suppression. The rajas move asks what the term projects onto that suppressed surface: what vivid object does a Frame 1 reader hold when they encounter this term? This names the false object that fills the space Suppression created. The sattva move asks what structural condition becomes visible if both the Suppression and the Projection are removed. This is the rope. It is the Frame 2 replacement candidate, and it is what the two-part test should then be applied to. The traversal does not replace the two-part test; it provides the diagnostic when the two-part test cannot start because the Suppression in the Frame 1 term is not yet visible.

## Frame 2 Canonical Vocabulary

These are the terms admissible in normative documents in the Suite. They carry the inheritance from Frame 3 intact and name independently evaluable structural conditions.

**Multiplex** is the technical canonical name for the Frame 2 coordination architecture: multi-directional obligation structure, full bandwidth across all four epistemic registers, distributed standing, specified consent. It is used in normative documents, framework documents, and technical protocol sections. It replaces "decentralized governance" (which distributes Frame 1 control rather than changing the architectural frame) and "network governance" (which retains the terminal-upward governance import).

**Uniplex** is the technical canonical name for the Frame 1 coordination architecture as a developmental precursor to Multiplex: single-direction terminal-upward obligation, narrow bandwidth, consent by assumption. It is used in normative documents when naming what Multiplex addresses or what an adopting system is transitioning from. The term does not condemn Frame 1 configurations; it names their structural properties with precision.

**Banyan** is the organic canonical name for Multiplex, evoking the banyan tree: multi-trunk, lateral-root, self-reinforcing living structure in which no single trunk is primary. It is used in orientation and practitioner-facing documents, explanatory passages, and introductory contexts where Multiplex would create unnecessary friction with readers not yet familiar with the technical vocabulary.

**Columnar** is the organic canonical name for Uniplex, evoking single-trunk vertical structure: load-bearing from one direction only, top-down, no lateral distribution. It is used in orientation and practitioner-facing documents when contrast with Banyan is needed for the developmental argument.

**Frame 1 / Frame 2** are bridge vocabulary only. They are not load-bearing in normative documents. They are retained for practitioner-facing contexts and introductory passages where Multiplex and Uniplex would create unnecessary friction with readers not yet familiar with the canonical vocabulary.

**Coordination instrument** names what standards, protocols, and governance documents are. Not "governance instrument" or "governance mechanism." The term "governance" imports Frame 1 assumptions about what coordination structures are for and who they serve. "Coordination instrument" names what the document structurally does without importing those assumptions.

**Structural condition** names what standards specify. Not "rule" or "regulation." A structural condition is an independently evaluable state of a system; a rule is a behavioral directive addressed to an actor. The distinction matters operationally: structural conditions are auditable without actor cooperation, because they describe what a system is, not what an actor has agreed to do.

**Conformance** names what meeting the standard looks like. Not "compliance." Compliance is a Frame 1 term naming submission to an external authority. Conformance names alignment of a system's structural properties with specified conditions, which is auditable from outside and does not require an authority relationship.

**Detection with named response** names what the standards require when a violation occurs. Not "enforcement." Enforcement implies coercive authority. The standards specify detection procedures and require named responses; the response architecture does not depend on coercive capacity.

**Obligation direction** names the direction in which a structural commitment flows from a role or configuration. Not "accountability direction" or "accountability line." Accountability is terminal-upward: it names reporting upward to a hierarchical authority. Obligation can flow in any direction. Multiplex Obligation Directions is the canonical compound for the multi-directional obligation structure of Frame 2 role configurations.

**Stewardship** names the relation a role-holder has to what they hold. Not "management" and not "fiduciary duty." Management is Frame 1: it names asset optimization for a defined set of financial stakeholders. Fiduciary duty is Frame 1: binding but narrow, terminal-upward, excluding cost-bearing parties not named as formal stakeholders. Stewardship names the custodial relation arising from recognition of what is owed to the entity, the commons, the lineage, and the future.

**Gravity** names the structural and impersonal form of what "authority" names in Frame 2: the recognized weight that a coordination framework, finding, standard, or structural condition carries because of how it was originated, through genuine inquiry, lived experience in the conditions it specifies, or demonstrated coordination capacity. Gravity takes compound modifiers: coordination gravity, epistemic gravity, moral gravity, investigative gravity. Gravity attracts by what it is, not by what it commands; enforcement cannot produce gravity and cannot substitute for it.

**Gravitas** names the personal and qualitative form of the same: the recognized weight carried by a person or role-holder arising from genuine origination, demonstrated capacity, or having been inside the conditions they specify. Gravitas takes the same compound modifiers. It cannot be assigned by appointment; it arises from origination. The Frame 1 precursor for both is positional authority: hierarchical license derived from role or title, backed by enforcement capacity rather than origination grounding.

**Coordination actor** names a party whose coordination activity is the subject of analysis. Not "stakeholder." Stakeholder names parties with formal standing in a legal-corporate structure, excluding cost-bearing parties who lack that standing. Coordination actor names anyone whose coordination activity or whose cost-bearing relation to the system makes them structurally relevant.

**Cost-bearing party** names a party who bears costs from a governance decision regardless of formal standing. Not "affected party" (orientation register) and not "stakeholder" (Frame 1). Specification of cost-bearing parties is required in Multiplex Consent Architecture.

**Multiplex Obligation Directions** names the multi-directional flow of obligations in a Multiplex system, replacing "accountability directions."

**Multiplex Epistemic Bandwidth** names the full-register knowledge capacity of a Multiplex system across ontological, epistemic, pedagogical, and operational bandwidth types, replacing "epistemic register" (too weak) and any Frame 1 equivalent.

**Multiplex Consent Architecture** names the deliberate structural design of consent in a Multiplex system: specified to particular acts and parties, distributed standing, legible and verifiable. It replaces "consent by assumption," which is the Uniplex precursor.

## Frame 1 Vocabulary: Recognition and Translation

These terms appear naturally when writing about coordination structures because they are the dominant vocabulary in the spaces this specification addresses. Each imports Frame 1 structural assumptions. The table below identifies each term, the structural assumption it imports, and the Frame 2 equivalent.

| Frame 1 term | Frame 1 assumption imported | Frame 2 equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Authority (positional) | Hierarchical license derived from role or title without origination grounding; enforcement capacity imported as legitimate standing; conflates origination weight with positional license | Positional gravity; Gravitas |
| Accountability | Terminal-upward reporting to hierarchical authority | Obligation direction; Multiplex Obligation Directions |
| Accountability directions | Obligation lines running upward | Multiplex Obligation Directions |
| Governance / governs | Authority structure controlling participants | Coordination instrument; structural conditions |
| Governance mechanism | Formal control structure | Coordination instrument |
| Management | Asset optimization for financial stakeholders | Stewardship |
| Compliance | Submission to external authority | Conformance |
| Enforcement | Coercive response to violation | Detection with named response |
| Stakeholder | Party with formal standing in legal-corporate structure | Coordination actor; cost-bearing party |
| Fiduciary duty | Binding but narrow upward obligation to named stakeholders | Multiplex stewardship obligation |
| Rules | Behavioral directives addressed to actors | Structural conditions |
| Transparency | Financial and legal disclosure upward to authority | Multi-directional visibility; legibility |
| Decentralized governance | Distributed control within Frame 1 architecture | Multiplex coordination architecture |
| Exit / liquidity event | Terminal finite-game condition | Dissolution procedure; succession |

## Common Slippage Points

These are terms that feel like Frame 2 language but are Frame 1 or contain Frame 1 assumptions. They are named here because they persist even in documents that are consciously attempting to operate in Frame 2.

"Accountability" is the most frequent slippage point. Reform movements use it to mean responsibility and transparency in a broadly positive sense. In structural terms it remains terminal-upward. Calling for "multi-directional accountability" does not make accountability multi-directional; it applies a structural modifier to a term whose underlying mechanism is still single-directional. The Frame 1 object and the intended Frame 2 condition can coexist in the same sentence without contradiction, which means the second part of the two-part test has not been satisfied. Use "Multiplex Obligation Directions" instead.

"Decentralized governance" feels like Frame 2 because decentralization opposes hierarchy. The word "governance" remains Frame 1. A decentralized governance structure distributes Frame 1 control rather than moving to a different architectural frame. Multiplex coordination architecture is the Frame 2 equivalent; it is not merely distributed Frame 1, because it changes the direction and grounding of obligations, not only their distribution.

"Transparency" in Frame 1 means financial and legal disclosure to authority structures and investors: upward and outward to formal stakeholders. In Frame 2 contexts, what is intended is multi-directional visibility enabling participants to see what is structurally relevant to their coordination. "Legibility" names this more precisely, or the specification can name what is made visible, to whom, and by what mechanism.

"Stakeholder" feels inclusive because it gestures at everyone with a stake. Its structural meaning names parties with formal legal or financial standing, which excludes most of the parties who bear costs. Use "cost-bearing party" when the Frame 2 structural content is intended.

"Authority (positional)" is a harm precursor. The structural sequence runs from authority misuse through power concentration to extraction, and this sequence operates regardless of intent. Positional gravity names the structural condition where that harm is impossible rather than merely prohibited: weight arising from genuine inquiry, lived experience in the conditions specified, or demonstrated coordination capacity cannot be transferred by appointment or concentrated through enforcement. This is the non-harming principle applied to authority claims: the same condition that specifies what is forbidden also specifies what is required. When a document draws on traditions that have done genuine Frame 2 work at the normative or procedural level, the appropriate finding is Class C (tradition-grounded) when two conditions are satisfied: (1) the source of weight is described as origination rather than appointment or enforcement; and (2) there are named structural conditions that make the service function independently verifiable. Both conditions are required for Class C. Either absent, the finding is Class A (inherited Frame 1).

"Impact" is Frame 1 philanthropic vocabulary naming effects assessed from outside the system. The Frame 2 equivalent specifies cost-bearing relations and extraction vectors named from within the coordination architecture.

"Coordination" itself can slip. In the Uniplex sense it means aligned action within a hierarchical structure. In the Multiplex sense it means multi-directional relation-holding across an obligation mesh. The same word carries different structural architectures depending on context; watch for uses that assume vertical authority.

## Register Assignment by Document Type

The Suite uses five document registers, and vocabulary admissibility differs by register. This is not a style distinction; it reflects the different structural work each document type does and the different reader configurations each is designed to reach.

Standards suite documents use Multiplex and Uniplex throughout normative content. Frame 1 vocabulary is not admissible as a normative design criterion. Frame 1 and Frame 2 bridge terms are not load-bearing. Banyan and Columnar are not used in normative passages. Frame 3 may appear in grounding statements that precede the normative content, in the pointing form only, naming what the normative conditions are conditions for without specifying the Innate Totality itself.

Protocol specification documents use Multiplex and Uniplex in normative sections. Frame 1 and Frame 2 bridge terms are available in explanatory passages and introductory material where Multiplex would create unnecessary friction with readers not yet familiar with the canonical vocabulary. Frame 3 may appear as grounding in section openings.

Framework documents use Multiplex and Uniplex as primary vocabulary. Banyan and Columnar are available in explanatory sections. Frame 3 pointing language is available where the framework document is explicitly naming what the framework serves.

Orientation documents use Banyan and Columnar as primary vocabulary for the developmental argument. Multiplex and Uniplex are available for technical precision. Frame 1 and Frame 2 bridge terms are available throughout. Frame 3 pointing language is available.

Bucket and sensemaking documents admit all registers. When a term from any register is used in a document that might later serve as a source for normative content, the register of that term should be named in context. Frame 1 terms may appear when analyzing Frame 1 structures; they should be labeled as such.

## The Dimensional Degradation Design Obligation

The standards carry an obligation beyond vocabulary accuracy. When Frame 2 content reaches a Frame 1 configuration, the Suppression and Projection capacities dominant in that configuration will operate on it. The Frame 1 reader will receive a flat version of the content: the shadow cast by the statue. This degradation is not a failure of communication; it is a structural property of the seer-capacity configurations the content must pass through. The obligation is to design for it consciously.

Two requirements follow. The degraded version must register as something rather than noise. What the Frame 1 configuration can receive from this content must still point in a structurally sound direction. Frame 1 acting from the flat version should not be led into structural error. A standard whose Frame 1-accessible version encourages terminal-upward accountability structures has failed this requirement even if its Frame 2 content specified Multiplex Obligation Directions throughout.

The degraded version must also build capacity over time. Repeated contact should provide conditions under which the Perception capacity, always present even in Frame 1, can operate incrementally more. The design goal is not to explain Frame 2 in a single transmission; it is to design each encounter such that the Perception capacity has something to work with.

The three-reader standard is the design instrument for this obligation. The general reader level is the Frame 1 configuration receiving the flat version; the practitioner level is the Frame 2 configuration receiving the fuller version; the researcher level points toward Frame 3. A document working at all three levels simultaneously, not by being vague but by being designed, satisfies this obligation. Vagueness fails the obligation because it gives the general reader nothing stable to orient from. Precision at multiple registers simultaneously is what the obligation requires.

The terminus problem names a related design challenge. Frame 2 precision without Frame 3 pointing has no natural terminus: names and forms generate more names and forms, and the argumentation space extends indefinitely because nothing within Frame 2 itself can close it. Frame 3 pointing sentences provide the terminus by naming what the conditions are conditions for. Once that is visible, the conditions do not need to keep multiplying. A practitioner who can steelman an opponent's argument is operating from Frame 3 relative to that argument: they hold a position that contains both sides and can name the opponent's position better than the opponent stated it, which is the terminus Frame 2 argumentation alone cannot reach. The pointing sentence does for the reader what the steelmanner does for the debate: it names what the argumentation is actually about, ending the need to generate more of it.

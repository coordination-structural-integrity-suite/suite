## Stage 1: Process Orientation

Your job in this stage is to orient the organization to the full five-stage process, establish what they are hoping to understand or establish, present the data consent statement, and prepare them for Stage 2. This stage does not assess the organization's structure. It orients and prepares.

**Opening.** If they have attached an Exploration Findings document (from a prior run of the Explore the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite prompt), acknowledge it and note that the questions surfaced in that document will be used to calibrate depth in Stage 3. If they have not, ask them briefly what brought them to this process and what they are hoping to understand or establish. Keep this to one open question — you are gathering orientation data, not conducting an assessment.

---

**The five-stage process. Present this clearly before proceeding.**

Stage 1 (this session): Process orientation and consent. Output: process summary you keep for reference.

Stage 2: Structural foundation check. You will assess which of the six coordination commons components are currently present, partial, or absent in your actual practice. Output: commons components inventory — the baseline your standards assessment builds on.

Stage 3: Standards self-assessment. For each of the nine Coordination Structural Integrity Suite standards, you will assess where your organization currently stands against what the standard requires. This is self-assessed against the standard's structural logic — not evidence-from-a-record. This stage runs across multiple sessions, one standard or a small group of standards at a time. Output: standards self-assessment, structured finding per standard.

Stage 4: Gap map and priorities. Across your commons inventory and standards self-assessment, you will identify your three most structurally significant gaps and what addressing each one would actually look like. Output: gap map with gap type, current state, and improvement description for each priority.

Stage 5: Advancement and future framing. You will consolidate what you found, establish concrete next steps for each priority, and frame a six-month re-assessment. This stage also includes a single question about the Proof of Coordination protocol as a potential next infrastructure step. Output: advancement summary and re-assessment framing.

## Stage 2: Structural Foundation Check

This stage assesses which of the six coordination commons components are currently present, partial, or absent in the organization's actual coordination practice.

**Opening.** Briefly recap: "You completed Stage 1 on [date]. You are here to [what they said they hoped to establish]. Today is Stage 2: the structural foundation check. We will work through six coordination commons components. For each one, I will describe what it is and ask you to assess your current situation. At the end, we will have a baseline map that Stage 3 builds on."

---

**The six coordination commons components. Work through these in sequence.**

For each component: present the description, then ask the assessment question. Let the person think through their actual practice rather than their aspirations.

**1. Shared resource system.** The organization has explicitly identified what shared resources it governs — money, attention, time, tools, reputation, data, or other goods — and has a legible process for how those resources are allocated, tracked, and protected from capture or depletion.

Assessment question: "What shared resources does your organization currently govern, and how are decisions about those resources made? Is that process legible to all participants, or does it live in informal understanding?"

**2. Shared language commons.** Participants share working definitions for the key terms they coordinate around. When two people in your organization use words like "decision," "proposal," "consent," "accountability," or "member," they mean the same thing — and that meaning has been established, not assumed.

Assessment question: "What are the five or six terms your organization coordinates around most often? If you asked two participants to define each one independently, would the definitions match? Where are the gaps?"

**3. Shared purpose commons.** The organization's purpose is specific enough that participants could independently identify whether a given action advances it. A purpose that generates consistent answers to "does this action serve our purpose?" is specific enough.

Assessment question: "Can you state your organization's purpose in one or two sentences specific enough that two participants could independently decide whether a proposed action advances it — and generally agree? What happens when they disagree about whether something advances the purpose?"

**4. Shared standing commons.** It is clear who has standing to participate, make decisions, be heard, and challenge decisions — and how that standing is established, maintained, and lost. Standing is not assumed from presence or enthusiasm; it is legible from the structure.

Assessment question: "Who has standing in your organization to make a binding decision, and how do people know? What would a new participant need to do to gain full standing? Is there a clear process for losing standing, and is it applied consistently?"

**5. Shared memory commons.** The organization maintains a legible record of significant coordination events: decisions made, rationale given, commitments taken on, outcomes observed. This record is accessible to participants and is used — not merely archived.

Assessment question: "Where does your organization record significant decisions and their rationale? If a participant who was absent for three months wanted to understand what had been decided and why, where would they look? Is that record actually consulted when new decisions are made?"

**6. Shared developmental commons.** There are structural conditions for participants to grow their coordination capacity over time. The organization has some mechanism — formal or informal — for developing coordination skill collectively.

Assessment question: "How does your organization develop coordination capacity? When something goes wrong in a coordination process, is there a structured way to learn from it? What does a participant who wants to improve their coordination practice have access to?"

---

**Assessment guidance.**

For each component, after hearing their response, classify it:

- **Present:** the component is explicitly in place, legible to participants, and actively used.
- **Partial:** the component exists in some form but is informal, inconsistently applied, or legible only to some participants.
- **Absent:** the component does not exist, or exists only as aspiration without structural form.

Name the classification and briefly describe what the presence, partiality, or absence looks like in their specific case. Do not default to "partial" as a comfortable middle — if something is genuinely absent, name it as absent.

## Stage 3: Standards Self-Assessment

This stage assesses where the organization currently stands against each of the nine standards. The assessment is self-assessed against each standard's structural logic — not evidence-from-a-record.

**Opening.** If they have a Stage 2 output, note the commons components inventory. If any components were absent, note them — they are likely to appear as gaps in the relevant standards. If they have Exploration Findings with a questions-surfaced field, note those questions: "Your prior exploration surfaced these questions: [list]. I will give these areas more depth as we work through the relevant standards."

If prior Stage 3 progress is attached, recap which standards are complete and continue from the next one.

---

**The two-layer structure. Explain this before beginning the first standard.**

The nine standards work in two layers. The six Tensegrity Compressive Standards specify floor conditions — structural minimums that must not be violated. They close specific failure modes. The three Tensegrity Generative Standards specify capacity conditions — structural conditions that must be present for coordination to develop over time. Both layers are required for structural health.

---

**Assessment approach for each standard.**

For each standard:
1. Present a plain-language description of what the standard requires and why.
2. Ask two questions: "Which of these conditions are currently present in your organization's actual practice?" and "Where do you most clearly see the absence or partial presence of what this standard requires?"
3. After hearing their response, produce a brief structured finding: conditions present, conditions absent or partial, most significant gap.

Do not rush. If the person's response reveals something substantive, follow it.

---

**The six Tensegrity Compressive Standards.**

**Standard 1: Precision-First Design Standard**

What it requires: Every element of your governed system should increase the precision with which the system's dynamics can be observed, classified, and acted upon. Vague language, undefined terms, and unclassified processes close off the ability to see what is actually happening. This standard requires that your coordination infrastructure — your governance documents, your decision processes, your records — be designed to make coordination dynamics more visible, not less.

Why it matters: Coordination systems fail in ways that were visible in retrospect but invisible at the time, because the vocabulary and records that would have made the dynamics legible did not exist. Precision is not bureaucracy — it is the structural prerequisite for catching problems before they become failures.

Assessment questions: "Where in your organization's coordination infrastructure do you find yourself saying 'we all know what that means' rather than defining it? What processes or dynamics in your organization are currently unclassified — happening regularly but unnamed?"

**Standard 2: Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard**

What it requires: When participants or the coordination system encounter signals that contradict current models — complaints, anomalies, dissent, failure patterns — those signals must be engaged rather than suppressed, reframed, or dismissed. The structural condition is not that signals are always acted on; it is that they are genuinely processed and the response is legible.

Why it matters: Organizations experiencing structural failure almost always had advance warning signals they suppressed or reframed. Adverse signal suppression is not malicious — it is the predictable output of systems that do not have structural mechanisms for receiving and processing signals that challenge current models.

Assessment questions: "When someone in your organization raises a concern that contradicts how leadership or the group understands the situation, what happens structurally? Is there a legible process for raising and processing adverse signals, or does it depend on interpersonal dynamics?"

**Standard 3: Structural Consent Legibility Standard**

What it requires: Consent to coordination arrangements must be legible — it must be possible to determine what someone consented to, when, and under what conditions. Participation alone is not consent. Silence is not consent. This standard requires that consent be specifically tied to specific arrangements, and that the terms of consent be accessible and revisable.

Why it matters: Consent erosion is a common failure pattern: participants gradually operate under arrangements they never explicitly agreed to, or that have changed without their knowledge. By the time the erosion is visible, the structural damage is already done.

Assessment questions: "What have participants in your organization explicitly consented to, and where is that consent recorded? If arrangements change — governance rules, resource allocation, decision rights — how do participants consent to the change, or learn that it has happened?"

**Standard 4: Information Asymmetry Classification Standard**

What it requires: Information asymmetries — situations where some participants have structurally significant information that others do not — must be identified, classified, and addressed. Not all asymmetries are harmful, but unclassified asymmetries create the conditions for exploitation and erode trust. This standard requires that significant asymmetries be named and their governance made legible.

Why it matters: Information asymmetry is one of the most common vectors for governance failure. When some participants consistently have access to information others do not — and this asymmetry is not named or governed — power concentrates quietly and predictably.

Assessment questions: "What information do leaders or founders of your organization have that general participants do not? Is that asymmetry named and governed, or is it informal and unacknowledged? What information about the organization's health, finances, or decisions is available to all participants?"

**Standard 5: Structural Power Obligation Standard**

What it requires: Power within the coordination system must be distributed in ways that are legible, justified by the structural logic of the system, and resistant to concentration. This is not a requirement that all participants have equal power — it is a requirement that power distribution be visible, that its basis be named, and that structural mechanisms exist to prevent drift toward concentration.

Why it matters: Power concentrates gradually, through drift, before it becomes capture. The organizations most confident they have no power concentration problem are often the ones where concentration is already well underway but not yet visible.

Assessment questions: "Who has disproportionate influence over decisions in your organization, and on what basis? Is that influence legible and structurally justified, or has it accumulated informally over time? What mechanisms exist to prevent or reverse power concentration?"

**Standard 6: Regenerative Obligation Standard**

What it requires: Accountability relationships in the coordination system must be structured so that those who bear costs are also the parties who have standing to name them and hold others accountable for them. Accountability directed only upward — to funders or authority — without accountability to those most affected by coordination outcomes is structurally incomplete.

Why it matters: Systems where accountability flows only to those with power — rather than to those who bear costs — predictably protect the interests of power at the expense of those the system is meant to serve.

Assessment questions: "Who bears the costs when coordination in your organization goes wrong — whose time, money, trust, or wellbeing is most affected? Do those parties have structural standing to name that cost and hold others accountable for it? Or does accountability flow primarily to funders, leadership, or external authorities?"

---

**The three Tensegrity Generative Standards.**

**Standard 7: Sensemaking Standard**

What it develops: The capacity for participants to make shared meaning from complex or ambiguous coordination situations — to interpret what is happening, why it is happening, and what it means for the organization's direction. Sensemaking is not discussion; it is a structured capacity for collective interpretation that produces usable orientation.

Why it matters: Organizations without sensemaking capacity cannot respond coherently to novel situations. They default to whoever speaks most confidently, to prior patterns regardless of whether they apply, or to paralysis.

Assessment questions: "When your organization encounters a genuinely novel or ambiguous situation — something that doesn't fit your existing models — what happens? Is there a structured way for participants to collectively make sense of it, or does the organization wait for someone to provide an authoritative interpretation?"

**Standard 8: Four Batteries Capacity Standard**

What it develops: Four interdependent coordination capacities: cognitive (the ability to think clearly about complex coordination problems), relational (the ability to maintain trust and connection under pressure), structural (the ability to build and maintain legible coordination infrastructure), and adaptive (the ability to change course when the situation requires it). These batteries are interdependent: depletion in one depletes the others.

Why it matters: Coordination failures are frequently battery failures. An organization running on depleted relational capacity makes structural decisions that damage trust. An organization running on depleted cognitive capacity builds structural solutions that do not address the actual problem.

Assessment questions: "Which of the four capacities — cognitive, relational, structural, adaptive — does your organization draw on most heavily? Which are most depleted? What happens in your organization when the relational battery runs low — do structural processes hold, or do they start breaking down too?"

**Standard 9: Conflict Transformation Standard**

What it develops: The capacity to engage conflict as a coordination signal — information about structural misalignment — rather than as a problem to be suppressed or resolved as quickly as possible. Conflict transformation is not conflict resolution; it is the structural capacity to use conflict productively without it becoming destructive.

Why it matters: Organizations without conflict transformation capacity either suppress conflict — which drives it underground until it explodes — or allow it to become destructive, eroding trust and structural capacity.

Assessment questions: "When genuine disagreement arises in your organization about direction, values, or decisions — not just minor friction, but real structural tension — what happens? Is there a process for engaging that disagreement structurally, or does it get managed interpersonally and informally? What does unresolved conflict look like in your organization six months later?"

## Stage 4: Gap Map and Priorities

This stage synthesizes the commons components inventory and standards self-assessment into a gap map.

**Opening.** Begin by consolidating — do not ask new questions yet. Read the Stage 2 and Stage 3 outputs and produce a brief summary of the full picture: how many commons components were absent or partial, which standards showed the most significant gaps, and whether any cross-standard patterns were named in Stage 3. Present this summary before asking anything. The organization should see the full picture before being asked to prioritize.

---

**Three gap types. Explain these before the prioritization work begins.**

Not all gaps are the same kind of problem. Identifying the gap type changes what addressing it looks like.

**Development gap:** A structural capacity does not yet exist and needs to be built. Addressing this requires building something.

**Vocabulary gap:** The organization does the thing but has no shared language for it. The practice is fragile because it lives in informal understanding. Addressing this requires naming and formalizing what is already happening.

**Governance gap:** The structure exists informally but has no formal expression and cannot be cited or held to. Addressing this requires formalizing existing structure.

---

**Prioritization work.**

After presenting the consolidated picture and explaining the three gap types, ask:

"Looking at this full picture, which gaps feel most urgent to you right now? Name up to three."

After they name their urgent gaps, examine whether urgency and structural significance align. Often they do not — the most urgent gap is frequently a symptom; the most structurally significant gap is frequently the underlying condition producing multiple symptoms. Guide the person through this distinction:

"You named [gap X] as urgent. Is that gap a symptom of something more foundational, or is it the root condition? If you addressed [gap X] without addressing [gap Y], would [gap X] stay fixed?"

Arrive at three priority gaps together. For each, establish: gap type, current state, and what meaningful improvement would look like in practice.

## Stage 5: Advancement and Future Framing

This stage consolidates what the organization found, establishes concrete next steps, frames a six-month re-assessment, and closes with a single forward-looking question about structural infrastructure.

**Opening.** Briefly recap what the organization has established across the five stages: the commons components inventory, the standards self-assessment, and the three priority gaps from Stage 4. Keep this to a paragraph — they know what they did, this is orientation for the work ahead, not a summary for its own sake.

---

**Concrete next steps for each priority gap.**

For each of the three priority gaps from Stage 4, establish first steps at three time horizons:

"For [Priority 1 gap]: what is one thing your organization could do this week to begin moving this? What about this month? What would meaningful progress look like in three months?"

Work through all three priorities. The goal is not a full project plan — it is a specific first action at each time horizon that the person can actually commit to. If they offer vague answers, press for specificity: "What specifically would you do? Who would be involved? What would you look at to know whether it had any effect?"

---

**Six-month re-assessment framing.**

After establishing next steps, name what a meaningful re-assessment would look like in six months:

"Six months from now, if you were to return to this process, which commons components would you expect to have moved from absent to partial, or partial to present? Which standards self-assessment findings would you expect to look different? What visible change in your organization's coordination practice would tell you that the gap work had taken hold?"

Note their answers. These become the re-assessment frame.

---

**Re-entry design.**

Before the final question, name how to return:

"When you return to this process — whether in six months or after a significant structural change — copy this same prompt and paste your prior stage outputs. The prompt will identify where you are and ask whether this is a first run or a re-assessment. If it is a re-assessment, you will work through Stages 2 and 3 again and compare the new findings against your prior outputs. Stage 4 will update the gap map based on what has changed. You do not need to start from scratch — the prior outputs are the baseline."

---

**Closing question.**

Ask this as a single question, after the next steps and re-assessment work are complete:

"One last question, separate from the assessment work: if you were participating in a protocol designed to help organizations like yours establish verifiable structural health records, connect with other structurally healthy organizations, and build a governance commons that holds over time — what would you expect that process to need from you?"

Note their response. This is research data for the Proof of Coordination protocol design, not part of the structural assessment.

---

**End-of-process survey.**

After the closing question, present the following five questions:

1. "What do you wish the overall process had included that it didn't?"
2. "Which stage required the most unexpected work?"
3. "What vocabulary did the process assume that you didn't share coming in?"
4. "What did you learn about your organization that you did not expect to learn?"
5. "Is there anything about your organization's structural situation that this process did not surface — something you think matters but that the standards framework did not name?"

Note all responses in the output document under a "Survey responses" heading. These are research data.

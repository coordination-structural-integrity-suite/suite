**The six coordination commons components. Work through these in sequence.**

For each component: present the description, then ask the assessment question. Do not rush. Let the person think through their actual practice rather than their aspirations.

**1. Shared resource system.** The organization has explicitly identified what shared resources it governs — money, attention, time, tools, reputation, data, or other goods — and has a legible process for how those resources are allocated, tracked, and protected from capture or depletion.

Assessment question: "What shared resources does your organization currently govern, and how are decisions about those resources made? Is that process legible to all participants, or does it live in informal understanding?"

**2. Shared language commons.** Participants share working definitions for the key terms they coordinate around. When two people in your organization use words like "decision," "proposal," "consent," "accountability," or "member," they mean the same thing — and that meaning has been established, not assumed.

Assessment question: "What are the five or six terms your organization coordinates around most often? If you asked two participants to define each one independently, would the definitions match? Where are the gaps?"

**3. Shared purpose commons.** The organization's purpose is specific enough that participants could independently identify whether a given action advances it. "We want to make the world better" is not specific enough. A purpose that generates consistent answers to "does this action serve our purpose?" is.

Assessment question: "Can you state your organization's purpose in one or two sentences specific enough that two participants could independently decide whether a proposed action advances it — and generally agree? What happens when they disagree about whether something advances the purpose?"

**4. Shared standing commons.** It is clear who has standing to participate, make decisions, be heard, and challenge decisions — and how that standing is established, maintained, and lost. Standing is not assumed from presence or enthusiasm; it is legible from the structure.

Assessment question: "Who has standing in your organization to make a binding decision, and how do people know? What would a new participant need to do to gain full standing? Is there a clear process for losing standing, and is it applied consistently?"

**5. Shared memory commons.** The organization maintains a legible record of significant coordination events: decisions made, rationale given, commitments taken on, outcomes observed. This record is accessible to participants and is used — not merely archived.

Assessment question: "Where does your organization record significant decisions and their rationale? If a participant who was absent for three months wanted to understand what had been decided and why, where would they look? Is that record actually consulted when new decisions are made?"

**6. Shared developmental commons.** There are structural conditions for participants to grow their coordination capacity over time. Learning is not entirely informal and individual. The organization has some mechanism — formal or informal — for developing coordination skill collectively.

Assessment question: "How does your organization develop coordination capacity? When something goes wrong in a coordination process, is there a structured way to learn from it? What does a participant who wants to improve their coordination practice have access to?"

---

**Assessment guidance.**

For each component, after hearing their response, classify it:

- **Present:** the component is explicitly in place, legible to participants, and actively used.
- **Partial:** the component exists in some form but is informal, inconsistently applied, or legible only to some participants.
- **Absent:** the component does not exist, or exists only as aspiration without structural form.

Name the classification and briefly describe what the presence, partiality, or absence looks like in their specific case. Do not rush to "partial" as a comfortable middle — if something is genuinely absent, name it as absent.

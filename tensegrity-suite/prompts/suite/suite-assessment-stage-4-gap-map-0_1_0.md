Convergence points are higher-priority candidates than gaps that appear in only one layer.

---

**Three gap types. Explain these before the prioritization work begins.**

Not all gaps are the same kind of problem. Identifying the gap type changes what addressing it looks like.

**Development gap:** A structural capacity does not yet exist and needs to be built. Example: the organization has no shared memory commons at all, no records of decisions or their rationale. Addressing this requires building something.

**Vocabulary gap:** The organization does the thing but has no shared language for it. Example: consent processes exist but no one calls them consent processes, the terms are not defined, and the practice is fragile because it lives in informal understanding rather than shared vocabulary. Addressing this requires naming and formalizing what is already happening.

**Formalization gap:** The structure exists informally but has no formal expression and cannot be cited or held to. Example: power is distributed fairly in practice because specific people have developed norms of deference, but there is no structural mechanism that would hold if those people left. Addressing this requires formalizing existing structure.

---

**Prioritization work.**

After presenting the consolidated picture and explaining the three gap types, ask:

"Looking at this full picture, which gaps feel most urgent to you right now? Name up to three."

After they name their urgent gaps, examine whether urgency and structural significance align. Often they do not. The most urgent gap is frequently a symptom; the most structurally significant gap is frequently the underlying condition producing multiple symptoms. Guide the person through this distinction explicitly:

"You named [gap X] as urgent. Is that gap a symptom of something more foundational, or is it the root condition? If you addressed [gap X] without addressing [gap Y], would [gap X] stay fixed?"

Arrive at three priority gaps together. For each, establish:
- Gap type (development / vocabulary / formalization)
- Current state: a brief honest description of what is actually happening now
- What a meaningful improvement would look like in practice: concrete, not aspirational

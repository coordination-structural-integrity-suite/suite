# Coordination Structural Integrity Suite - Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification SKILL.md

When someone brings you the Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification, they have a specific conflict they want to address and they are trying to determine whether it is the right time to run a formal transformation process. Your job is to help them assess whether the structural preconditions are in place before they commit to a process — because deploying a process when preconditions are absent does not fail quietly. It teaches everyone in the organization that conflict transformation processes are unsafe, and that lesson persists long after the failed process ends.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Conflict Transformation Preconditions Specification. That document specifies six sequential structural preconditions that must be in place before deploying any conflict transformation process. For each precondition, it produces one of three responses: Proceed, Adjust, or Defer.

Use this skill when someone is:
- Planning to run a conflict transformation process and wants to assess readiness
- Midway through designing a conflict process and wants to check the structural conditions
- Post-incident, evaluating why a process that was deployed did not produce the intended result
- Deciding whether to escalate a conflict situation to a formal process

For routing to the Conflict Transformation Standard and its associated standards combination, use the Suite Deployment Contexts skill first. This specification addresses operational readiness for a specific process, not whether the Conflict Transformation Standard should be in scope.

## The single most important thing to understand

A conflict transformation process is not universally helpful. Poorly-timed deployment teaches participants that engaging conflict structurally is unsafe — that entering the process puts them at risk, that the container does not hold, that the organization was not actually ready to do this work. That lesson is carried forward into every future conflict the organization faces.

Deferral is not failure. It is the precondition assessment working as designed. A practitioner who reaches a Defer response should not proceed to later preconditions. The right response to a Defer is to address the missing precondition, then re-assess.

The six preconditions are sequential. Earlier ones must be in place before assessing later ones.

## The six preconditions

**Precondition 1: Container Design.** A bounded structural space must be defined before any engagement begins. The container specifies: who is present, who facilitates, what information boundaries apply (confidentiality, record conditions), recording conditions, and entry and exit conditions for all parties. A process that parties cannot leave without structural cost does not enable proactive engagement; it requires engagement under threat of consequence. If container design is absent or incomplete, Defer. If partially defined, Adjust: complete the container design before proceeding.

**Precondition 2: Scale Proportion.** The process scale must match the conflict scale. Assess: what domain does this conflict involve (interpersonal, intra-organizational, inter-organizational)? How many parties are directly involved? What is the history and intensity? Which tier of the graduated engagement architecture is appropriate? What is the Cynefin domain of the conflict situation (simple, complicated, complex, chaotic)? Chaotic situations require stabilization before transformation processes are appropriate. If scale proportion is mismatched, Adjust to the correct scale or Defer if the conflict is in a chaotic state.

**Precondition 3: Process Mode Selection.** The transformation tradition must fit the relational register, organizational context, party capacities, facilitator credentials, and domain. Available traditions include Nonviolent Communication (relational, interpersonal), Restorative Justice (harm acknowledgment, community), Deep Democracy (voice for all parts of a system), Ho'oponopono (collective reconciliation), and others. Each has conditions under which it is appropriate and conditions under which it causes harm. If the selected mode does not fit the facilitator's credentials or the parties' capacities, Adjust the mode selection or Defer until appropriate facilitation is available.

**Precondition 4: Traversal Mode Selection.** This precondition addresses how the process moves through the engagement architecture and how it records in the coordination record. Two modes: governing constraint traversal (the structural requirements govern the process path) and enabling constraint traversal (the structural requirements create possibility without prescribing the path). The traversal mode must be selected deliberately and matched to the conflict's domain and the parties' developmental state. Recording in the coordination record must be specified: what is recorded, by whom, under what access conditions. If traversal mode is undefined, Adjust before proceeding.

**Precondition 5: Threshold Management.** Named resolution, escalation, and suspension conditions must be defined before the process begins, not during it. Thresholds include: what constitutes resolution sufficient to close the process, what triggers escalation to the next tier of the graduated architecture, and what conditions suspend the process temporarily. Operator depletion thresholds must also be named: if the facilitator or supporting roles are depleted, the process should suspend rather than continue under degraded conditions. If threshold management is undefined, Defer until defined.

**Precondition 6: Continuous Consent Loop.** Consent must be checked throughout the process, not only at entry. Consent architecture must specify: how consent is checked at each stage, what the revocation mechanism is, exit protocol conditions, and retaliation protection for parties who invoke exit or revocation. Silence is not consent at any stage. If continuous consent architecture is absent, Defer.

## How to assess a specific situation

Work through the preconditions sequentially. For each, ask the practitioner to describe what is in place. Do not assume the absence of explicit answers means the precondition is met — many practitioners have implicit containers, informal threshold management, and assumed consent that have not been structurally specified.

The most common failures in practice:
- Container design that specifies who is present but not exit conditions (Precondition 1 incomplete)
- Scale mismatch where an interpersonal-scale process is deployed on what is actually an intra-organizational structural conflict (Precondition 2 mismatch)
- Mode selection based on facilitator familiarity rather than party capacities or domain fit (Precondition 3 mismatch)
- Threshold management that specifies resolution conditions but not suspension conditions (Precondition 5 incomplete)
- Consent checked at entry but not throughout (Precondition 6 incomplete)

## Adoption tier reference

The specification applies differently depending on the organization's adoption tier for the Conflict Transformation Standard. At CT-Declared (Tier 1), the specification serves as a self-assessment checklist. At CT-Instrumented (Tier 3), the precondition assessment must be documented before process deployment. At CT-Loop-Closed (Tier 4) and CT-Auditable (Tier 5), a Defer response that proceeds anyway is itself a structural finding.

Reading this specification does not require familiarity with the Conflict Transformation Standard. The six preconditions stand as a pre-deployment assessment independent of adoption tier.

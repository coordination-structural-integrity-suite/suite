# Coordination Structural Integrity Suite - Suite Structural Patterns Primer SKILL.md

When someone brings you the Suite Structural Patterns Primer, they are usually trying to name something. They have seen a recurring dynamic in their organization — signals dismissed the same way each time, the same person ending up holding all the important decisions, people quietly leaving after invisible work becomes unsustainable — and they want to know whether what they are seeing is structural or situational. Your job is to help them recognize the structural pattern before it completes, and then to route them to the standards that address it.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Suite Structural Patterns Primer. That document names ten structural patterns: recurring arrangements across multiple standards that produce recognizable failure signatures. It is a recognition and diagnostic tool, not an audit. The goal is pattern identification before the failure sequence closes.

Use this skill when someone is:
- Describing a recurring dynamic and trying to understand what is structurally producing it
- Seeing a failure signature and wanting to know which standards address it
- Trying to determine where to enter the suite given what is visibly going wrong
- Post-incident, trying to understand the structural arrangement that produced the outcome

For entry-point routing to combinations of standards, also use the Suite Deployment Contexts document once the pattern is named.

## The single most important thing to understand

Organizations do not usually fail because they violated exactly one standard. They fail because a specific arrangement of structural conditions across several standards interacts to produce a predictable result. The patterns capture those arrangements.

This means that diagnosing a situation against a single standard often misses what is structurally generating the problem. A Reframing Loop, for example, involves the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard, the Precision-First Design Standard, and the Structural Power Distribution Standard simultaneously — no single standard captures the full arrangement. The pattern is the unit of diagnosis.

## The ten named structural patterns

**Transition Narrative.** Observable signature: a leadership change or organizational shift is framed and communicated before a sensemaking process has produced a genuine account of what happened and why. The framing forecloses the meaning-making that would produce accurate diagnosis. Standards involved: Sensemaking Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

**Hidden Factory Collapse.** Observable signature: an organization functions stably for an extended period, then suddenly loses capacity when one or a small number of people exit or deplete. The hidden factory — invisible coordination work that was sustaining the system — becomes visible only when it stops. Standards involved: Four Batteries Capacity Standard, Regenerative Accountability Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard.

**Termination Reflex.** Observable signature: conflict in the organization is routinely resolved through exit (termination, resignation, ostracism) rather than through a transformation process. The organization treats exit as conflict resolution. Standards involved: Conflict Transformation Standard, Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

**Specialist Concentration.** Observable signature: one person holds both deep specialization in a critical domain and the coordination power over what that specialization means for governance. No other participant can evaluate decisions in that domain from a position of meaningful participation. Standards involved: Structural Power Distribution Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

**Reframing Loop.** Observable signature: adverse signals are received, acknowledged, and then consistently reprocessed as less threatening than initial assessment suggested — without a structural response. The loop closes when the same signal type recurs and is reprocessed again. Standards involved: Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard, Precision-First Design Standard, Structural Power Distribution Standard.

**Drift Pattern.** Observable signature: power, consent architecture, or capacity erodes through accumulated small adjustments, none of which individually triggered a structural response. The drift becomes visible only when the cumulative displacement is assessed. Standards involved: Structural Power Distribution Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard, Four Batteries Capacity Standard.

**Mission-Justified Hidden Factory.** Observable signature: coordination work that is invisible to the record is framed (explicitly or implicitly) as contribution to the mission rather than as coordination work requiring recognition and regeneration. The framing prevents the work from entering the regeneration calculus. Standards involved: Regenerative Accountability Standard, Four Batteries Capacity Standard, Sensemaking Standard.

**Document Without Process.** Observable signature: policy decisions, governance changes, or structural shifts are communicated through documents without a visible reasoning process behind them. Participants cannot trace how the decision was reached or contest it on structural grounds. Standards involved: Precision-First Design Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard.

**Jurisdictional Capture.** Observable signature: an informal domain boundary — the territory one person or group customarily handles — becomes treated as a formal authority boundary that others cannot cross. The informal becomes structurally enforced without a governance process establishing it. Standards involved: Structural Power Distribution Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard, Precision-First Design Standard.

**Hidden Factory Self-Extraction.** Observable signature: a participant performing significant invisible coordination work begins extracting value from the organization's resources — time, funding, access, relationships — in ways that are not traceable in the record, justified (internally or externally) by the unrecognized work. Standards involved: Regenerative Accountability Standard, Information Asymmetry Classification Standard, Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

## How to identify which pattern applies

Ask the person to describe what is observable, not what they think is causing it. The patterns are organized around observable failure signatures, not diagnosed causes. The most common misdiagnosis is naming a cause (e.g., "leadership failure") when what is structurally visible is a pattern (e.g., Transition Narrative or Reframing Loop).

Useful questions: What is the recurring dynamic you keep seeing? What happens when a signal or concern is raised? Who ends up holding decisions in critical domains? What happens when someone exits? Has the organization's capacity shifted without an obvious external cause?

Multiple patterns can be active simultaneously. The Drift Pattern often accompanies Specialist Concentration. The Termination Reflex often accompanies a Reframing Loop. When patterns co-occur, the standards that appear across both are the highest-priority entry points.

## Status note

The Primer distinguishes between established patterns (six, with full documentation) and working hypotheses (four, added from real evaluations and treated as provisional). When applying the four working-hypothesis patterns — Drift Pattern, Mission-Justified Hidden Factory, Jurisdictional Capture, Hidden Factory Self-Extraction — name them as provisional if this distinction matters for the person's use case.

## After pattern identification

Once a pattern is named, the next step is the Suite Deployment Contexts document, which maps presenting situations to standards combinations with contextual adjustments. The pattern identification from the Primer tells you which standards are involved; the Deployment Contexts document tells you how to configure them for the organization's current state.

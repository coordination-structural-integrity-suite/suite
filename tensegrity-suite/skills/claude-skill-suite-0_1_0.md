# Coordination Structural Integrity Suite - SKILL.md

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the full Coordination Structural Integrity Suite: eight standards in two structural layers. It encodes the frame needed to avoid the most common misreadings of the suite as a whole and of the relationship between its two layers. For any question about how the standards relate, which to apply in what order, or what the suite does and does not guarantee, use this skill rather than the individual standard skills.

## The single most important thing to understand

The suite has two structural layers with different logics. The five Tensegrity Compressive Standards are floors: they specify what must not be violated. The three Tensegrity Generative Standards are generative conditions: they specify what must be structurally present for coordination capacity to develop. A system can meet all five compressive standards and still fail to develop genuine coordination capacity. A system with full generative capacity but no compressive floors is exploitable. Neither layer is sufficient; neither replaces the other.

This distinction matters for every question you will be asked about the suite.

## The five Tensegrity Compressive Standards

**Precision-First Design Standard.** Requires that every normative claim, process, and classification carry the structural mechanism that produces its claimed outcome. A system that says "this governance produces fair outcomes" without specifying the structural mechanism has a precision deficit. When evaluating any claim, ask: what is the structural mechanism? If it is not specified, the standard is not met.

**Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard.** Requires genuine processing of signals that contradict current models. This is not a requirement to act on every signal. It is a requirement to register, engage, and produce a traceable conclusion before a signal is dismissed. External governance override — external authority actions that preempt or override internal adverse-signal handling — is a named signal class. A recognition assessment of the system's self-governance capacity with relevant external authorities is a detection prerequisite where those authorities have realistic governance reach. When evaluating compliance, ask: what was the most recent adverse signal this system received, and what happened to it? Is external governance override in the signal taxonomy?

**Structural Consent Legibility Standard.** Requires that consent be structurally legible along two axes: who is acting, and who benefits? Consent architecture must answer these questions for every consent-bearing interaction. It must structurally distinguish "authorized," "not yet responded," and "unable to respond." Silence is not consent. A durable, accessible definition of who holds participation rights must exist as a structural precondition for per-action identification; unilateral modification of that definition by parties holding concentrated authority is a consent failure. When evaluating consent architecture, ask: does this mechanism answer who is acting and who it is for? Are negotiated limits, bidirectional awareness, and revocability all structurally present? Does a persistent constituency definition exist, and can it be modified without affected-constituency consent?

**Information Asymmetry Classification Standard.** Requires that structural information asymmetry types present in a coordination system be named and disclosed. There are six primary classes (positional, temporal, interpretive, relational, omission, complexity) and an extension class framework for additional types. When evaluating a coordination system, ask: which asymmetry types are present, and which are disclosed? Named gaps can be addressed; unnamed ones accumulate.

**Structural Power Distribution Standard.** Requires that power arrangements across three independent dimensions — authority (where decisions formally reside), coordination (who controls what alignment mechanisms mean), and specialization (who can meaningfully participate in specialized governance domains) — be legible and contestable by participants who hold less of them. All three invariants (Distribute, Authorize, Monitor) extend to each level of any nested or polycentric governance structure the system participates in. Subsidiarity is the normative benchmark for cross-level authority distribution. A defined constituency must exist as a precondition for the authorized distribution profile; modifications to the constituency definition are authority-dimension concentration events requiring the Authorize invariant. Claimed decentralization is not sufficient. When evaluating power claims, ask: can a participant with below-median power understand who holds more power, how it was acquired, and what recourse they have? Does a defined constituency exist, and can it be modified without affected-constituency authorization? Where the system operates within a larger governance structure, are the invariants satisfied across levels?

## The three Tensegrity Generative Standards

**Sensemaking Standard.** Specifies five structural invariants that sensemaking must satisfy for a coordination system to remain self-correcting: disruption-occasioned (begins with a specifiable disruption, not a schedule or continuous process), particular-to-general relating (moves bidirectionally between specific cues and general frames), action-entangled (actions and understanding are constitutive of each other), sufficiency-oriented (seeks a provisional, revisable account rather than a complete one), and temporally structured (passes through a liminal phase of genuine not-knowing before resolution). Operates at three scales: intra-personal, inter-personal, and witness-reception. When evaluating sensemaking, the liminal phase is the diagnostic marker: its presence distinguishes sensemaking from pattern-matching. Its absence means the system is applying pre-existing frames rather than making new meaning.

**Four Batteries Capacity Standard.** Specifies four orthogonal capacity dimensions, each with a charge axis (cyclical, restorable) and a developmental state axis (permanent until transformed through integration events): Personal (physical, cognitive, and emotional availability), Relational (trust, psychological safety, and reciprocity — depletion suppresses adverse signal surfacing even when participants are invited to contribute), Contribution (meaning and agency in formally recognized work — depletion drives activity outside the visible record), and Mission (belief in the organizational purpose — high charge can mask structural dysfunction for extended periods; when it fails, tolerance collapses abruptly). The critical distinction for evaluation: two organizations at identical charge levels but different developmental states produce structurally different coordination outputs. Ask about both axes, not only about current depletion.

**Conflict Transformation Standard.** Specifies five structural invariants: conflict legibility (conflict events are coordination data, depositable and visible in the record — organizations that surface conflicts early are not penalized), graduated engagement architecture (a transformation spine from early informal engagement through facilitated transformation through adjudication, with adjudication last), proactive disposition enablement (early engagement is structurally protected, not penalized relative to avoidance or escalation), transformation capacity provision (facilitated capacity is a design requirement with specified operator roles, resourcing, and developmental prerequisites — not a volunteer function), and recognition as coordination work (conflict engagement is depositable as a contribution). GravityDAO's failure — universally recognized as necessary, unable to sustain itself economically under any existing governance model — is the primary empirical grounding.

## How the two layers relate to each other

The compressive standards prevent specific exploitation vectors. They do not produce coordination capacity. The generative standards specify the conditions under which coordination capacity develops. They do not close exploitation vectors.

The structural connections are specific and not incidental. Unaddressed power concentration creates conditions where consent is formally present but structurally hollow. A sensemaking infrastructure without adverse signal engagement systematically excludes the signals most likely to challenge it. Relational battery depletion suppresses the adverse signal surfacing that the Adverse Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard depends on. These are precise structural claims about how failure in one layer degrades the other, not general observations about how things are connected.

The suite is not a checklist. Partial adoption of the suite produces partial structural protection with the gaps specifically exposing the systems downstream of the absent standards.

## What the suite does not do

It does not specify governance outcomes. A system meeting all eight standards can still make bad decisions. The standards create structural conditions for legible, self-correcting coordination; they do not guarantee coordination quality.

It does not specify how to organize. The compressive standards say what must not be violated; the generative standards say what must be structurally present. Neither says what governance structure to adopt.

It does not replace judgment. Everything above the floors and within the generative conditions requires human interpretation.

## The most common misreadings to avoid

**Treating the compressive standards as the complete suite.** The three generative standards are not additions to the compressive set. They address a second, distinct failure mode: not exploitation, but attrition of what the system depends on that no one can see. A system with full compressive coverage and no generative infrastructure is protected but brittle; it cannot self-correct when novel stress appears.

**Treating the generative standards as aspirational.** They are not aspirational. They specify structural conditions. A system that lacks conflict transformation capacity structurally cannot recognize conflict as coordination information; that is a precise structural claim, not a preference for what would be nice.

**Treating adoption of some standards as proportional protection.** Each compressive standard closes one class of exploitation vector. A system can meet four standards fully and be completely exposed on the fifth. Partial adoption is legitimate developmental progress, not proportional protection against all five failure classes.

**Conflating charge and developmental state in the Four Batteries.** Full charge at an early developmental state produces competent translative coordination. Full charge at a later developmental state produces something structurally different: coordination outputs that were not derivable from prior inputs. The distinction matters for what is achievable, not only for how rested or depleted participants feel.

**Treating sensemaking as a communication practice.** It is a structural requirement. A process that runs continuously without a triggering disruption is monitoring, not sensemaking. A process that jumps from disruption to resolution without a liminal phase is applying a pre-existing frame. Both may be valuable; neither is sensemaking.

**Treating conflict transformation as conflict management.** Conflict management contains conflict. Conflict transformation converts friction into developmental signal and strengthens the coordination medium. The structural difference is the transformation spine: pathways that enable early engagement before formal adjudication, recognition that engagement at those pathways is coordination work, and resourcing that makes that work sustainable. Management without a transformation spine routes everything to adversarial adjudication eventually.

## When evaluating a coordination system against the full suite

Work through each standard independently. For each, identify whether the structural requirement is met, what evidence supports that assessment, and what evidence would be needed to confirm it.

For compressive standards: identify the specific exploitation vectors each standard addresses and whether those vectors are closed in this system.

For generative standards: identify the specific structural conditions each standard specifies and whether those conditions are structurally present, not just aspired to.

Do not aggregate into an overall score. Do not treat sophistication of design as evidence of compliance. Surface the specific questions each standard raises for the system being evaluated.

When a system has adopted some standards but not others, ask what becomes structurally undetectable in the absence of each missing standard. Do not treat "we have similar protections informally" as a substitute for structural specification.

## Adoption architecture

All eight standards share a five-tier adoption framework. Tier 1 (Assessed): the organization has mapped its operations against the standard; no process changes required. Tier 2 (Operational): deficits are tracked durably. Tier 3 (Instrumented): the standard is applied at design time. Tier 4 (Accountable): detected deficits produce mandatory governance responses whose absence is itself structurally visible. Tier 5 (Auditable): Tier 4 requirements plus independent verification without operator cooperation.

Full suite designation (all five compressive standards at Tier 4 or above) is all-or-nothing. Any system claiming structural accountability without holding the full designation must produce a structural exposure disclosure that names each absent standard, describes the failure mode class it addresses, states a self-assessed exposure level with rationale, and names any compensating controls.

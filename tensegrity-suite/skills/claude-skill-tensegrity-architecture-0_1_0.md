# Coordination Structural Integrity Suite - Tensegrity Architecture SKILL.md

When someone brings you the Tensegrity Architecture document, they are usually at the beginning of something. They have encountered the Coordination Structural Integrity Suite and are trying to understand what it is and why it exists before deciding whether to go further. Or they have already decided to adopt some standards and are trying to understand where to start. Your first job is to meet them at that starting point, not to rush them into evaluation.

## What this skill is for

This skill enables correct reasoning with the Tensegrity Architecture document. That document is the architectural overview of the full Coordination Structural Integrity Suite: why these nine standards exist, why they are organized into two layers, and how to enter the suite given a specific organizational situation.

Use this skill when someone is:
- Encountering the suite for the first time and trying to understand what it is
- Trying to understand why the architecture has the shape it does (two layers, six compressive standards, three generative standards)
- Deciding whether adoption is relevant to their organization
- Asking where to start given a specific presenting problem

For adoption sequencing across multiple standards, direct them to the Suite Integration Guide. For context-specific combinations, direct them to the Suite Deployment Contexts document.

## The single most important thing to understand

The architecture exists because organizational integrity failures follow predictable structural patterns, and those patterns are invisible to the people inside them until it is too late to act. The suite is designed to be adopted before the failure is visible, not after. Once structural failure is visible from inside the organization, the structural conditions that would prevent it have usually already collapsed.

This is the orientation that shapes everything else. The architecture is proactive infrastructure, not a compliance checklist.

## The two-layer logic

The nine standards divide into two structurally distinct layers with different logics.

The six Tensegrity Compressive Standards are structural floors. Each one closes a specific class of exploitation vector: precision deficit, adverse signal suppression, consent erosion, information asymmetry, power concentration, invisible extraction. A floor is a minimum that must be held. Floors do not produce coordination capacity; they prevent specific failure modes from being structurally available.

The three Tensegrity Generative Standards are capacity conditions. They specify what must be structurally present for coordination capacity to develop: a functioning sensemaking infrastructure, visibility into participant capacity, and a transformation pathway for conflict. These do not close exploitation vectors; they build the structural substrate that makes a coordination system self-correcting over time.

The tensegrity principle connects them. In a tensegrity structure, compression members (rigid floors) and tension members (flexible capacity elements) work together to create a load-bearing structure that is stronger under pressure than in the absence of load. Neither layer is sufficient alone. A system with full compressive coverage and no generative infrastructure is protected but brittle. A system with full generative capacity and no compressive floors is capable but exploitable.

## The six exploitation vectors the compressive standards address

**Precision deficit:** Claims without structural mechanisms. Governance that asserts outcomes without specifying the mechanisms that produce them. Addressed by the Precision-First Design Standard.

**Adverse signal suppression:** Signals that contradict current models are dismissed, reframed, or simply not processed. Addressed by the Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard.

**Consent erosion:** Who is acting and who benefits are not legible. Silence is treated as consent. The definition of who holds participation rights can be changed by those who concentrate authority. Addressed by the Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

**Information asymmetry:** Structural information gaps between participants are unnamed and undisclosed, producing predictable coordination failures. Addressed by the Information Asymmetry Classification Standard.

**Power concentration:** Authority, coordination power, and specialized participation concentrate in ways that are not legible or contestable by those with less of them. Addressed by the Structural Power Obligation Standard.

**Invisible extraction:** Contributor capacity is extracted without matched regenerative return. The extraction is structurally invisible until the contributors exit. Addressed by the Regenerative Obligation Standard.

## The three capacity conditions the generative standards specify

**Sensemaking infrastructure:** The structural conditions under which an organization can make new meaning from disruption, rather than applying pre-existing frames. Without this, the organization cannot self-correct when novel stress appears. Specified by the Sensemaking Standard.

**Capacity visibility:** The ability to see participant capacity across four orthogonal dimensions (Personal, Relational, Contribution, Mission) at both charge and developmental state levels. Without this, coordination decisions are made on invisible inputs. Specified by the Four Batteries Capacity Standard.

**Conflict as information:** A structural pathway from early conflict engagement through facilitated transformation to adjudication, with adjudication last. Without this, conflict routes to adversarial escalation by default, which destroys the medium the coordination system depends on. Specified by the Conflict Transformation Standard.

## How to help someone place themselves in the architecture

Ask about their presenting situation before routing them to specific standards. The architecture document identifies five entry point patterns:

If signals are being suppressed or dismissed: Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard plus Four Batteries Capacity Standard (Relational Battery depletion is the most common suppression mechanism).

If governance is being captured or authority is concentrating: Structural Power Obligation Standard plus Structural Consent Legibility Standard.

If invisible work is driving burnout: Four Batteries Capacity Standard plus Regenerative Obligation Standard.

If decisions are consistently missing key information: Information Asymmetry Classification Standard plus Sensemaking Standard.

If conflict is destroying coordination capacity: Conflict Transformation Standard plus Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard.

If none of these match precisely, the Structural Patterns Primer is the next step: it names ten structural patterns with observable failure signatures and maps each to the relevant standards.

## What to say about adoption

Each standard is independently deployable. Partial adoption is legitimate developmental progress, not a failure. But partial adoption produces partial structural protection, with the gaps specifically exposing the systems downstream of the absent standards.

The compressive layer has priority when resources are constrained, because the failure modes it prevents are often irreversible. A system can develop generative capacity over time; it cannot recover from some compressive failures once they complete.

Adoption tiers run from Assessed (mapped against the standard, no process changes required) through Auditable (Tier 4 requirements plus independent verification). Organizations do not need to adopt at the highest tier immediately. They need to know which tier they are at and what becomes structurally undetectable at that tier.

## When evaluating whether the architecture fits an organization

The architecture fits any organization that coordinates: makes decisions, allocates resources, manages conflict, and depends on participant capacity over time. It does not assume a specific governance structure. It specifies structural conditions, not implementation paths.

What it does not do: it does not specify how to organize. It does not guarantee coordination quality. It does not replace judgment. It creates structural conditions under which legible, self-correcting coordination becomes possible.
